The shipping industry is under increasing pressure to decarbonize, and Liquified Petroleum Gas (LPG) is emerging as a viable transitional fuel option. A recent report from Lloyd's Register (LR) and the World Liquid Gas Association (WLGA) argues that LPG offers an "immediately deployable, scalable and technically proven" pathway to reduce emissions and improve compliance with environmental regulations like the EU ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## LPG's Environmental and Regulatory Advantages

The LR/WLGA report highlights significant emissions reductions achievable with LPG compared to traditional marine fuels. Fossil LPG can reduce sulphur oxides (SOx) by 97%, particulate matter (PM) by 90%, and carbon dioxide (CO2) by approximately 20% compared to heavy fuel oil (HFO). Furthermore, lifecycle greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions can be cut by up to 17% compared to marine gas oil (MGO). These reductions are particularly relevant in the context of increasingly stringent environmental regulations. The report positions LPG as a fuel that can help shipowners improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html) and reduce their exposure to carbon pricing mechanisms like the EU ETS. The lower carbon-to-hydrogen ratio of LPG is key to achieving these environmental gains.

## Existing Infrastructure and Market Growth

One of the key arguments for LPG is the existing infrastructure and market maturity. According to the report, over 1,600 LPG carriers are already in operation, supported by a global supply chain with over 1,000 storage facilities and terminals. Seaborne LPG trade reached a record 145 million tonnes in 2023 and is expected to continue growing. This existing network potentially allows for quicker adoption compared to other alternative fuels requiring significant new infrastructure investment. The report suggests around 500 global LPG terminals could potentially become bunkering points for non-gas carriers with minimal investment.

## Economic Considerations and Retrofit Potential

The report also emphasizes the economic advantages of LPG as a transitional fuel. It suggests lower capital expenditure compared to other alternative fuel solutions, along with competitive fuel pricing in key regions. Crucially, the report highlights the potential for retrofitting existing vessels to use LPG propulsion. A significant number of existing ship types are technically capable of conversion, offering a potentially cost-effective way for shipowners to reduce their emissions footprint without investing in newbuilds. This is important given the diverse fleet composition tracked by the EMSA MRV data that informs our [emissions data](/charts.html).

## The Future of LPG: Renewable Production and Carbon Capture

Looking ahead, the LR/WLGA report emphasizes the role of renewable LPG (rLPG) and bio-LPG. These fuels are chemically identical to conventional LPG and can be used as drop-in replacements without requiring modifications to existing engines or infrastructure. Depending on the feedstock, rLPG can offer up to 80% emissions reductions. The report projects that rLPG could meet up to 50% of global demand by 2050. The report also mentions the potential to combine LPG propulsion with onboard carbon capture systems. This could further reduce emissions and extend vessel lifespans, offering significant cost benefits including reduced carbon pricing exposure within the EU ETS, and lower onboard storage requirements due to the already lower CO2 emissions from burning LPG.

## Challenges and Regulatory Gaps

While the report presents a positive outlook for LPG, it also acknowledges existing challenges. The regulatory framework for LPG bunkering is still under development, and engine options, particularly beyond two-stroke applications, are not yet fully mature. Wider adoption will depend on addressing these technical and regulatory gaps. These limitations may affect the [company rankings](/companies.html) as companies choose alternative compliance pathways.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The LR/WLGA report presents a compelling case for LPG as a viable transitional fuel to accelerate decarbonization within the shipping industry. Its scalability, existing infrastructure, and potential for emissions reductions make it an attractive option for shipowners looking to comply with increasingly stringent environmental regulations. However, the report also highlights the importance of addressing existing regulatory and technical barriers to ensure widespread adoption and realize LPG's full potential as a low-carbon fuel. The development of renewable LPG and integration with carbon capture technologies are crucial to achieving long-term decarbonization goals.
