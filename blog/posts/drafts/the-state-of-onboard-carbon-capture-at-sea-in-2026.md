The global shipping industry faces immense pressure to decarbonize, with regulators worldwide, especially in Europe, demanding tangible reductions. As the transition to low-carbon fuels proves slower and more costly than initially hoped, Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage (OCCS) is emerging as a critical near-term bridge technology. A landmark report from Lloyd’s Register Advisory, commissioned by the International Chamber of Shipping, provides the most comprehensive independent assessment to date, concluding that OCCS is feasible and capable of capturing 70-95% of emissions. However, its widespread adoption and impact on metrics like [CII ratings](/cii.html) and EU ETS costs hinge on overcoming significant integration, infrastructure, and regulatory hurdles.

## The Promise of Onboard Carbon Capture

The gCaptain article highlights that while future fuels are on the horizon, they won't cover the entire fleet swiftly or affordably enough to meet immediate decarbonization targets through the 2030s. This gap makes OCCS a compelling solution for a "practical industry" like shipping. The LR Advisory report's findings are broadly encouraging: OCCS has been demonstrated at sea across multiple vessel types, achieving capture rates of 70-95% with mature systems. This level of reduction, if properly recognized by regulatory bodies, could drastically alter a vessel's compliance status under schemes like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and significantly improve its annual operational Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) rating. However, the report also candidly points out three "interlocking problems" that will dictate commercial deployment: seamless integration into existing ship designs, the development of port infrastructure for offloading captured CO2, and a clear regulatory framework that rewards these efforts.

## Diverse Technologies, Varying Impacts

The OCCS landscape is characterized by a range of technologies, each with its own advantages and challenges. Post-combustion systems, which treat exhaust gas, currently dominate. Amine chemical absorption, at Technology Readiness Levels (TRL) 7-8, is the most developed, involving a solvent that captures CO2, then regenerates, releasing a concentrated stream for storage. This process, however, imposes a notable "fuel penalty" of 9-30%, which could increase operational costs and, if not mitigated by waste-heat recovery, could partially offset the emissions benefits.

Calcium looping (TRL 6-7) offers an alternative, forming solid calcium carbonate which can potentially be utilized in industries like cement production, offering a circular economy link. While it avoids cryogenic storage, it carries a significant mass penalty (2.27:1 against captured CO2). Other options include membrane separation (60-85% capture) and cryogenic separation, best suited for LNG carriers due to their existing cold energy infrastructure. Pre-combustion thermocatalytic decomposition, primarily for gas-fueled vessels, aims to prevent CO2 formation entirely by cracking methane into hydrogen and solid carbon. The choice of technology will have direct implications for a vessel's operational profile, its ability to meet emissions targets, and ultimately its position in [company rankings](/companies.html).

## Early Adopters and Commercial Rollout

Despite the hurdles, significant progress is being made on the ground. Wärtsilä's amine-based system achieved TRL 8 with a 2025 installation on a Solvang LPG tanker, targeting 70% CO2 capture. China's CSSC 711 has demonstrated commercial reach with installations on an Evergreen container vessel and a bulk carrier, completing the first overseas offloading operation in Rotterdam. Value Maritime is also making strides, with installations across tonnage managed by Berge Bulk, Eastern Pacific Shipping, and MOL. London-based startup Seabound, using calcium looping, has deployed its full-scale commercial installation on the UBC Cork cement carrier in partnership with Heidelberg Materials, directly feeding captured calcium carbonate into cement production. Other key players include Hanwha Ocean and Panasia, achieving nearly 90% capture rates on LNG carriers and container vessels, and Sinotech, which has reduced system weight by 40%—a critical factor for retrofits. These early deployments will be crucial for gathering real-world operational data that feeds into global emissions monitoring, including EMSA MRV.

## Sector-Specific Viability and Regulatory Gaps

The gCaptain analysis highlights varying suitability across vessel types. LNG carriers and large crude tankers are identified as "strongest near-term candidates" due to existing cryogenic systems, ample deck space, waste-heat availability, and crew familiarity with complex fluid handling. Containerships present a "mixed picture"; while frequent port calls and waste-heat availability are positives, the trade-off between OCCS equipment footprint and revenue-generating TEU capacity is a significant constraint. Bulk carriers face the "most challenging environment" due to limited deck space and waste-heat, and crew unfamiliarity with chemical management, with cement carriers and short-sea operators being partial exceptions.

The critical missing piece, as noted by the report, is a clear "regulatory framework that actually recognizes and rewards" OCCS. Without explicit inclusion and accounting mechanisms within regulations like the EU ETS, the significant investments made by shipping companies in OCCS may not translate into tangible reductions in their carbon costs or improvements in their [CII ratings](/cii.html). The pathway for the captured carbon – primarily permanent geological sequestration – also needs robust, recognized protocols for accounting purposes to ensure genuine emissions reduction.

## What this means for shipping emissions

Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage represents a promising and increasingly demonstrated pathway to significantly reduce CO2 emissions from the global shipping fleet, offering a vital bridge solution amidst the slower rollout of alternative fuels. However, its ultimate impact on global shipping emissions and the efficacy of regulatory instruments like the EU ETS hinges on crucial developments. The technology itself is progressing rapidly, but the industry urgently needs coherent port infrastructure for offloading captured carbon and, critically, a robust regulatory framework that provides clear incentives and accounting methodologies. DirtiestShips.com will continue to monitor these developments, analyzing how OCCS implementations translate into verifiable emissions reductions reported through EMSA MRV and impact [emissions data](/charts.html) across the fleet. The next few years will be pivotal in determining if OCCS can genuinely move the needle on shipping's decarbonization journey.
