The escalating costs and supply chain vulnerabilities in the bunker fuel market are highlighting the limitations of a purely regulatory approach to maritime decarbonisation, forcing the industry to explore market-based solutions to accelerate emissions reductions and improve fuel efficiency. With conventional bunker prices surging past $1000 per tonne in some hubs, and alternative fuels remaining significantly more expensive, the economic incentives for change are becoming more compelling than regulatory mandates alone.

## The Cost of Waiting: Regulation vs. Market Imperatives

As reported by Ship & Bunker, the traditional reliance on regulatory deadlines for adopting cleaner fuels and technologies may no longer be a viable strategy. While regulations like FuelEU Maritime and the EU ETS are designed to incentivize decarbonisation by imposing costs on emissions, the current volatility in the bunker fuel market creates an immediate economic imperative for efficiency and alternative fuel adoption. This could drive adoption ahead of regulatory timelines as companies proactively seek to reduce fuel consumption and mitigate price risk.

The EU ETS, covered extensively on DirtiestShips.com, extends carbon pricing to shipping, making every tonne of CO2 emitted more expensive. This, coupled with FuelEU Maritime's emissions intensity targets, theoretically narrows the cost gap between conventional and lower-carbon fuels. However, as our [company rankings](/companies.html) demonstrate, different companies are responding to these pressures at vastly different rates, suggesting that market forces and internal corporate strategies also play a significant role.

## Insetting Models and Shared Value

The article highlights the potential of "insetting" models, also known as book-and-claim systems, to address the structural disconnect between those who bear the cost of decarbonisation (shipowners) and those who benefit from it (cargo owners). These models allow emissions reductions achieved through fuel switching, operational improvements, or efficiency gains to be allocated across the supply chain, enabling cargo owners to address their Scope 3 emissions and shipowners to recoup some of the costs associated with lower-carbon fuels.

Initiatives like Carbon3 are cited as examples of platforms that facilitate the allocation of verified emissions reductions across shipping value chains. These systems can capture incremental reductions in fossil fuel consumption, whether from efficiency gains or the use of renewable fuels.

## The Role of Efficiency: More Than Just Alternative Fuels

It's important to note that the focus isn't solely on expensive alternative fuels. Simple efficiency measures – voyage optimisation, speed management, technical upgrades – can significantly reduce fuel consumption and emissions, generating immediate cost savings, which is particularly relevant in a high-cost, supply-constrained environment. The value of these savings, however, often goes uncaptured or is not shared across stakeholders. Market-based mechanisms can help monetise and distribute these savings, incentivising broader participation in decarbonisation efforts.

Our [CII ratings tool](/cii.html) demonstrates the impact of operational efficiency on a vessel's carbon intensity. Even without switching to alternative fuels, optimised routing and speed can dramatically improve a vessel's rating and reduce its overall emissions profile. Accessing and using the data from EMSA MRV could help companies make informed decisions on improving vessel efficiency and reducing fuel consumption.

## Challenges and Open Questions

Despite the promise of market-based mechanisms, several challenges remain. The article highlights the need for robust verification to prevent double counting of emissions reductions, and also cites uncertainty around how these mechanisms will interact with existing regulations like FuelEU Maritime and the EU ETS, or future IMO frameworks. Market acceptance is another hurdle; cargo owners need confidence that allocated emissions reductions are meaningful, auditable, and aligned with their corporate reporting frameworks. Finally, markets for fractionalised emissions reductions and efficiency gains are still nascent and may take time to mature.

## What this means for shipping emissions

While regulation will continue to provide the framework for maritime decarbonisation, the increased fuel costs and supply concerns are creating a potent market-based incentive for change. By aligning incentives across the value chain and enabling the monetisation of even incremental emissions reductions, these market mechanisms may accelerate the adoption of cleaner fuels and efficiency measures, moving the industry towards a more sustainable future faster than regulation alone could achieve.
