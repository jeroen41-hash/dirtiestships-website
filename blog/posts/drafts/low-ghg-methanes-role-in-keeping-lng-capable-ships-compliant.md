The global shipping industry faces an intensifying regulatory squeeze to slash greenhouse gas emissions, and the significant fleet of LNG-capable vessels is no exception. A new DNV white paper, as reported by Hellenic Shipping News, highlights how low-GHG methane – encompassing bio-methane and e-methane – could offer a crucial compliance pathway, allowing these ships to meet increasingly stringent targets under regimes like FuelEU Maritime and the EU ETS. For a platform like DirtiestShips.com, dedicated to tracking real-world emissions and regulatory performance, understanding these emerging fuel strategies is vital, as they directly impact vessel [CII ratings](/cii.html) and operator carbon costs.

## The LNG Fleet's Decarbonization Tightrope

Beyond dedicated LNG carriers, the global fleet already boasts over 800 ships capable of running on LNG, with an additional 600+ on order. This represents a substantial portion of shipping capacity, initially championed as a bridge fuel for its lower CO2 emissions compared to heavy fuel oil. However, under the impending FuelEU Maritime regulation, fossil LNG’s compliance window is closing. DNV projects that these ships can remain compliant only until around 2035, with exact timelines dependent on engine configuration. Vessels fitted with two-stroke high-pressure dual-fuel engines, which are more common in larger cargo ships, generally offer a longer compliance horizon than the four-stroke low-pressure engines prevalent in cruise and RoPax vessels. Without a viable low-carbon alternative, these vessels risk plummeting [CII ratings](/cii.html) and incurring significant penalties.

## Low-GHG Methane: A Retrofit-Friendly Solution

The DNV paper posits that bio-methane and e-methane offer a compelling solution for the existing LNG fleet. These "drop-in" fuels are fully compatible with current LNG engines and bunkering infrastructure, negating the need for costly and complex major retrofits. This compatibility is a game-changer, allowing shipowners to progressively decarbonize by blending in or entirely switching to these fuels as regulatory pressures intensify. Crucially, bio-methane and e-methane can achieve very low, or even negative, life cycle emissions, depending on their production methods.

Demand for these alternatives is projected to surge. The white paper forecasts compliance-driven low-GHG methane demand in shipping to reach 2–4 million tonnes by 2040 under FuelEU Maritime alone. If the IMO's proposed Net Zero Framework base target is adopted, this demand could skyrocket to 40–95 million tonnes. While current supply of e-methane is nascent (0.01 million tonnes operational capacity), bio-methane production reached 7 million tonnes globally in 2024, projected to hit 15 million tonnes by 2030. The challenge, however, lies in securing this supply amidst competition from other sectors.

## Economics, Competition, and Regulatory Incentives

The economic reality of low-GHG methane is currently stark: liquefied bio-methane in Rotterdam was priced at approximately USD 1,860 per tonne in late 2025, nearly three times the cost of fossil LNG. This price disparity presents a significant hurdle for adoption. However, regulatory mechanisms are beginning to shift the economic calculus. The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) and FuelEU Maritime are actively incentivizing uptake, making bio-methane "cost-competitive on selected EU-to-EU voyages" when reduced carbon costs and pooling revenues are factored in. This dynamic pricing, heavily influenced by [EU ETS carbon costs](/ets.html), will be pivotal in shipping's ability to compete with other sectors for limited low-GHG methane supplies. Strategic partnerships and long-term off-take agreements will become essential for shipowners looking to secure access.

## Infrastructure and Chain-of-Custody: The Supply Chain Challenge

From a technical perspective, existing LNG bunkering infrastructure is largely compatible with low-GHG methane, removing a significant barrier. The more persistent constraint, as the DNV paper notes, lies in economics and market access. The efficient distribution and accounting of these fuels are critical. "Flexible approaches, such as mass-balance and book-and-claim systems," can significantly reduce logistical complexity and distribution costs by allowing low-GHG methane to be injected into existing gas grids and attributed to end-users without requiring physical delivery to a specific location.

Europe is leading the way, with FuelEU Maritime and the EU ETS permitting mass balancing, thereby facilitating shipping's access to low-GHG methane through established infrastructure. The absence of similar flexibility in global regulations, however, could place international shipping at a disadvantage, facing higher costs and constrained access to vital supplies. Additionally, while methane slip remains a concern for LNG engines, advancements in engine design and the use of real-world measurement data – rather than conservative defaults – are improving the assessed well-to-wake emissions profile, which directly impacts compliance under [EMSA MRV data](/emissions-data).

## What this means for shipping emissions

Low-GHG methane offers a credible and technically compatible pathway for the substantial global LNG-capable fleet to meet increasingly stringent emissions targets. However, its widespread adoption hinges on two critical factors: the scalability of supply and economic viability. Regulatory frameworks like the EU ETS and FuelEU Maritime are proving essential drivers, creating the demand and willingness-to-pay mechanisms that can level the playing field against cheaper fossil fuels. The development of flexible chain-of-custody models will further unlock access and reduce costs. Shipowners and companies that strategically invest in securing these fuels and leverage regulatory incentives will be better positioned for compliance, influencing their [company rankings](/companies.html) and overall emissions performance. As the industry races towards decarbonization, monitoring developments in low-GHG methane production, policy, and market dynamics will be crucial for understanding the trajectory of global shipping emissions.
