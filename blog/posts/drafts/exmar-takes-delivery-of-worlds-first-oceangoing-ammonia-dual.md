The global race to decarbonize shipping just gained a significant frontrunner. Exmar, the Belgian shipping company, has announced the delivery of the MGC ANTWERPEN, heralded as the "world's first" oceangoing vessel powered by a dual-fuel ammonia engine. This development marks a pivotal moment, shifting ammonia propulsion from theoretical discussion to operational reality, with profound implications for vessel emissions, EU ETS compliance, and future [CII ratings](/cii.html) across the maritime sector.

## Ammonia's Operational Debut

The MGC ANTWERPEN is a mid-size gas carrier capable of transporting up to 46,000 cubic meters of ammonia or liquefied petroleum gas (LPG). Developed in collaboration with HD Hyundai Heavy Industries, WinGD, Nord Gas Solutions, and Lloyd's Register, this newbuild represents a significant engineering feat. According to Exmar, the vessel can reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions by "up to 90%" compared to conventional vessels, particularly when operating on low-carbon ammonia. This level of reduction meets and exceeds current International Maritime Organization (IMO) emissions reduction targets, underscoring the potential for ammonia as a key decarbonization pathway. With a further three ammonia dual-fuel midsize gas carriers on order, Exmar is clearly committing to this future.

## Redefining CII Ratings and Performance

A claimed "up to 90% reduction" in GHG emissions fundamentally alters the performance landscape for vessels. For a ship like the MGC ANTWERPEN, this means an immediate and strong position within the IMO's Carbon Intensity Indicator ([CII ratings](/cii.html)) framework. While the precise methodology for calculating CII with new fuels is still evolving, a vessel achieving such drastic reductions would almost certainly secure an 'A' rating from its inception, assuming the consistent use of low-carbon ammonia. This sharply contrasts with the challenges many existing vessels face, struggling to meet tightening CII requirements and avoid a 'D' or 'E' rating that triggers mandatory corrective action plans. Exmar's move sets a new benchmark for future newbuilds, placing immense pressure on competitors and shipowners to invest in similarly ambitious technologies to avoid falling behind in environmental performance and, consequently, market attractiveness.

## EU ETS and Carbon Cost Mitigation

The arrival of the MGC ANTWERPEN also holds substantial implications for operating costs within the expanding EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS). For voyages conducted within or to/from EU ports, conventional vessels are now subject to significant carbon costs based on their CO2 emissions. A vessel operating on low-carbon ammonia, achieving "close to zero emissions" for relevant voyages, would see its EU ETS liability drastically reduced, if not eliminated. This provides a clear competitive advantage, particularly as carbon allowance prices continue to fluctuate at elevated levels. For companies like Exmar, early adoption of such technology translates directly into lower operational expenditure on carbon, potentially freeing up capital for further green investments. This shift will increasingly differentiate the "dirtiest ships" from the cleaner ones not just in terms of environmental impact, but also financial viability, as tracked in our [company rankings](/companies.html).

## The Path Forward: Safety, Scale, and Fuel Availability

While the MGC ANTWERPEN represents a monumental step, the article highlights the critical importance of operational safety. Exmar’s extensive experience in transporting ammonia and other liquefied gases has informed rigorous crew training, risk assessments, and collaboration with industry partners and authorities. This focus on safety is paramount for the broader adoption of ammonia as a marine fuel, addressing legitimate concerns about its toxicity.

The vessel's successful deployment also shifts the focus to the next major hurdle: the scaled production and availability of *low-carbon ammonia*. The "up to 90%" emissions reduction is contingent on the ammonia itself being produced via renewable energy sources (green ammonia) or with carbon capture (blue ammonia). Without a robust supply chain of low-carbon ammonia, even the most advanced dual-fuel vessels will struggle to realize their full decarbonization potential. However, Exmar's commitment, echoed by CEO Carl-Antoine Saverys' statement that "ammonia propulsion is no longer theoretical – we are deploying it," signals a strong market pull that will undoubtedly accelerate investments in green fuel production infrastructure globally.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The delivery of the MGC ANTWERPEN marks a tangible leap forward in shipping's decarbonization journey. It demonstrates that advanced, low-emission propulsion systems are viable and entering commercial operation. This achievement will accelerate the adoption of similar technologies, influencing newbuild designs, fleet renewal strategies, and fuel infrastructure investments across the industry. For DirtiestShips.com, it signals a clearer bifurcation in our [emissions data](/charts.html): between vessels still reliant on fossil fuels and those actively embracing zero-emission alternatives, with profound implications for their carbon costs, regulatory compliance, and environmental footprint. The pressure is now squarely on other shipping companies to follow suit or risk being left behind in the race to sustainable shipping.
