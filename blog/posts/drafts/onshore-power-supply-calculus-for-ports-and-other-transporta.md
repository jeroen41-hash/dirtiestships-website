The global shipping industry faces a pivotal moment in its decarbonization journey, with Onshore Power Supply (OPS), or "shore power," emerging as a critical, yet increasingly challenging, pathway to reduce in-port emissions. As EU regulations tighten and the cost of carbon under the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) climbs, the "chicken-and-egg" conundrum between electrification mandates and robust infrastructure investment now threatens Europe's ambitious emissions reduction targets, directly impacting vessel operational efficiency, [CII ratings](/cii.html), and shipping company bottom lines.

## Europe's Electrification Crossroads: Mandates vs. Reality

Analyst Gisele Widdershoven highlights the strategic test facing Europe's maritime sector, arguing that providing electric OPS for ships in major EU ports like Rotterdam and Antwerp-Bruges "has now become one of the defining strategic tests of whether Europe can maintain its industrial competitiveness and maritime relevance." The core challenge, as detailed in the ResourceWorks article, is the "contradiction between rapid electrification mandates and underprepared infrastructure."

Specific port-level hurdles include severe electricity grid congestion in Rotterdam, exposing shipping lines and ports to long-term power cost exposure in Antwerp-Bruges, and intense industrial competition for electricity in Amsterdam. These issues directly undermine the viability of OPS, which is increasingly being mandated by regulations like FuelEU Maritime. If ports cannot supply sufficient, affordable shore power, ships will continue to burn fossil fuels at berth, directly contributing to higher greenhouse gas emissions, despite regulatory pressure to switch. This situation poses a significant threat to achieving the decarbonization goals underlying the EU's maritime strategy.

## The Direct Cost to Shipping: ETS and CII

For shipping companies, the availability and cost-effectiveness of OPS have direct financial and operational consequences. When ships utilize OPS, they switch off their auxiliary engines, eliminating direct emissions from fuel combustion during port calls. This reduction in fuel burn is directly reflected in EMSA MRV data, leading to a decrease in reported CO2 emissions. Crucially, fewer emissions mean lower liabilities under the EU ETS, where companies must purchase allowances for their carbon output. Conversely, if OPS is unavailable, unreliable, or prohibitively expensive, vessels are forced to continue running their auxiliary engines, incurring fuel costs and significant ETS expenses.

Furthermore, the operational emissions at berth contribute to a vessel's overall operational carbon intensity, influencing its [CII ratings](/cii.html). Ships consistently burning fuel in port will see their CII scores negatively impacted, potentially dropping them into D or E ratings and requiring corrective actions. This dynamic places shipping companies in a difficult position: face higher operational costs from continuous engine use and ETS liabilities, or navigate the logistical and financial uncertainties of inadequate OPS infrastructure. The challenges outlined in the ResourceWorks article therefore directly feed into the financial health and regulatory compliance of every shipping company operating within EU waters, ultimately affecting [company rankings](/companies.html) based on emissions performance.

## Global Decarbonization: A Mixed Bag

Beyond Europe, the broader energy transition for transportation reveals a similar pattern of ambition clashing with practical realities. The article notes that DNV has scaled back its long-term forecast for clean hydrogen by 45% compared to its 2022 outlook, citing weakened decarbonization actions and policy support cutbacks. While hydrogen still attracts significant investment projections, its immediate viability as a large-scale marine fuel appears to be receding, leaving fewer 'ready-for-prime-time' alternative fuel options.

Despite these global challenges, localized decarbonization efforts are underway, albeit on a smaller scale. The Port of Vancouver's Electric Container Trucking Program (ELECTRA) and initiatives by Global Container Terminals (GCT) to deploy an EV fleet and transition to renewable diesel at its container terminals demonstrate localized commitments. DP World is also testing hydrogen fuel cell container cranes. However, the sobering reality of infrastructure costs is exemplified by the Greater Victoria Harbour Authority's (GVHA) decision to abandon its plan for cruise ship OPS due to spiraling costs exceeding $95 million. This example underscores the formidable financial and logistical hurdles preventing widespread OPS adoption, even for seemingly straightforward applications.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The "chicken-and-egg" dilemma around shore power infrastructure is a defining challenge for global shipping's decarbonization efforts. Without coordinated and substantial investment in grid upgrades and port electrification, the ambitious mandates from regulations like FuelEU Maritime risk becoming punitive rather than transformative. Shipping companies will face higher operational costs from increased fuel consumption and EU ETS liabilities, alongside negative impacts on their [CII ratings](/cii.html). This scenario not only hinders progress on reducing in-port emissions but also jeopardizes Europe's industrial and maritime competitiveness, potentially leading to a system "burdened by higher costs, unstable power access, operational vulnerabilities, and declining competitiveness." The data captured by EU MRV will continue to highlight the ongoing emissions from ships at berth until this fundamental infrastructure gap is closed.
