The European Union’s FuelEU Maritime regulation, designed to stimulate the uptake of lower-carbon fuels in shipping, is facing an early test of its effectiveness as recent market dynamics suggest that compliance might be becoming too cheap to drive genuine fuel transition. As the first reporting year for FuelEU Maritime closed, an active compliance market has indeed emerged, but a rapid fall in surplus unit prices raises critical questions about whether the regulation risks becoming merely an "accounting exercise" rather than a true catalyst for decarbonization in the industry.

## FuelEU Maritime: Market Creation vs. Fuel Transition

FuelEU Maritime's primary objective is clear: to accelerate the shipping industry's shift away from conventional fossil fuels by setting increasingly stringent targets for the greenhouse gas intensity of energy used on board ships. The regulation aims to achieve this by creating a market where shipowners are incentivized to use cleaner fuels or purchase compliance surpluses from those exceeding the targets. EmissionLink, a market observer, notes that FuelEU has successfully established this market, with shipowners quickly engaging in strategies like pooling, utilizing biofuels, and participating in surplus trading to meet their obligations rather than simply incurring penalties. This initial engagement is a positive sign, indicating the industry's readiness to adapt to new regulatory frameworks.

## The Steep Price Drop and Its Implications

However, the rapid drop in FuelEU surplus prices has introduced a significant concern. EmissionLink's observations indicate that prices, which were discussed around €175 to €185 per unit during the busy pre-deadline period, swiftly moved down towards €120 to €130 after the main pooling window closed. This decline suggests a potential oversupply of compliance units, possibly due to more surplus being available than anticipated, strong generation of units, or banked surplus from long positions.

While lower prices translate to reduced immediate compliance costs for shipowners, EmissionLink's Managing Director, Philippos Ioulianou, warns of a critical downside: "If buying compliance becomes easier and cheaper than using lower-carbon fuels, then the regulation risks becoming an accounting exercise rather than a driver of decarbonisation." This scenario could disincentivize operators from making necessary investments in cleaner fuels and advanced fuel strategies, meeting the rules on paper but failing to contribute to the fundamental fuel transition FuelEU was designed to deliver.

## Connecting the Dots: FuelEU, CII, and ETS

This situation highlights a broader challenge within the EU's multifaceted regulatory push for shipping decarbonization, which includes not only FuelEU but also the [CII ratings](/cii.html) and the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). All these mechanisms aim to reduce emissions, but they tackle different aspects: FuelEU targets fuel choice, CII focuses on operational efficiency, and ETS directly prices carbon emissions.

If FuelEU compliance becomes too cheap, it could allow some companies to meet their regulatory obligations without significantly altering their fuel mix. This divergence between paper compliance and actual investment in sustainable fuels could obscure real decarbonization efforts, potentially affecting how companies are perceived in [company rankings](/companies.html) that might prioritize regulatory compliance over tangible emissions reductions. The effectiveness of FuelEU, like the EU ETS, relies on the economic incentive for greener choices outweighing the cost of compliance. Data derived from EMSA MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, Verification) underpins these regulatory frameworks, making transparent and robust data essential for tracking genuine progress, not just compliance transactions.

## Call to Regulators: Balancing Flexibility and Ambition

EmissionLink urges regulators to treat FuelEU as a live commercial market, susceptible to various factors including fuel spreads, geopolitical events, and trader positions. The key question for policymakers, as posed by EmissionLink, is whether the current framework strikes the right balance between offering flexibility and genuinely stimulating the uptake of renewable and low-carbon fuels. For FuelEU Maritime to fulfill its ambitious goals, the cost of compliance must remain sufficiently high to make investment in actual fuel transition a more attractive and economically viable option than simply purchasing surplus units.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The softening of FuelEU Maritime surplus prices is a crucial development that could potentially dilute the regulation's power to drive fundamental fuel transition in the shipping industry. While the creation of a compliance market is a positive step, the risk of compliance becoming an 'accounting exercise' without corresponding real-world investment in lower-carbon fuels is a significant concern for global shipping emissions. For meaningful decarbonization, the regulatory framework must ensure that economic signals consistently favor genuine fuel switching and innovation.
