The push to decarbonize shipping is hitting a critical bottleneck: commercial decision-making. As CII ratings and EU ETS compliance ramp up the pressure, simply optimizing vessel operations isn't enough. A ship's environmental fate is often sealed long before it leaves port, dictated by chartering choices that lock it into high-emission trade patterns.

## The "Geographical Trap" and CII Ratings

According to Natalia Liashenko, CEO and founder of Marine Solver, writing in splash247.com, vessels can be trapped in a "geographical trap." This occurs when a ship, despite efficient operations, receives a poor [CII rating](/cii.html) due to factors like long ballast legs, frequent port delays, or operating in regions with limited cargo options. As the article highlights, a ship receiving a C rating on one route might receive an E rating on another route despite similar operations, demonstrating that "environmental performance is not just about how you sail, but where you are sent.”

This is a crucial point often missed in discussions about decarbonization. While investments in fuel-efficient engines and alternative fuels are vital, the initial commercial decisions have a profound impact. Even the most technologically advanced vessel is at a disadvantage if it's consistently deployed on inefficient routes.

## Disconnect Between Voyage and Annual Planning

A key challenge, as noted in the splash247.com article, is the disconnect between short-term voyage planning and long-term, annual-based decarbonization goals. Commercial teams often focus on maximizing profit on a voyage-by-voyage basis, without fully considering the cumulative impact on the vessel's annual CII rating. The sheer complexity of variables – cargo combinations, port congestion, bunker prices – makes it difficult to assess the long-term consequences using traditional planning methods. By the time companies realize the error through mid-year reports, it is often too late to make changes.

This highlights a critical need for integrated planning tools that can evaluate the environmental impact of voyage chains over extended periods. The [company rankings](/companies.html) we track clearly show some operators are successfully navigating this challenge, while others lag behind.

## The Role of EU ETS and Carbon Costs

The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) adds another layer of complexity. Every extra mile sailed and every idle hour in port equates to a "carbon tax" via the ETS, further impacting profitability and CII ratings as stated in splash247.com. Vessels operating in geographical traps are not only penalized by lower CII ratings, but also face increased carbon costs under the EU ETS. Companies need to factor in the potential [EU ETS] carbon costs when making chartering decisions, favoring routes and trades that minimize emissions.

## Decision Support Systems: A Way Out?

The splash247.com article suggests that advanced decision support systems are essential for escaping geographical traps. These systems can model the entire decision space, evaluating not just the immediate voyage, but also potential chains of voyages to identify opportunities for improved environmental performance. By considering factors like cargo availability, port congestion, and emissions profiles, these systems can help commercial teams make informed decisions that align with both profitability and decarbonization goals. They can minimize total time and distance, which can reduce emissions.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The splash247.com article makes it clear that decarbonizing shipping requires a holistic approach that integrates commercial decision-making with operational efficiency and technological advancements. While regulations like the EU ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html) incentivize emissions reductions, their effectiveness is limited if commercial teams continue to operate in silos, focusing solely on short-term profits. A shift towards continuous situational modelling and the adoption of advanced decision support systems are crucial for unlocking significant emissions reductions and avoiding the "geographical trap" that can undermine even the most well-intentioned decarbonization efforts.
