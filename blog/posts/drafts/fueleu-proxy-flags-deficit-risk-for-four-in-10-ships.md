The shipping industry is navigating a rapidly tightening regulatory landscape designed to slash emissions, and the EU's FuelEU Maritime initiative is poised to be one of the most impactful. A recent analysis by European climate policy consultancy Kickster, drawing on public EU monitoring, reporting and verification (MRV) data, indicates that a significant 40.5% of nearly 9,000 vessels assessed are associated with potential FuelEU Maritime deficits. This finding underscores the immediate and substantial challenge facing shipowners to decarbonize or face escalating compliance costs, directly impacting global shipping's carbon footprint tracked by DirtiestShips.com.

## Kickster's Proxy Analysis: A Warning Bell for FuelEU Readiness

Kickster's comprehensive assessment scrutinized 8,973 vessels using a proxy that combines technical efficiency (under EEXI) with operational CO2-equivalent intensity per transport work, derived from publicly available EU MRV data. The results provide a crucial, albeit unofficial, snapshot of the fleet's readiness for FuelEU Maritime's ambitious targets.

Out of the nearly 9,000 vessels, Kickster categorized 2,036 in a "minor analytical-deficit" category and 1,596 in a "major" one, totalling 3,632 ships – a combined 40.5% facing potential compliance shortfalls. Conversely, 3,620 vessels (40.3%) were estimated to have a surplus, while 1,721 were deemed close to balance. It is important to note, as Kickster stressed, these categories are not official FuelEU compliance balances. The public MRV dataset, which forms the basis of DirtiestShips.com's [emissions data](/charts.html), lacks crucial elements for official compliance determination, such as verified well-to-wake greenhouse gas intensity, pooling allocations, or banking/borrowing data.

## FuelEU Deficits: More Than Just a Number

A FuelEU Maritime deficit signifies a vessel's failure to meet the progressively stringent greenhouse gas intensity limits for marine fuels. This can lead to significant compliance costs, often necessitating the purchase of additional allowances or the use of more expensive, lower-emission fuels. While Kickster's analysis is a proxy, it aligns with broader trends indicating that a substantial portion of the global fleet will struggle with upcoming environmental regulations.

Kickster's assessment highlights that official figures already show considerable activity, with over 13,000 FuelEU reports verified between January and March 2026. Crucially, over 90% of the fleet's compliance balance reports were satisfactory, largely due to around 90% of vessels utilising pooling mechanisms. This suggests that while individual vessel performance might be challenging, strategic compliance solutions like pooling are already heavily in play. For owners whose ships consistently fall into deficit categories, this will directly impact their [company rankings](/companies.html) as the costs of compliance inevitably feed into operational expenses.

## The Interplay of Efficiency, Operations, and Fuel Choices

A key conclusion from Kickster's analysis is that "technical efficiency alone was not enough to determine FuelEU performance." Instead, vessel operations and fuel choice played a central role. This finding is particularly pertinent when considering other major regulatory frameworks like the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII).

The [CII ratings](/cii.html) are directly tied to operational efficiency, penalizing ships that operate below a certain threshold. A vessel struggling with operational CO2 intensity for FuelEU Maritime is highly likely to also face a poor CII rating. This creates a compounding challenge for shipowners, demanding a holistic approach to fleet management that goes beyond just engine upgrades. Strategies such as slow steaming, optimized routing, and port turnarounds become critical, alongside the fundamental shift towards alternative, lower-carbon fuels. The selection and procurement of these fuels, ranging from LNG and methanol to future hydrogen and ammonia, will become increasingly vital, directly influencing both FuelEU compliance and overall EU ETS carbon costs.

## Implications for Shipping Emissions

This initial look at FuelEU Maritime readiness, even through a proxy, sends a clear message: the industry faces a substantial and immediate need for decarbonization. The 40.5% of ships at risk of deficits indicate that a 'business as usual' approach is no longer viable for a significant segment of the fleet. While pooling offers a crucial compliance pathway, it doesn't eliminate the underlying emissions problem. It rather distributes the cost.

**What this means for shipping emissions:** The FuelEU Maritime regulation, alongside the EU ETS, will be a powerful driver for the accelerated adoption of lower-carbon fuels and improved operational efficiency. Owners of vessels identified as being at deficit risk will face increasing pressure to invest in retrofits, adopt new technologies, or potentially scrap older, less efficient tonnage. DirtiestShips.com will continue to track these developments through EU MRV data, providing transparency on how individual companies and the global fleet are adapting to these critical emissions reduction targets. The path to cleaner shipping is not just about technology; it's about strategic operational shifts and decisive fuel choices.
