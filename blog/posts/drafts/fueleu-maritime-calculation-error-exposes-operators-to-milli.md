A critical flaw in the calculation methodology used for FuelEU Maritime compliance threatens to expose ship operators, particularly those with ice-class vessels, to millions of euros in unintended penalties. The error, embedded within EMSA's Thetis system and widely adopted by third-party verifiers like DNV, affects how energy is allocated to voyages with partial EU regulatory exposure and went unnoticed until after the individual vessel verification deadline. This means many ships received approved compliance balances based on incorrect data *before* the pooling period even began, potentially skewing [CII ratings](/cii.html) and significantly impacting financial liabilities under the new regulations.

## Thetis Error Impacts Ice-Class Vessels

The core issue lies in the incorrect calculation of energy allocation for voyages with only partial EU regulatory exposure, specifically affecting ice-class vessels. According to the splash247.com report, the error went undetected within EMSA's Thetis compliance system and was adopted by verifiers until April 16, when EMSA corrected the formula. Risto-Juhani Kariranta, CEO of Ahti Climate, which manages emissions exposure for a large fleet, estimates that individual cases could involve exposures in the order of several million euros. This is because the incorrect figures were used to calculate compliance balances for voyages undertaken during the compliance period.

## Financial Exposure and Pooling Complications

The financial consequences of this error could be substantial, particularly for operators who finalized their emissions pools before the correction was implemented. The article provides a stark example: A pool organized by Ahti Climate, already approved using the incorrect values, involved a transfer of approximately 16,000 tCO₂e with a market value of just over €3 million. Under the corrected methodology, that pool could be rendered non-compliant, with potential penalty consequences exceeding €10 million. This highlights the disparity between initial calculations and the revised figures, putting operators in a precarious position due to no fault of their own. These penalties would greatly impact the viability of operating certain ships and could impact where companies fall within our [company rankings](/companies.html).

## EMSA and Verifier Response

EMSA corrected the formula on April 16, updating compliance balances in Thetis, and DNV began notifying affected customers on April 21. However, a critical issue remains: pools already finalized and verified before the correction are locked in the system, with no mechanism to reopen or revise them. This leaves operators who acted in good faith, relying on the then-approved data, facing potentially crippling penalties. The delay in identifying and rectifying the error has compounded the problem, leaving operators with limited options and potentially significant financial liabilities. Ahti Climate has requested guidance from DG MOVE and EMSA on whether affected pools can be reopened and adjusted.

## Impact on EU ETS and Carbon Costs

While the article focuses on FuelEU Maritime, the situation has implications for the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) as well. Both regulations aim to decarbonize shipping, and inconsistencies or errors in compliance calculations undermine the effectiveness of both. The fluctuating price of carbon allowances under the EU ETS, combined with the potential penalties under FuelEU Maritime, creates a complex and potentially volatile financial landscape for ship operators. This incident raises questions about the robustness of the verification processes and the need for greater transparency and communication between regulators, verifiers, and operators. Ultimately, these costs will influence where companies sit with our [emissions data](/charts.html).

## What this means for shipping emissions

The FuelEU Maritime calculation error underscores the complexities and challenges of implementing new environmental regulations. The potential for significant financial penalties, stemming from an error outside operators' control, highlights the need for robust verification processes, clear communication from regulatory bodies, and flexibility in addressing unforeseen consequences. This incident serves as a cautionary tale for the shipping industry as it navigates the evolving landscape of decarbonization regulations. The situation also adds more weight to the argument that operators should be cautious and take time to understand the full implications of the scheme before making major decisions about their fleets. Ultimately, the resolution of this issue will significantly impact the willingness of companies to commit to the program and the speed at which the industry moves towards decarbonization.
