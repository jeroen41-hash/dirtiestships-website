South Korea's Ulsan Port has claimed a significant first in the maritime sector, completing what it describes as the world's first port-to-ship ammonia bunkering operation for a commercial vessel. This marks a crucial step in the industry's pursuit of zero-carbon fuels, but the practical implications for overall shipping emissions and compliance with regulations like the EU ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html) remain to be seen.

## Ammonia Bunkering Details at Ulsan

According to a report on gcaptain.com, the bunkering operation took place on April 23rd at Ulsan Main Port. Lotte Fine Chemical supplied approximately 600 metric tons of clean ammonia to a 45,000-cubic-meter ammonia dual-fuel gas carrier constructed by HD Hyundai Heavy Industries, using a port-to-ship (PTS) method. This demonstration adds ammonia to Ulsan's repertoire of alternative fuel trials, which already includes methanol and LNG bunkering. Ulsan is actively trying to position itself as a leading port for multiple low- and zero-carbon marine fuels.

## The Promise and Challenges of Ammonia

Ammonia's appeal lies in its potential for carbon-free combustion. However, it is still in early stages of commercialization. As the article notes, safety protocols, regulatory frameworks, engine technology, and reliable fuel supply networks are still under development. The availability of port infrastructure capable of safely handling ammonia is a critical bottleneck to broader adoption. This contrasts sharply with fuels like LNG, which have a more mature infrastructure, and methanol, which is rapidly gaining traction. The Ulsan demonstration is therefore important but represents a small step in a long journey.

## Collaborative Effort Behind the Milestone

The Ulsan Port Authority (UPA) highlighted the collaborative effort behind the ammonia bunkering. A memorandum of understanding signed in January 2024 brought together key stakeholders including Korean Register, Lotte Fine Chemical, HD Hyundai Heavy Industries, and HMM, alongside government agencies. This collaboration addressed various aspects, including fuel supply logistics, vessel readiness, regulations, and safety procedures.

## Fuel Hedging Strategies and the Role of Ammonia

The article correctly points out that shipowners, fuel suppliers, and ports are hedging their bets across multiple fuel pathways. While LNG and methanol have seen greater commercial adoption, ammonia is gaining traction for deep-sea applications. Engine manufacturers and shipbuilders are actively developing ammonia-capable vessel designs. The adoption rate of ammonia powered vessels will likely influence [company rankings](/companies.html) in terms of carbon intensity.

## Regulatory Implications and ETS Considerations

While the Ulsan bunkering demonstration is technologically significant, its immediate impact on shipping emissions, especially regarding the EU ETS, is limited. Currently, there are very few ammonia-fueled vessels in operation. The carbon intensity improvements from using ammonia will only start to affect ETS obligations when these vessels become more prevalent, and the ammonia itself is produced via a green or blue process. The demonstration doesn't specify the production method of the ammonia used, which is crucial for assessing its true emissions impact. Furthermore, the operational efficiency and potential for methane slip (in the case of LNG) or nitrous oxide (in the case of ammonia) need to be considered. Without widespread adoption and a robust regulatory framework, ammonia's contribution to reducing the shipping industry's carbon footprint under the EU ETS and other regulations will remain marginal. The EMSA MRV data will need to track the fuel usage of these ships in the coming years to get a clearer picture.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The successful ammonia bunkering operation at Ulsan Port is a promising sign for the future of zero-carbon fuels in shipping. However, it's just the first step. Scalability, safety, and a clear regulatory pathway are essential before ammonia can significantly impact shipping emissions and the industry's ability to meet increasingly stringent regulations like the EU ETS. It's also critical that lifecycle emissions are considered, including the production of the ammonia itself.
