The shipping industry is increasingly turning to biofuels as a "drop-in" solution for decarbonization, but new analysis highlights potential pitfalls and the need for rigorous due diligence. As regulatory pressure mounts with FuelEU Maritime and IMO targets, a white paper from NorthStandard, World Fuel, and Veritas Petroleum Services (VPS) underscores the growing importance and complexities of biofuel adoption.

## Surging Biofuel Demand and Regulatory Drivers

The white paper highlights the rapid increase in biofuel testing, with VPS reporting a twelvefold jump between 2021 and 2025, reaching over 1 million metric tons. This surge reflects the intensifying regulatory landscape. FuelEU Maritime, for example, mandates a 2% reduction in greenhouse gas (GHG) intensity as of last year, escalating to 6% by 2030, 14.5% by 2035, and a substantial 80% by 2050. To meet these escalating requirements, higher bio-component blends will be necessary. The International Maritime Organization (IMO) target of a 65% reduction in well-to-wake GHG intensity by 2040 (compared to 2008) aligns with a 30%-40% bio-component in fuel blends, according to the paper. This shift directly impacts the [CII ratings](/cii.html) of vessels, as fuel choices are a key determinant of a ship's carbon intensity.

## Biofuel Blend Variability and Quality Concerns

The white paper stresses the importance of understanding the composition of biofuel blends. While blends with 5%-10% bio content are increasingly common, the specific type of bio-component matters. The paper identifies Fatty Acid Methyl Esters (FAME), Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil (HVO), and Cashew Nut Shell Liquid (CNSL) as key components. Experts from World Fuel suggest HVO is ideal due to its similarities to traditional marine diesel fuels, but note that availability and cost present significant challenges. The use of CNSL, while potentially addressing availability gaps, requires vigilance regarding quality, even with improvements in refining processes.

The variability in biofuel composition and quality could lead to inaccurate emissions reporting and potentially impact a company's position in [company rankings](/companies.html) that rely on accurate data. This issue is especially relevant considering the inclusion of shipping in the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS).

## The Importance of Documentation and Traceability

The white paper emphasizes the critical role of certification and chain-of-custody documentation. This is crucial for verifying compliance with regulations and ensuring the sustainability of the biofuels used. Specifically, the experts recommend that no biofuel should be transferred until the Union Database (UDB) transaction ID has been provided and linked to the Bunker Delivery Note. This recommendation aligns with the growing need for transparency in emissions reporting, particularly in the context of the EU ETS, where accurate accounting of fuel consumption and emissions is essential for compliance and determining carbon costs. Robust documentation is also vital for accurate reporting under the EU's Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) regulation, the data source for DirtiestShips.com.

## Quality-Related Claims and Loss Prevention

The white paper's authors at NorthStandard have already received biofuel quality-related claims and inquiries regarding proof of sustainability, highlighting the practical challenges ship operators face. Mark Smith, Loss Prevention Director at NorthStandard, emphasizes the need for stronger due diligence given the regulatory tightening and rapid demand growth. The white paper serves as a guide for mitigating potential risks associated with biofuel adoption, focusing on fuel quality and documentation. The use of non-compliant or poor-quality biofuels could lead to operational problems, increased maintenance costs, and ultimately, financial losses.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The increased adoption of biofuels is a double-edged sword for shipping emissions. While biofuels offer a potential pathway to meet stringent regulatory targets like those set by FuelEU Maritime and the IMO, the complexities surrounding biofuel quality, composition, and traceability present significant challenges. Without robust due diligence, transparent documentation, and standardized testing, the industry risks undermining its decarbonization efforts and facing potential operational and financial repercussions. The points raised in the white paper serve as a reminder that transitioning to alternative fuels requires a holistic approach, encompassing fuel sourcing, quality control, and emissions accounting. The information provided through EMSA MRV data on DirtiestShips.com can help benchmark performance and drive continuous improvement.
