The European Commission is poised to significantly expand the reach and financial impact of its Emissions Trading System (ETS) on the shipping industry, proposing an overhaul that brings thousands more vessels into its carbon pricing mechanism. This move underscores the EU's escalating commitment to decarbonize maritime transport, directly impacting operating costs, technology adoption, and the global competitive landscape for shipping companies. As a data-driven platform tracking CO2, DirtiestShips.com sees this as a pivotal development that will shape emissions trajectories and drive the demand for comprehensive emissions data and [CII ratings](/cii.html) across an even broader fleet.

## Expanding the Net: Smaller Vessels Face Carbon Costs

The most notable change proposed is the extension of EU ETS requirements to a new category of vessels: those between 400 gross tonnage (GT) and 5,000 GT, effective from 2031. Currently, only ships of 5,000 GT and above are subject to the system, surrendering EU carbon allowances (EUAs) for 100% of intra-EU emissions and 50% of emissions for voyages touching non-EU ports. This expansion will bring a substantial number of smaller cargo ships, tugs, offshore vessels, and other commercial craft into scope. While Ro-pax and passenger ships in this GT range are temporarily excluded, Danish Shipping welcomed the move, stating it "will contribute to a more level playing field and increase demand for alternative fuels and propulsion solutions." For many smaller operators, this represents a new and significant operational cost, forcing a re-evaluation of fuel efficiency and potentially accelerating investment in cleaner technologies that previously might have seemed economically out of reach for their vessel size.

## Funding the Green Transition: A Drop in the Ocean?

The proposal also signals an acknowledgment of the massive investment required for shipping's energy transition. The Commission plans to set aside up to 110 million EUAs, valued by ECSA at approximately €10 billion ($11 billion), specifically to support the uptake of sustainable bunker fuels, electric propulsion, and wind-assisted technologies through 2040. While seemingly a large sum, the European Community Shipowners’ Association (ECSA) estimates the sector is expected to pay around €90 billion ($103 billion) into the ETS between 2030 and 2040. This means the proposed support package amounts to roughly 11% of the sector's projected contribution to the ETS. ECSA has rightly argued that "the full potential of the revenues generated by shipping must be used for the energy transition of the sector." For companies seeking to improve their [company rankings](/companies.html) by reducing their carbon footprint, this funding is crucial, but its scale relative to total payments highlights the significant financial burden that will remain on individual operators to invest in decarbonization.

## Plugging Loopholes and Protecting EU Trade

To prevent "carbon leakage" and ensure the integrity of the ETS, the proposed changes include several key adjustments. Offshore worksites in EU waters will now be treated as ports of call, closing a potential loophole for offshore vessels operating from non-EU bases to avoid ETS costs. Furthermore, rules for nearby non-EU transshipment ports would be tightened, and large containerships (10,000 TEU and above) transferring cargo at EU hubs after long voyages would receive only limited ETS relief until 2035. These measures are designed to discourage shipping lines from shifting transshipment activity outside the EU to circumvent carbon costs, ensuring that the ETS effectively targets emissions related to EU trade. Temporary exemptions for ice-class ships, island routes, outermost regions, and public-service passenger services would also be extended from 2030 to 2035, acknowledging specific operational challenges.

## Streamlining Reporting and Future-Proofing Regulations

In parallel with the ETS review, the Commission proposes a significant revision to the MRV Regulation, aiming to streamline reporting by combining EU ETS, MRV, and FuelEU Maritime requirements into a single, shared database with a single responsible entity. This consolidation is projected to cut annual MRV compliance costs by 10–20%. For platforms like DirtiestShips.com, this simplification promises more consistent and potentially more accessible [emissions data](/charts.html), improving the accuracy and efficiency of our analyses. Looking ahead, the Commission also proposes reviewing the EU ETS if the IMO adopts a global carbon-pricing measure, a proactive step to prevent shipping companies from paying twice for the same emissions, which is critical for global regulatory coherence.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The proposed EU ETS review represents a decisive stride towards comprehensive maritime decarbonization. By broadening the scope to smaller vessels, increasing the financial imperative for emissions reduction, strategically closing loopholes, and streamlining reporting, the EU is constructing a more robust framework to drive down CO2 emissions. While the funding for green transition technologies is a welcome step, the vast majority of the carbon cost burden will fall directly on shipping companies. This will undoubtedly accelerate the demand for fuel-efficient operations, sustainable fuels, and innovative propulsion solutions across the fleet, pushing the industry towards a cleaner, more accountable future.
