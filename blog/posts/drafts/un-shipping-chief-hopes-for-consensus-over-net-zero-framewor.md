The fate of the International Maritime Organization's (IMO) proposed Net-Zero Framework (NZF), designed to put a price on lifecycle greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from marine fuels, hangs in the balance as member states grapple with divisions at the 84th session of the Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC84). The framework, which could significantly impact the shipping industry's transition to low-carbon fuels, faces strong opposition from some nations despite recent progress in technical discussions. This regulatory uncertainty adds another layer of complexity for ship owners already navigating evolving EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) carbon costs and the IMO's [CII ratings](/cii.html) scheme.

## Contentious Negotiations Continue

According to hellenicshippingnews.com, IMO Secretary-General Arsenio Dominguez expressed hope for a consensus on the NZF, despite acknowledging divided views among member states. The framework narrowly passed an initial vote in April 2023 but faced a setback in October when opponents successfully delayed negotiations by a year. The current MEPC84 session provides another opportunity for member states to find common ground. Dominguez has urged delegates to return to the IMO’s tradition of consensus-based decision-making, avoiding a repeat of last October’s contentious vote.

## Diverging National Interests

Significant differences persist among member states regarding the NZF. The United States remains a staunch opponent, advocating for its abolition. Japan has proposed removing the cost element to appease Middle Eastern oil producers. In contrast, Pacific Island nations strongly support the regulation in its original form. These opposing viewpoints highlight the complex geopolitical landscape surrounding shipping decarbonization. The differing interests of nations will impact shipping companies based or operating in these regions, and subsequently affect their standings in our [company rankings](/companies.html).

## Progress on Technical Aspects

Despite the political challenges, Dominguez noted "momentum" and "progress" during recent intersessional working group meetings. These discussions focused on crucial technical elements of the framework, including GHG intensity requirements, emission calculators, fuel certification, life-cycle assessment guidelines, and revenue collection mechanisms for an IMO decarbonization fund. Specifically, member states were working on guidelines supporting the technical element of the framework. These advancements are essential for ensuring the NZF's practicality and effectiveness if it is ultimately adopted.

## Bunker Fuel Price Dynamics

The hellenicshippingnews.com article points out that the impetus for GHG pricing remains, due to a cost advantage of fossil fuels over greener alternatives. As the article highlights, Very Low Sulfur Fuel Oil (VLSFO) was at $913.32/metric ton in Singapore last month, compared to $1,964.19/mtVLSFOe for 100% sustainable methanol. A GHG cost could help make the lower emission options more competitive, incentivizing uptake. This pricing data underscores the economic hurdles associated with the transition to alternative fuels and highlights the potential role of regulations like the NZF in driving the adoption of cleaner energy sources. The data collected under the EU MRV and made accessible by EMSA (European Maritime Safety Agency) is critical for monitoring bunker fuel consumption and its associated emissions. This data, combined with [CII ratings](/cii.html), can provide a comprehensive view of a ship's environmental performance.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The ongoing debate surrounding the IMO's Net-Zero Framework underscores the complexity of achieving global consensus on shipping decarbonization. While progress has been made on technical aspects, significant political hurdles remain. The ultimate fate of the NZF will have a profound impact on the industry's transition to low-carbon fuels and its ability to meet ambitious emissions reduction targets. The IMO has set a target to reduce the carbon intensity of international shipping by at least 40% by 2030 compared with 2008 levels. Based on its latest data, the international fleet’s Energy Efficiency Operating Indicator was 38% lower in 2024 than in 2008. Regulations such as the NZF, coupled with initiatives like the EU ETS, will be critical in accelerating the adoption of cleaner technologies and fuels, thereby significantly reducing shipping emissions.
