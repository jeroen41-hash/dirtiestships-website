Escalating geopolitical tensions in the Middle East are rippling through the global bunker fuel market, sending prices soaring and potentially impacting shipping companies' operational costs and emissions reduction strategies. With prices for all fuel types spiking, the economic viability of different compliance strategies, including scrubber use and alternative fuels, is shifting rapidly.

## Bunker Fuel Prices Experience Dramatic Surge

According to a report by MABUX, bunker fuel prices experienced a significant surge in Week 10. The 380 HSFO index jumped by USD 112.58 to USD 565.22/MT, while VLSFO rose by USD 108.59 to USD 656.93/MT. The most dramatic increase was observed in MGO LS, which climbed by USD 205.02 to reach USD 1,020.21/MT – the first time it has surpassed USD 1,000 since October 2023 (hellenicshippingnews.com). These price hikes, attributed to escalating geopolitical tensions, create immediate cost pressures for ship operators, potentially influencing decisions around vessel speed, routing, and fuel switching.

## Scrubber Spread Narrowing, but Remains Volatile

The price differential between HSFO and VLSFO, known as the Global Scrubber Spread (SS), is a key indicator of the economic attractiveness of installing scrubbers to comply with sulphur regulations. While the overall spread narrowed slightly, it exhibited mixed dynamics across different bunkering hubs (hellenicshippingnews.com). In Rotterdam, the spread remained relatively stable at around USD 51.00, while in Singapore, it widened to nearly USD 95.00, approaching the USD 100.00 breakeven threshold for scrubber economics. This volatility makes it difficult for companies to predict the long-term return on investment for scrubber technology. The report suggests that the VLSFO continues to retain stronger economic competitiveness compared to the 380 HSFO + scrubber combination. However, this could change quickly. Increased demand for compliant fuels such as VLSFO could cause its price to increase and in turn widen the spread again.

## ECA Spread Impacted by Regional Volatility

The Istanbul ECA Spread (ES), reflecting the price difference between compliant fuel and standard fuel within Emission Control Areas, experienced a sharp increase, rising by USD 125.00 to USD 225.00 (hellenicshippingnews.com). This surge is linked to increased diesel prices driven by Middle East tensions, which have tightened supply expectations in the distillate segment. This localized impact highlights the sensitivity of fuel prices to regional geopolitical events and the challenges of maintaining consistent fuel costs across different operating regions. This is especially important as some nations implement their own national or regional regulations beyond IMO and EU mandates.

## LNG Market Disrupted, Though Prices Dip in Sines

The news article highlights a significant disruption in the LNG market caused by the suspension of QatarEnergy's LNG production following Iranian drone strikes. This disruption effectively removes approximately 20% of global LNG supply from the market. While LNG bunker prices at the port of Sines (Portugal) decreased marginally, the price differential between LNG and conventional fuel widened significantly in favor of LNG (hellenicshippingnews.com). Despite the price decrease at the port of Sines, the disruption of 20% of global LNG supply, as stated by the article, threatens to increase LNG prices in the medium- and long-term. This will have a significant impact on the emissions profile of LNG-powered vessels.

## Implications for Shipping's Carbon Footprint

The spike in bunker fuel prices could disincentivize some emission reduction measures. As fuel costs rise, companies might reduce vessel speeds (slow steaming) to conserve fuel, potentially leading to lower [CII ratings](/cii.html) in the short term. However, prolonged slow steaming can also negatively impact supply chains and increase overall voyage times. The increased volatility in fuel prices could also make it more challenging for companies to accurately budget for [EU ETS carbon costs](/eu-ets.html), adding another layer of financial uncertainty. Companies with strong environmental, social, and governance (ESG) commitments may find themselves at a competitive disadvantage if they are forced to prioritize short-term cost savings over long-term emission reductions. It remains to be seen whether these circumstances influence our [company rankings](/companies.html).

What this means for shipping emissions: Geopolitical instability is translating into higher and more volatile fuel costs for the shipping industry. While some may try to lower costs, this will likely mean the adoption of a short-term solution like slow steaming. This does not address the long-term problem that is emissions, and will likely mean the industry struggles to meet its goals under IMO 2030 and EU Fit for 55.
