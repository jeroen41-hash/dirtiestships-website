SEA-LNG's 2026 Mid-Year Market Review paints a picture of robust growth for LNG as a marine fuel, positioning methane as a dominant "decarbonisation pathway." However, as we at DirtiestShips.com analyze this data, the real-world implications for actual greenhouse gas emissions, CII ratings, and EU ETS costs remain complex, particularly when distinguishing between fossil LNG and its biogenic or synthetic counterparts. While the industry celebrates increased orders and bunkering infrastructure, the critical question for regulators and environmental advocates centers on total well-to-wake emissions, including the often-overlooked issue of methane slip.

## The Methane Momentum: LNG's Continued Growth

The report, drawing on DNV analysis of vessel orders from January to June 2026, highlights the continued strength of LNG. A total of 73 LNG dual-fuel vessels were ordered, representing almost 90% of the alternatively fuelled order book, dwarfing orders for ammonia, hydrogen, and methanol vessels. This trend is heavily concentrated in liner trades, with container vessels and Pure Car and Truck Carriers (PCTCs) leading the charge. The World Shipping Council's analysis further corroborates this, showing LNG as the preferred fuel for container ship owners, accounting for 58% of total ordered tonnage compared to 36% for conventional fuels.

This significant order book signals a substantial shift in the global fleet, which will inevitably impact future EU MRV data. The increase in LNG bunker volumes, reaching around 770,000 cubic meters per month globally in early 2026 (a 13% increase from 2025), further underscores this operational expansion. With 67 LNG bunker vessels in operation and 42 more on order, the infrastructure is clearly evolving to support this growth. For companies investing heavily in this pathway, these figures will influence their [company rankings](/companies.html) and overall emissions profiles under existing regulations.

## Bio-LNG's Rise: A Glimmer of Green?

A key focus of the SEA-LNG report is the significant growth of liquefied biomethane (LBM/bio-LNG). The report notes that LBM is now routinely bunkered, driven by regulations such as FuelEU Maritime. Data from the European Biogas Association shows biomethane production capacity hitting 8.2 bcm a year by the end of Q2 2026, an impressive 17% increase (or 1 bcm) in a single year. This growth is backed by substantial investment, with the number of operational biogas plants rising from 1,678 to 1,975, powered by €36 billion in allocated capital. The report also mentions the development of liquefied e-methane (synthetic methane).

This distinction is crucial for shipping emissions. While fossil LNG offers CO2 reductions compared to heavy fuel oil, bio-LNG and e-methane represent a much more direct pathway to genuine decarbonization due to their biogenic or synthetic, rather than fossil, origins. The growing availability and bunkering of LBM directly translates into lower lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions and, importantly, can significantly reduce or even eliminate carbon costs under the EU ETS, depending on the certified origin of the fuel. This also positively impacts a vessel's [CII ratings](/cii.html) and overall fleet compliance.

## Navigating Regulation: CII, ETS, and Methane Slip

SEA-LNG's recent provisional consultative status at the IMO underscores its influential role in shaping future regulations. The coalition advocates for "goal-based and technology-neutral decarbonisation regulations," aiming to foster a global market for low and net-zero fuels. While admirable in principle, this approach can sometimes obscure the nuances of specific fuel pathways, particularly concerning methane.

For vessels reliant on fossil LNG, maintaining favorable [CII ratings](/cii.html) in the long term presents a challenge. While LNG generally offers a better CO2 performance than conventional fuels, it is still a fossil fuel. More critically, the issue of methane slip – the uncombusted methane released during engine operation – remains a significant concern. Methane is a potent greenhouse gas, with a Global Warming Potential (GWP) far higher than CO2 over a 20-year timeframe, even if its atmospheric lifetime is shorter. The report, while optimistic about methane as a pathway, does not directly address the implications of methane slip, which is vital for a comprehensive well-to-wake emissions assessment. For companies operating within the EU ETS, fossil LNG still incurs carbon costs, though potentially lower than HFO. Only certified bio-LNG or e-methane can significantly reduce these liabilities by moving to a carbon-neutral or negative fuel source.

## What This Means for Emissions Tracking

The SEA-LNG 2026 Mid-Year Market Review confirms that the methane pathway – encompassing fossil LNG, bio-LNG, and future e-methane – is expanding rapidly within global shipping. For DirtiestShips.com, this means our [emissions data](/charts.html) derived from EU MRV will increasingly reflect a more diverse fuel mix. It emphasizes the need for granular analysis, distinguishing between the various forms of methane. While the growth of bio-LNG and e-methane offers genuine promise for significant emissions reductions and lower EU ETS costs, the continued reliance on fossil LNG means that shipping's overall decarbonization journey, and the attainment of stringent CII ratings, is still deeply intertwined with addressing methane slip and transitioning to genuinely renewable fuel sources. The reported trends underscore the necessity for rigorous, data-driven scrutiny of actual operational emissions to ensure that "decarbonisation pathways" translate into tangible environmental benefits.
