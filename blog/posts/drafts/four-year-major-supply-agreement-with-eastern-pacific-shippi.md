The global shipping industry faces immense pressure to decarbonize, with regulators tightening screws through initiatives like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII). Against this backdrop, news of major shipowners investing in solutions promising immediate fuel efficiency gains with "zero capex" holds significant weight. The recent four-year supply agreement between greentech company Sulnox and Eastern Pacific Shipping (EPS) for its Eco™ fuel additive is a prime example, expanding deployment to over 50 vessels within EPS's vast fleet. This partnership underscores the industry's drive for operational efficiencies that can translate directly into reduced emissions and lower compliance costs, offering a tangible step towards navigating an increasingly stringent regulatory landscape.

### Navigating the Green Transition: EPS's Strategic Move

Eastern Pacific Shipping, a company managing over 350 vessels across diverse segments including containers, tankers, dry bulk, and car carriers, has signed its largest commercial agreement with Sulnox. This deal significantly expands the use of Sulnox Eco™ from approximately 30 to more than 50 vessels and involves the supply of around 1.2 million litres of the additive over the contract term, as reported by hellenicshippingnews.com. This move by EPS, known for its commitment to innovation and decarbonisation, signals a strategic decision to integrate proven operational efficiency tools into its fleet management. Furthermore, EPS Ventures' increased strategic investment in Sulnox highlights a deeper commitment beyond just a supplier-customer relationship, indicating confidence in the additive's long-term potential. This financial backing suggests that major players are looking not just for quick fixes, but for scalable, validated solutions that offer a competitive edge in the decarbonization race.

### The Promise of Fuel Additives: Savings and Emissions Reductions

The core appeal of the Sulnox Eco™ additive lies in its reported ability to deliver tangible operational benefits. Following over two years of operational deployment and validation across various vessel types, EPS has reported fuel savings of between 3% and 5%. For a fleet of EPS's scale, even a modest percentage reduction in fuel consumption can translate into substantial cuts in CO2 emissions and operating costs. Beyond just fuel efficiency, the additive is also credited with reducing visible black smoke, improving engine cleanliness, lowering sludge generation, and enhancing lubricity – a critical factor for lower-sulphur fuels like LSMGO, where independent testing commissioned by EPS showed 20-23% lubricity improvements. These benefits are particularly pertinent as the industry grapples with the operational challenges and performance inconsistencies of new, lower-sulphur and alternative fuels. The agreement also notes positive results when Sulnox Eco™ is used alongside biofuel blends, suggesting its role in supporting the reliable adoption of future fuels.

### CII and EU ETS: The Regulatory Dividend of Efficiency

The reported 3-5% fuel savings directly impact a vessel's Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) rating, a crucial metric for compliance under IMO regulations. For vessels on the cusp of a 'C' or 'D' rating, such efficiency gains could be the difference between compliance and facing a mandatory corrective action plan. Our [CII ratings](/cii.html) tool demonstrates how even marginal improvements can shift a vessel's operational profile. Crucially, these fuel savings also translate into a direct reduction in CO2 emissions, which in turn lowers a company's financial exposure to the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). For voyages covered by the EU ETS, every tonne of CO2 avoided means one less EU Allowance (EUA) to purchase, offering a significant economic incentive. As EPS expands this deployment, it can be expected to positively influence their overall position in global [company rankings](/companies.html) based on reported emissions.

### MRV Data and the Path to Verification

While reported fuel savings and efficiency gains are promising, their true impact on shipping emissions is ultimately validated through robust monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) processes. The EU MRV regulation requires shipowners to submit verified emissions data, providing a transparent look at actual CO2 output. For solutions like Sulnox Eco™, the long-term, expanded deployment across EPS's fleet will provide invaluable real-world data points that, once reflected in EU MRV reports, will allow for an objective assessment of the additive's contribution to decarbonization. The commitment of EPS to supporting Sulnox's research and development programs, including operational testing and real-world deployment, suggests a continuous drive to refine these solutions and verify their benefits, contributing to the broader pool of [emissions data](/charts.html) crucial for tracking industry progress.

### What this means for shipping emissions

The expanded partnership between Eastern Pacific Shipping and Sulnox represents a tangible example of how established shipowners are actively seeking and deploying operational efficiency solutions to tackle the dual pressures of decarbonization and rising fuel costs. While fuel additives are not a silver bullet, the reported 3-5% fuel savings, when scaled across a significant portion of a large fleet, offer a material reduction in CO2 emissions. This not only eases the burden of regulatory compliance, particularly for CII ratings and EU ETS costs, but also demonstrates a pragmatic approach to environmental stewardship. The increasing adoption of such "zero capex" solutions, verifiable through MRV data, signals a growing trend towards optimizing existing assets as a critical step in the shipping industry's complex journey towards net-zero emissions.
