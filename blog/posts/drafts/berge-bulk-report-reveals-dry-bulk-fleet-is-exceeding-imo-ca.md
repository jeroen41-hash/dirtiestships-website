## The Nuance of Carbon Intensity vs. Absolute Emissions

The core of the issue lies in the distinction between carbon intensity and absolute emissions. The IMO's Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII), for instance, measures a ship's operational efficiency in terms of grams of CO2 emitted per tonne-mile of cargo carried. An improvement in CII (achieving an A or B rating) indicates that a ship is becoming more efficient at transporting goods for the distance travelled. However, if the overall volume of goods transported (tonne-miles) significantly increases, even with improved intensity, the total amount of CO2 emitted (absolute Scope 1 emissions) can still rise. This is particularly relevant for the dry bulk sector, which is highly sensitive to global trade volumes for commodities like iron ore, coal, and grain. A booming market for these goods means more voyages and potentially higher total fuel consumption, even if individual ships are operating more efficiently. ## Dry Bulk's Performance in the Regulatory Landscape

The dry bulk segment, representing a substantial portion of the global shipping fleet, has been under increasing pressure to decarbonize. IMO regulations like EEXI (Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index) and CII are designed to push efficiency improvements. While Berge Bulk’s report suggests progress in exceeding IMO intensity goals, the rise in absolute Scope 1 emissions indicates that the existing measures, or their impact, might not be sufficient to curb the overall environmental footprint. Our platform's [CII ratings](/cii.html) tool allows users to scrutinize how individual dry bulk carriers and fleets are performing, often revealing a wide spectrum of compliance and efficiency. The challenge for dry bulk specifically includes navigating often unpredictable tramp trade routes, port waiting times, and varying cargo loads, all of which can impact operational efficiency and, consequently, a vessel's CII rating and total emissions.

## The Rising Tide of EU ETS Carbon Costs

For dry bulk carriers calling at EU ports, the increase in absolute Scope 1 emissions carries direct financial implications due to the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). From 2024, shipping emissions from voyages to and from EU ports are being progressively included in the ETS. This means that an increase in overall CO2 output translates directly into higher costs for shipping companies, as they must purchase allowances for their emissions. Even if a vessel's CII rating improves, a significant uptick in activity leading to higher overall fuel burn—and thus higher Scope 1 emissions—will result in a larger carbon bill. This places a dual burden on operators: striving for intensity improvements while also managing the financial exposure from their absolute emissions. ## Beyond Berge Bulk: Industry-Wide Implications

The findings from Berge Bulk, while specific to one prominent operator, likely reflect a broader trend across the dry bulk industry. As the global economy demands more raw materials, the shipping industry responds with increased activity. This growth, while economically beneficial, directly challenges the absolute emissions reduction targets necessary to meet the Paris Agreement goals. The industry cannot solely rely on intensity improvements if the sheer volume of shipping activity outstrips those gains. This necessitates a stronger focus on alternative fuels, operational optimization across entire fleets, and potentially, demand-side measures. ## What this means for shipping emissions

The Berge Bulk report, even in its summarized form, highlights a critical reality: achieving IMO's carbon intensity goals is a necessary step, but it is not sufficient to deliver the absolute emissions reductions required for deep decarbonization. As trade volumes expand, the shipping industry must accelerate its transition to zero-emission fuels and implement more aggressive operational strategies to genuinely curb its overall environmental impact.
