In a year marked by unprecedented geopolitical disruption and economic volatility, the shipping industry's decarbonization efforts faced a formidable test. The latest Sea Cargo Charter (SCC) annual disclosure report offers a critical, albeit nuanced, snapshot of how a significant segment of the global fleet is performing against crucial climate benchmarks, underscoring both resilience and the stark realities of the path to net-zero. While SCC signatories managed to hold the line on emissions intensity, the report signals that incremental operational improvements, though vital, are insufficient to meet the IMO's increasingly ambitious targets and the escalating regulatory pressures from frameworks like the EU ETS.

## Sea Cargo Charter: A Mixed Bag of Progress

The SCC report reveals a complex picture of climate performance among its 32 signatories, which collectively represent approximately 14% of global seaborne trade within the dry bulk and tanker sectors. On average, signatories were 11.6% behind the International Maritime Organization’s (IMO) minimum decarbonisation trajectory, a slight improvement from 12.2% a year prior. This modest gain indicates a concerted effort amidst challenging conditions, as highlighted by James Lewis, chair of the Sea Cargo Charter, who praised signatories for not letting decarbonization efforts slip.

However, performance against the IMO’s more ambitious "striving" trajectory worsened slightly, standing at 18.9% behind target compared with 18.1% in the previous year. This suggests that while maintaining baseline compliance is achievable, pushing towards the more aggressive goals remains a significant challenge. Encouragingly, 20 out of the 29 companies that reported in both 2024 and 2025 succeeded in lowering their carbon emission intensity, demonstrating that individual efforts can yield measurable improvements, impacting their potential standing in [company rankings](/companies.html).

## Operational Efficiencies: The Immediate Impact on CII

The report underscores that operational measures remain the primary tool for emissions reduction. Companies highlighted strategies such as speed optimisation, weather routing, live performance monitoring, voyage planning, and fuel-efficiency improvements as the most effective near-term actions. These measures directly contribute to improving a vessel's operational efficiency and, consequently, its Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) rating.

For vessels operating under the EU MRV framework and subject to IMO's CII regulations, optimizing voyages through these methods is not just good practice but a regulatory necessity. Better operational performance directly translates into improved CII grades, reducing the risk of falling into the undesirable 'D' or 'E' categories that necessitate corrective action plans. Our [CII ratings tool](/cii.html) further illustrates how critical these operational adjustments are for maintaining compliance and competitiveness. While these measures offer immediate gains, the SCC report's conclusion that "incremental gains alone will not be enough" serves as a stark reminder that a deeper transformation is required.

## Beyond Incremental Gains: The Regulatory Imperative

Despite the commitment shown by SCC members, the report issues a strong warning: maintaining progress will become increasingly difficult without a robust global regulatory framework. This sentiment aligns with the evolving landscape of shipping emissions regulation, particularly the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). As the shipping sector integrates into the EU ETS, the financial implications of high emissions become very real. Companies with less efficient fleets or operations face substantial carbon costs, impacting profitability.

The SCC’s call for "stronger incentives for emissions reductions" and "clearer global regulation" directly speaks to the necessity of market-based measures like the EU ETS. Such frameworks aim to drive investment in low-carbon fuels and energy-efficiency technologies, areas where the report notes growing interest but limited widespread adoption. Without a global carbon price or equivalent incentives, the high upfront costs of transitioning to cleaner fuels and advanced propulsion systems (such as wind-assisted propulsion) remain significant barriers for many. ## Transparency as a Foundation

A positive highlight from the report is the high level of transparency among signatories. On average, companies reported 91% of eligible shipping activities, with 13 companies achieving 100% disclosure for their eligible voyages. Furthermore, more than 90% of signatories utilized third-party verification for their reporting. This commitment to transparency and data integrity is crucial, mirroring the rigorous reporting requirements of the EU MRV and the data-driven approach we advocate at DirtiestShips.com. Accurate and verified data is the bedrock upon which effective decarbonization strategies are built and allows platforms like ours to provide detailed [emissions data](/charts.html) and analysis.

### What this means for shipping emissions

The Sea Cargo Charter report provides a crucial reality check. While the industry has demonstrated resilience and a capacity for operational efficiency improvements, the pace of decarbonization is not sufficient to meet long-term climate targets. The slight improvement against the IMO minimum trajectory, coupled with a regression against the more ambitious "striving" goal, indicates that the current toolkit of operational measures, while effective for immediate CII compliance and reducing ETS liabilities, cannot deliver net-zero alone. Deeper decarbonization will necessitate a fundamental shift in technology adoption, fuel choices, and, critically, the implementation of comprehensive, global regulatory frameworks that provide the economic incentives for transformative change. The "holding the line" strategy, while commendable in turbulent times, is merely a pause; the real race to net-zero is yet to begin in earnest.
