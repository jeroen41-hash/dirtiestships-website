The Strait of Hormuz, a critical chokepoint for global shipping, is experiencing a significant disruption due to escalating tensions, leading to a sharp contraction in vessel movements and increasing complexity for all major shipping segments. This disruption has direct implications for shipping emissions monitoring and regulation, particularly under the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## Vessel Concentration and AIS Irregularities

According to hellenicshippingnews.com, by mid-March, over 1,000 merchant vessels were positioned west of the Strait, reflecting a substantial build-up of tonnage. The article, citing AXSMarine AIS data, highlights that only a handful of vessels were able to transit the Strait over several consecutive days, indicating a significant deviation from normal operating conditions. Furthermore, the article notes a rise in AIS transmission irregularities, with vessels either ceasing to broadcast signals or transmitting inconsistent data. This poses a challenge to accurate emissions tracking via platforms like DirtiestShips.com, which rely on [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) for calculating CO2 output. When AIS data is unreliable, the true emissions picture becomes obscured.

## Segment-Specific Impacts: Tankers, Bulkers, and Gas Carriers

The impact of the disruption varies across different vessel types. Dry bulk vessels, particularly Panamax, Supramax, and Handysize units, are experiencing an imbalance between outbound and inbound flows. Outbound movements exceed inbound traffic, suggesting operators are prioritizing exits from the region. Tankers, carrying crude oil and refined petroleum products, are heavily exposed, with hundreds positioned within the Gulf. Of particular note is the increased activity of the "shadow fleet," sanctioned or non-traditional operators seemingly more willing to take risks to maintain outbound flows. Gas carriers, especially LNG vessels, exhibit the most cautious behavior, with minimal activity due to the high value of their cargoes and specialized logistics. Understanding these segment-specific behaviors is crucial for interpreting regional emissions trends, especially when considering the carbon intensity of different fuels and cargo types. The change in mix of cargo type and ship type will skew overall emissions for the region.

## Chemical Tankers and Cargo Exposure

Chemical tankers, carrying complex and diverse cargoes, are also facing limited mobility. The article notes that no new inbound chemical tanker traffic has been recorded over extended periods. This constriction of activity extends beyond energy and bulk commodities, affecting general cargo and liner shipping. The interruption to dry bulk cargoes such as grains, fertilizers, and metals, and tanker cargoes led by crude oil, exposes multiple global supply chains to risk. It remains unclear if this is a short-term blip in the data or the start of a larger trend.

## The Role of Shadow Fleets and Sanctioned Entities

The increased presence of "shadow fleets," which are willing to operate under heightened risk conditions and often outside the regulatory framework, raises significant concerns for emissions monitoring. These vessels may not adhere to the same operational standards, potentially using dirtier fuels or engaging in less efficient practices, impacting their [CII ratings](/cii.html). Increased shadow fleet activity can distort the accuracy of emissions data and undermine efforts to reduce the shipping industry's carbon footprint. This disruption further complicates efforts to properly assess vessel status.

## Implications for EU ETS and Company Emissions

The Strait of Hormuz disruption has a knock-on effect for the EU ETS. Any vessel calling at a European port following a transit through the region will be liable for carbon costs under the scheme. Disrupted routes can lead to increased voyage times, potentially resulting in higher fuel consumption and therefore higher ETS costs for shipping companies. It remains to be seen if this will affect [company rankings](/companies.html) in the long-term. Furthermore, the presence of sanctioned entities introduces complexities related to enforcement of EU ETS regulations.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The Strait of Hormuz disruption underscores the vulnerability of global shipping to geopolitical instability. The resulting congestion, altered routes, and potential increase in the use of less regulated vessels can lead to a short-term rise in overall shipping emissions. Monitoring the situation closely using reliable data sources and adjusting emissions models accordingly will be essential to accurately assess the environmental impact.
