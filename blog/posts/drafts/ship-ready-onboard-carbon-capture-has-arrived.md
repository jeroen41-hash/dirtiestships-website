The global shipping industry faces immense pressure to decarbonize, with evolving regulations from the IMO and EU imposing significant financial and operational challenges. A technology once considered futuristic, onboard carbon capture and storage (OCCS), is now rapidly moving from concept to reality, presenting a potential third pillar alongside efficiency measures and alternative fuels. However, as the maritime-executive.com article "Ship-Ready: Onboard Carbon Capture Has Arrived" highlights, while the technology is advancing quickly, the regulatory frameworks, port infrastructure, and economic models needed for widespread adoption are still playing catch-up, leaving shipowners in a familiar position of navigating uncertainty.

### The Regulatory Landscape: A Work in Progress

The regulatory journey for OCCS is complex and fragmented. At MEPC 83 in April 2025, the IMO approved a work plan to develop a comprehensive regulatory framework, targeting a formal structure by 2028. This move signals international recognition but underscores the significant lead time required for global standards. Concurrently, the EU's FuelEU Maritime, effective January 1, 2025, notably excluded OCCS from its initial scope. The European Commission cited "limited maturity, limited demonstrated results and no international framework for traceability and long-term sequestration" as reasons.

This disparity means that while an IMO framework is on the horizon, the immediate recognition for OCCS in critical regions like Europe remains elusive. For vessels operating under the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), this lack of recognition could mean that captured CO2 still incurs carbon costs, creating a financial disincentive. Future [EMSA MRV data](/emissions-data.html) will need to adapt to accurately account for onboard captured CO2 to provide verifiable net emissions reductions, ensuring transparency and preventing double counting or greenwashing.

### OCCS: A Complementary Decarbonization Tool

DNV's Head of Shipping Advisory, Merten Stein, emphasizes that OCCS is not a "winner-take-all rival" to alternative fuels but rather a "third complementary solution." In a portfolio approach, OCCS can support fleets in managing decarbonization pressure and fuel scarcity, particularly given the challenges in scaling zero-carbon fuel production. DNV's February 2026 OCCS report, modeling capture across major container lines in the North Europe-Asia corridor, suggested that OCCS could become cost-competitive with low-GHG fuels if regulatory clarity, offloading infrastructure, and cost conditions align.

The primary barriers to OCCS scaling are identified as regulation, offloading infrastructure, and offloading price, not a lack of willingness from shipowners. Stein points to the crucial need for CO2 terminals and permanent storage solutions, driven primarily by land-based industries rather than shipping. This means maritime OCCS will largely "piggyback" on existing or developing land infrastructure, such as the CO2next and Aramis projects in Rotterdam targeting a final investment decision (FID) in 2026 or 2027. Owners must conduct a total cost of ownership analysis, weighing CAPEX, parasitic load (DNV assumes a 20% energy penalty for a 75% capture rate), and remaining carbon liabilities against the cost of simply paying the carbon bill through mechanisms like the EU ETS.

### Carbon Ridge: Pioneering Ship-Ready Solutions

Amidst the regulatory and infrastructural hurdles, technology developers are making tangible progress. Carbon Ridge, a Santa Monica-based startup, is demonstrating the viability of OCCS with a focus on practical maritime realities: size, retrofit capability, and energy consumption. Their centrifuge-based approach uses conventional amine solvents but significantly reduces the system's footprint by approximately 75% and thermal heat requirements by 30-50%. This design incorporates components familiar to engine rooms, ensuring a 15-20 year operating life.

Carbon Ridge is past the concept stage, having deployed its first pilot system aboard Scorpio Tankers' LR2 product carrier, *STI Spiga*, since July. This pilot has shown strong CO2, sulfur, and NOx removal results. Their modular systems range from 0.25 to 10 tons per hour of CO2 capture, with current shipowner interest concentrated in the 1-4 tons per hour range. For context, a 3-ton-per-hour system delivers approximately 70% CO2 removal for an LR2 tanker, equating to roughly 25 to 100 tons per day depending on configuration. Critically, Carbon Ridge also acts as an intermediary, bridging the gap between shipowners and storage developers to handle the captured CO2 from ship to terminal, alleviating a significant commercial friction point for early adopters. The company emphasizes the "very traceable and measurable" nature of captured CO2, providing clear data on inlet CO2, captured CO2, and parasitic energy use, which is vital for future [CII ratings](/cii.html) and regulatory compliance.

### What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The advent of viable onboard carbon capture technology offers a promising, albeit complex, pathway for the global shipping industry to accelerate decarbonization. While OCCS is not a silver bullet, it represents a crucial "third tool" that could help vessels reduce their CO2 footprint, improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html), and mitigate escalating EU ETS costs. However, its widespread adoption hinges on critical external factors: the development of clear international and regional regulatory frameworks that recognize captured CO2 as a verifiable emission reduction, the rapid expansion of port-side offloading infrastructure, and favorable carbon accounting rules that address the legal classification of captured CO2 (waste vs. commodity). Until these systemic issues are resolved, OCCS will remain a pioneering technology on the cusp of truly impacting global shipping emissions, requiring continued vigilance from operators, regulators, and environmental advocates alike.
