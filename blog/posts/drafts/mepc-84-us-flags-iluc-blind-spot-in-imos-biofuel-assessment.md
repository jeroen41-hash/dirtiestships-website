The push for decarbonization in the shipping industry is increasingly focused on alternative fuels, but the lifecycle emissions of these fuels are under intense scrutiny. A recent submission by the United States to the IMO highlights a critical flaw in how the organization assesses the indirect land-use change (ILUC) risks associated with crop-based biofuels, potentially undermining their purported sustainability benefits. This has implications for the entire sector as ships look to comply with stringent emissions regulations.

## The ILUC Blind Spot in IMO's Biofuel Guidelines

The US proposal, submitted to the IMO’s Intersessional Working Group on Reduction of GHG Emissions from Ships (ISWG-GHG 21), targets the IMO’s 2024 Guidelines on lifecycle GHG intensity of marine fuels (2024 LCA Guidelines). The core argument is that the current guidelines only assess ILUC risk – deforestation or grassland clearing caused by repurposing land for biofuel crops – within the biofuel project's boundaries. This narrow focus, as the US argues, misses the crucial point that "ILUC emissions, by definition, occur outside of the project boundary" (hellenicshippingnews.com).

The consequence of this localized assessment is that biofuels produced from feedstocks grown in regions with high deforestation rates could be incorrectly classified as low-ILUC risk, simply because the producer themselves isn't clearing forest within their immediate project area. Conversely, producers in areas with lower deforestation rates might be unfairly penalized. This loophole undermines the entire purpose of lifecycle assessments and could lead to unintended consequences, potentially worsening deforestation in vulnerable regions.

## The Multicropping Conundrum

The US submission further highlights inconsistencies in how the guidelines treat multicropping. The current approach could classify some feedstocks on a farm as low-ILUC risk while others on the same farm are classified as high-ILUC risk. This ignores the interconnected nature of farming operations, where farms often jointly co-produce different feedstocks.

The submission explicitly mentions corn and soybean multicropping, alongside evidence linking Brazil’s sugarcane industry to deforestation in the Amazon and Pantanal. Regions of Southeast Asia, where biofuel demand has historically driven land-use change beyond project boundaries, are also cited as concerns (hellenicshippingnews.com). These examples showcase how a project-level assessment fails to capture the broader environmental impact.

## A Region-Based Approach

As an alternative, the US proposes a region-based approach. Under this system, feedstocks sourced from areas with high deforestation rates would automatically be classified as high risk, regardless of the specific conditions within the project boundary. This shift in focus aims to address the systemic drivers of deforestation and incentivize more sustainable sourcing practices. The US also cautions against project-level risk criteria, fearing these could incentivize land conversion near carbon-rich forests simply to meet marine fuel demand.

## Implications for EU ETS and CII Ratings

The debate surrounding ILUC is directly relevant to the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's [CII ratings](/cii.html). Under the EU ETS, ships operating in European waters are required to pay for their carbon emissions. If the lifecycle emissions of biofuels are underestimated due to the ILUC blind spot, it will skew the perceived carbon footprint of ships using these fuels, potentially allowing them to avoid paying their fair share under the ETS. This could create an unfair competitive advantage for vessels using biofuels with questionable sustainability credentials.

Similarly, [CII ratings](/cii.html) are based on a ship's annual efficiency ratio, which takes into account its fuel consumption and distance traveled. If biofuels are assigned artificially low carbon intensity values, it will positively impact a ship's CII rating, even if the fuel's true environmental impact is significantly higher. This can lead to inaccurate assessments of a ship's environmental performance and potentially reward unsustainable practices. A miscalculation on the fuel carbon intensity means a miscalculation on the annual efficiency ration. [Company rankings](/companies.html) would also be inaccurate.

The [emissions data](/charts.html) collected via EMSA's MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification) system provides a wealth of information on fuel consumption and emissions from ships calling at EU ports. Ensuring the accuracy of lifecycle GHG assessments of alternative fuels is crucial for the integrity of this data and its use in informing policy decisions.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The US challenge to the IMO's biofuel assessment highlights the complexities of decarbonizing the shipping industry. While biofuels may offer a pathway to reducing emissions, it is essential to account for the full lifecycle impact of these fuels, including indirect land-use change. Failure to do so could lead to unintended consequences and undermine the environmental integrity of the sector's decarbonization efforts. A more comprehensive, region-based approach to ILUC risk assessment is needed to ensure that biofuels are truly sustainable and contribute to a genuine reduction in shipping emissions.
