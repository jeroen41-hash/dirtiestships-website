Europe's ambitious plans for large-scale carbon capture and storage (CCS) are set to create an entirely new, specialized shipping segment, demanding a fleet of around 65 dedicated CO2 carriers by 2050. This significant development, highlighted in a new report by energy consultancy Xodus, underscores the shipping industry's evolving role in global decarbonization efforts, bringing new regulatory challenges and opportunities under regimes like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII).

## The Emerging CO2 Carrier Fleet: A New Maritime Frontier

According to the Xodus "CCUS Enabling Infrastructure Study," Europe's forecast for captured emissions will surge from 70 million tonnes per annum (mtpa) in 2030 to a colossal 320 mtpa by 2050. While pipeline networks are expected to grow and eventually dominate high-volume transport corridors, shipping's role, particularly for cross-border connections and flexible regional solutions, remains critical. The report projects that despite a decreasing share of the overall transport market (from 48% in 2030 to 24% in 2050), the *absolute volume* of CO2 moved by sea will more than double, reaching 79 mtpa by mid-century.

To meet this demand, a fleet of approximately 22 CO2 carriers will be needed by 2030, expanding to 65 vessels by 2050, each with an average cargo capacity of 15,000 tonnes. This specialized fleet will serve a network of around 33 ports equipped for CO2 handling, with heavily industrialized areas like the Port of Rotterdam, Humberside, and Liverpool Bay identified as prime locations for dual-purpose 'emitter' and 'store' ports. This represents a substantial newbuild requirement and a distinct shift in maritime infrastructure investment.

## Scaling Up: Challenges for Shipowners and Infrastructure

The technology for transporting captured CO2 is not entirely new, drawing parallels from the established LPG industry and already proven in projects like Northern Lights. However, the scale of deployment required presents a significant challenge. "The task now is scaling it," noted James McAreavey, global head of CCUS at Xodus, emphasizing the need for immediate investment in port infrastructure and vessels to connect emitters to offshore storage sites, particularly across the North Sea region.

Shipowners will need to evaluate the viability of investing in these highly specialized vessels. This includes assessing future cargo availability, routing efficiencies, and the long-term regulatory stability of the carbon capture and storage market. For shipping companies considering diversification or new ventures, this segment offers a unique opportunity to directly contribute to broader industrial decarbonization, potentially attracting green financing and strategic partnerships. ## Regulatory Implications: CII and EU ETS for CO2 Carriers

A critical aspect for DirtiestShips.com's readership is how these CO2 carriers will fit into the existing and evolving regulatory landscape. While they are instrumental in *reducing* overall industrial emissions, the operational emissions of these vessels themselves will not be negligible.

The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), now extended to maritime transport, will apply to these vessels' voyages. The question arises: will CO2 carriers face the same ETS obligations as conventional cargo ships, or will there be specific provisions recognizing their unique role in the carbon value chain? Similarly, their Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) ratings will be scrutinized. Given their specialized nature and the critical service they provide, will they be benchmarked against general cargo vessels, or will a new, specific CII category emerge? Understanding these nuances is crucial for owners to project operational costs and assess vessel performance in line with global decarbonization goals. ## Market Dynamics and Investment Priorities

The Xodus report highlights a future hybrid CO2 transport system, where pipelines handle high-volume corridors, and shipping provides flexibility, cross-border connectivity, and serves regions where pipelines are not competitive. This suggests a dynamic market where strategic investment in both ship capacity and port infrastructure will be essential for creating a resilient and commercially viable network.

The identification of 60 suitable ports, with 33 expected to be operational by 2050, points to a concentrated network in heavily industrialized coastal regions. This implies a need for coordinated action between port authorities, industrial emitters, storage operators, and shipping companies. As Iain Martin, CCUS technology manager at NZTC, stated, "The priority now is to translate this insight into coordinated action that accelerates deployment." This coordination will be vital to de-risk investment and ensure timely deployment of both the maritime assets and the supporting landside infrastructure. ## What this means for shipping emissions

The emergence of a dedicated fleet of CO2 carriers marks a pivotal moment for the shipping industry. While these vessels will not directly reduce shipping's own operational carbon footprint in the traditional sense, they represent a vital enabling technology for Europe's broader decarbonization efforts. Their operational emissions will, however, be subject to increasing scrutiny under EU MRV data collection, EU ETS, and CII regulations. This demands a clear regulatory framework and significant investment to ensure that the "carbon lifeline" ships are both efficient in their mission and compliant within their own operational sphere. This new segment adds complexity and opportunity, underscoring the maritime industry's multifaceted role in the global energy transition.
