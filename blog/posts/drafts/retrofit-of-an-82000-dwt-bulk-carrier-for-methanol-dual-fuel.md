The global shipping industry faces mounting pressure to decarbonize, and retrofitting existing vessels for alternative fuels is emerging as a crucial strategy. A recent report highlighted by hellenicshippingnews.com examines the technical and economic feasibility of retrofitting an 82,000 DWT bulk carrier for methanol dual-fuel capability, a potentially significant step towards reducing emissions from this sizable segment of the global fleet. This is particularly important considering the IMO's ambitions for net-zero GHG emissions by 2050, and the increasing cost implications of emissions through mechanisms like the EU ETS.

## Bulk Carriers: A Significant Emissions Source

Bulk carriers, transporting unpackaged dry goods like grain and ore, constitute 14% of the global merchant vessel fleet. Within this category, medium-sized vessels (60,000-99,000 DWT) make up roughly 28% of the bulk carrier fleet. The Kamsarmax vessel, one of the largest in this medium-sized range, represents a notable source of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. In 2018, this specific category contributed approximately 63 million tonnes of GHG emissions, equivalent to 6% of total fleet emissions. This aligns with the IMO's Fourth GHG Study, which also identifies medium-sized bulk carriers, particularly Kamsarmax vessels, as key emitters. This highlights the importance of decarbonization efforts targeting this segment. Vessels with poorer [CII ratings](/cii.html) are increasingly likely to be forced to upgrade, or be scrapped.

## Methanol Retrofit: Technical Feasibility and Design Considerations

The study detailed in the article investigates the design considerations for retrofitting a representative Kamsarmax bulk carrier for methanol fuel. Key aspects examined include optimal tank arrangements (considering volume, endurance, and technical acceptance), installation approaches for the methanol storage tank (including yard handling capabilities and coating requirements), and a thorough HAZID review to assess potential hazards and mitigation strategies. The retrofit design received Approval in Principle (AiP) from ClassNK, confirming its safety compliance. This is a critical step, as safety concerns are often cited as a barrier to alternative fuel adoption. The design package developed is intended to be widely applicable across the Kamsarmax fleet, potentially accelerating the transition to low-emission fuels. However, the article lacks specifics on the estimated cost of such a retrofit and the projected reduction in emissions, making it difficult to fully assess the economic viability and environmental impact.

## Tramp Shipping and the Multi-Fuel Challenge

Bulk carriers predominantly operate in the tramp sector, meaning they don't adhere to fixed schedules and operate globally. This presents a unique challenge for decarbonization, as operators must navigate a multi-fuel reality while securing access to green fuels in various ports worldwide. The report highlights the need to consider drop-in biofuels or retrofitting for alternative fuels like methanol. The success of methanol as a viable option hinges on the development of sufficient bunkering infrastructure and the availability of sustainably produced "green" methanol. Without these, the emissions benefits may be limited. Owners may factor these operational costs into their decisions regarding vessel upgrades, as well as potential [EU ETS carbon costs](/ets.html).

## Adoption Hurdles: Cost, Infrastructure, and Conventional Fuel Dominance

Despite the technical feasibility demonstrated by the study, significant hurdles remain for widespread adoption of methanol retrofits. The article mentions that 99.8% of the existing Kamsarmax fleet consists of conventional single-fueled vessels, and even among newbuilding vessels, 94.8% will be conventionally fueled. This indicates a strong inertia towards traditional fuels, likely driven by factors such as lower upfront costs, established infrastructure, and readily available fuel supply. While retrofitting offers a pathway to decarbonize the existing fleet, the cost of conversion, the availability of methanol fuel, and potential operational restrictions (e.g., cargo capacity loss) will influence owner decisions. The economic incentive could come increasingly from regulators, as the costs of operating non-compliant vessels increase.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The study underscores the technical viability of retrofitting existing bulk carriers for methanol dual-fuel capability, representing a potentially impactful strategy for reducing GHG emissions from a significant segment of the global fleet. However, the limited information available prevents an adequate assessment of its economic viability, and the challenges of infrastructure development, fuel availability, and the prevailing dominance of conventional fuels remain significant obstacles. Whether shipowners prioritize these retrofits or opt for alternative pathways – such as new builds running on alternative fuels, speed reductions, or simply paying the rising carbon costs levied on conventional fuels – will shape the future emissions trajectory of the shipping industry and the [company rankings](/companies.html) on sites like this one.
