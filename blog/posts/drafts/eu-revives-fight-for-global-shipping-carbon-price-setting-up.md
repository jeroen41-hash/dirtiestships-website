The European Union is doubling down on its push for a global carbon price for the shipping industry, setting the stage for a renewed showdown at the International Maritime Organization (IMO) despite previous setbacks and staunch opposition from countries including the U.S. This renewed effort underscores the EU's commitment to decarbonizing shipping, a sector increasingly scrutinized for its significant contribution to global greenhouse gas emissions. A global carbon price would have a major impact on companies' [company rankings](/companies.html) based on emissions.

## EU Reasserts Its Position on Shipping Carbon Pricing

According to a *Reuters* report, EU member states have agreed to advocate for a global CO2 emissions pricing mechanism for shipping at upcoming IMO talks. The document outlining the EU's negotiating position states that EU countries "shall oppose any attempts" to remove the climate measures from being negotiated. This firm stance follows a previous IMO decision to postpone discussions on the climate plan, a decision heavily influenced by opposition from the Trump administration. While the EU is willing to consider modifications to the original carbon pricing plan to garner broader support, some officials remain skeptical about achieving a compromise, citing continued resistance from the U.S. and other nations. The details of the original plan have not been disclosed in the article.

## The Divisive Vote and Shifting Alliances

The previous IMO vote revealed deep divisions within the international community. Fifty-seven countries, including China and major shipping nations like Liberia, voted to delay the carbon pricing proposal, while 49 countries, including European nations and Brazil, supported its implementation. The article states the coalition of the world’s top three ship registries, Liberia, Panama and the Marshall Islands, plus oil tanker companies including Saudi Arabia’s Bahri urged IMO members to consider alternatives to the original carbon pricing plan. This indicates a growing resistance to the current proposal. The internal divisions extend to the EU itself, with Greece and Cyprus, both home to substantial shipping industries, abstaining from the initial vote. Greece, Malta and Italy also declined to endorse the new EU negotiating position, signaling a lack of complete consensus within the bloc.

## Potential Alternatives and Compromises

Norway's environment minister, Andreas Bjelland Eriksen, suggested exploring "different approaches" to avoid a repeat of last year's failure. This includes potentially implementing some measures immediately while deferring other aspects of the regulation to a later stage. This hints at a possible phased approach or the exploration of alternative mechanisms beyond a simple carbon tax. One alternative may be a feebate system where efficient ships earn credits from those exceeding emissions targets. Such compromise proposals are likely to be discussed at the IMO, potentially including the interplay with existing schemes like the EU ETS.

## Impact on EU ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html)

While the global carbon pricing scheme is debated at the IMO, it's crucial to remember the EU has already implemented its own regional measures. The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) now includes shipping, requiring companies to purchase carbon credits for emissions generated within the EU and on voyages to and from EU ports. This adds a significant cost burden to shipping companies operating in the region. Moreover, ships are now subject to [CII ratings](/cii.html), which assess their operational carbon intensity and assign them a rating from A to E. Ships with poor ratings may face restrictions on their operations within the EU. The interplay between the EU ETS, CII ratings and a potential global carbon pricing mechanism creates a complex regulatory landscape for shipping companies. The cost of EU ETS allowances will impact [company rankings](/companies.html).

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The EU's renewed push for a global carbon price on shipping emissions signals a continued commitment to decarbonizing the industry. Even if a global agreement remains elusive, the EU's own measures, such as the ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html), are already driving changes in shipping operations. Companies operating in or trading with Europe must invest in efficiency measures and explore alternative fuels to mitigate the financial impact of carbon pricing and improve their CII ratings. The ongoing debates at the IMO will shape the future regulatory landscape for the industry, potentially leading to a more harmonized global approach to emissions reduction. The fact remains that even without full global agreement, the EU is moving forward and impacting a significant percentage of the world's shipping through its regional regulation.
