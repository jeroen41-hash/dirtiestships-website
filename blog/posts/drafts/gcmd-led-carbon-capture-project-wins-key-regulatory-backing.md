The global shipping industry faces intense pressure to decarbonize, grappling with escalating regulatory costs and the imperative to meet increasingly stringent emissions targets. In this challenging landscape, the prospect of Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage (OCCS) has garnered significant attention, though its practical application has been hampered by technical and regulatory uncertainties. A recent development from the Global Centre for Maritime Decarbonisation (GCMD)-led Project CAPTURED marks a pivotal step forward, securing crucial regulatory backing from both the EU ETS and the IMO, potentially accelerating the adoption of this technology as reported by shipandbunker.com. This dual recognition provides much-needed clarity on the compliance value and post-capture handling of CO2, impacting everything from carbon costs to company emissions profiles.

## EU ETS & The Compliance Value of Captured Carbon

The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) now covers maritime transport, imposing a direct cost on CO2 emissions from vessels calling at EU ports. Project CAPTURED's achievement of EU ETS recognition means that verified volumes of CO2 captured onboard ships can now be deducted from a vessel's reported emissions. This directly translates into a reduced requirement for surrendering EU Allowances (EUAs), thereby lowering a company's carbon liability under the scheme.

The critical condition for this deduction is that the captured CO2 must be "permanently bound in eligible products." Project CAPTURED demonstrated this by converting captured CO2 into stable industrial materials through carbon mineralisation. This provides a clear pathway for OCCS to offer tangible economic benefits, particularly for operators of high-emitting vessels frequently operating within EU waters. While the shipandbunker.com article states the project achieved 7.9% overall GHG savings across the full carbon value chain in its pilot, this initial figure, combined with EU ETS deductions, could offer a significant advantage for companies seeking to manage their [EU ETS costs](/news/#euts) and improve their standing in our [company rankings](/companies.html).

## IMO Backing and Permanent Carbon Storage Solutions

Beyond the EU, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) plays a crucial role in setting global standards. Project CAPTURED also received in-principle support from the IMO’s MEPC 84 for recognising carbon mineralisation as a form of permanent CO2 storage. This is a significant development, as it complements geological sequestration, which the IMO already accepts.

This IMO endorsement clarifies how captured CO2 can be treated after it leaves a vessel, providing a globally recognized method for ensuring permanent storage. For OCCS to scale, the logistics of offloading and safely storing captured carbon are paramount. Expanding the accepted options for permanent storage, such as carbon mineralisation, broadens the potential infrastructure solutions and reduces a key regulatory bottleneck for wider global adoption.

## OCCS as a Decarbonisation Pathway: A Reality Check

While these regulatory milestones are positive, it's essential to contextualize OCCS within the broader decarbonisation efforts. The demonstrated 7.9% GHG savings from Project CAPTURED's pilot in 2025 (which included capture, liquefaction, ship-to-ship transfer, and onshore mineralisation) is a promising start. However, achieving the IMO's ambitious targets of a 20% (striving for 30%) reduction by 2030 and 70% (striving for 80%) by 2040, ultimately reaching net-zero by 2050, will require a multitude of solutions.

For individual vessels, even a 7.9% reduction in CO2 emissions could be instrumental in improving or maintaining their [CII ratings](/cii.html). A vessel teetering on a 'D' or 'E' rating might find OCCS a viable option to improve its operational efficiency score, preventing potential penalties or restrictions. However, the energy penalty of OCCS systems, the space requirements onboard, and the nascent infrastructure for CO2 offloading and storage remain practical hurdles. The project involved 11 partners, including major players like Evergreen Marine, indicating serious industry interest and investment in making OCCS a practical reality for specific ship types and routes.

## The Road Ahead: Implementation and Data Verification

Despite the regulatory clarity, the widespread implementation of OCCS will depend heavily on robust infrastructure, economic viability, and rigorous monitoring. The EU ETS deduction is contingent on "verified volumes" of captured CO2, underscoring the critical need for robust Measurement, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) systems. As a platform tracking CO2 emissions via EU MRV data, DirtiestShips.com will be closely monitoring how captured emissions are reported and validated in actual vessel operations. The accurate tracking of captured CO2, distinguishing it from emitted CO2, will be crucial for maintaining transparency and preventing greenwashing.

The pilot successfully demonstrated the full end-to-end value chain, a significant technical feat. However, scaling this to thousands of vessels and establishing a global network of CO2 offloading and mineralisation facilities presents considerable logistical and financial challenges that will unfold over the coming decade.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The GCMD-led Project CAPTURED's regulatory breakthroughs for OCCS are a significant win for the maritime industry's decarbonisation efforts. They provide crucial clarity on how captured CO2 can be accounted for under the EU ETS, offering a direct pathway to reduce carbon costs, and establish IMO-backed global recognition for carbon mineralisation as a permanent storage solution. This bolsters OCCS as a credible, albeit partial, emissions reduction strategy, potentially impacting [CII ratings](/cii.html) and shifting [company rankings](/companies.html). While practical implementation and infrastructure development remain substantial challenges, these milestones move OCCS from a theoretical concept to a more tangible compliance option, which DirtiestShips.com will continue to track through our analysis of [emissions data](/charts.html) and [latest news](/news.html).
