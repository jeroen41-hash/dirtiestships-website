FuelEU Maritime Pooling Deals Highlight Complexity of Decarbonization

The recently announced FuelEU Maritime pooling agreement between Viking Line and Wallenius SOL underscores a critical aspect of the shipping industry's decarbonization journey: compliance flexibility. FuelEU Maritime, set to bite in 2025, introduces a complex system of incentives and penalties related to the greenhouse gas (GHG) intensity of energy used onboard ships. This agreement highlights how companies are strategizing to navigate these new regulations, leveraging the potential for surplus compliance units from low-emission vessels to offset emissions from those struggling to meet the targets.

## Bio-LNG: A Questionable Silver Bullet?

The shippax.com article focuses on the unique case of bio-LNG, specifically noting that Gasum's "premium bio-LNG" used by Viking Line and Wallenius SOL's gas-powered vessels boasts "negative emissions." This claim hinges on the biogas being produced from livestock waste. While, in theory, capturing and utilizing methane from this source could reduce overall atmospheric GHG concentrations, the carbon accounting behind these claims is often complex and contested. Lifecycle assessments are crucial, and issues such as methane leakage during production and transportation of bio-LNG can significantly impact the overall environmental benefit. The article lacks details on the specific lifecycle analysis used to support this negative emissions claim, raising questions about its validity. It's crucial to approach such claims with scrutiny, and to compare the results against standardized methodologies to assess the true environmental impact.

## FuelEU Maritime: Incentivizing Early Adoption

FuelEU Maritime introduces a mechanism where vessels exceeding emissions reduction targets can generate surplus compliance units, which can then be transferred to vessels within the same "pool" struggling to meet the required GHG intensity targets. This pooling system encourages early adoption of low- and zero-emission fuels, allowing companies to spread the cost of these technologies across their fleet. While this offers flexibility, it also creates potential for strategic maneuvering. For example, companies might prioritize low-emission fuels on select routes or vessels, while maintaining higher emissions on others, and then using pooling to achieve overall compliance. The effectiveness of FuelEU Maritime will depend on careful monitoring and enforcement to prevent such loopholes from undermining the overall emissions reduction goals.

## Connecting to the EU ETS and CII Ratings

FuelEU Maritime is just one piece of the EU's regulatory framework targeting shipping emissions. The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) now includes maritime transport, placing a price on CO2 emissions. [Company emissions rankings](/companies.html) will therefore increasingly reflect both operational efficiency (reflected in [CII ratings](/cii.html)) and fuel choices. Vessels with poor CII ratings will likely face higher EU ETS costs due to their higher CO2 emissions. FuelEU Maritime adds another layer of complexity, incentivizing the use of lower-GHG fuels beyond just CO2 reductions. Companies like Viking Line and Wallenius SOL need to navigate both the carbon pricing of the EU ETS *and* the GHG intensity requirements of FuelEU Maritime. Data from the EMSA MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification) system is critical for tracking progress towards both ETS compliance and FuelEU Maritime targets, providing the raw data from which the [emissions data](/charts.html) displayed on this site is derived.

## Strategic Implications for Shipping Companies

Deals like the Viking Line/Wallenius SOL agreement highlight the importance of strategic planning and collaboration in navigating the evolving regulatory landscape. Smaller shipping companies may find it advantageous to join larger pools, gaining access to compliance units they might not otherwise be able to generate themselves. Conversely, companies with a strong focus on decarbonization, perhaps through investments in alternative fuels or energy-efficient technologies, could potentially generate revenue by selling surplus compliance units. These pooling arrangements will likely become increasingly common as FuelEU Maritime comes into full effect, transforming the economics of fuel consumption in the shipping industry.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The FuelEU Maritime pooling mechanism, as exemplified by the Viking Line and Wallenius SOL agreement, introduces a layer of flexibility and complexity to the shipping industry's decarbonization efforts. While it incentivizes the adoption of low-emission fuels, it also requires careful monitoring and enforcement to prevent loopholes and ensure that overall emissions reduction goals are achieved. The effectiveness of FuelEU Maritime will ultimately depend on robust data collection and transparent reporting, as well as the willingness of companies to invest in truly sustainable solutions.
