The maritime industry's ongoing quest to decarbonize is heavily influenced by fuel choices, and LNG (liquefied natural gas) continues to assert its position as a key transition fuel despite tightening regulations and scrutiny of its overall environmental impact. A new report from Lloyd's Register (LR) highlights LNG's continued dominance in the alternative fuel space, focusing on orderbook growth, cost-effectiveness, and advancements in methane slip reduction. This has major implications for shipping companies navigating the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and aiming for higher [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## LNG Orderbook Swells Despite Uncertainty

According to LR’s “Fuel for thought: LNG” report, the dual-fuel LNG fleet has reached 1,665 vessels as of March 2026, with a substantial 982 vessels on order. This expansion signifies continued confidence in LNG as a viable alternative fuel, especially amidst regulatory pressures. The report notes that while recent developments may have introduced uncertainty impacting investment in other alternative fuels, LNG remains the preferred choice in the current orderbook. Growth is particularly strong in the container sector, poised to more than double its LNG-capable fleet, with cruise ships, tankers, and PCC/RoRo vessels also demonstrating increased adoption. These trends will certainly influence the [company rankings](/companies.html) based on emissions.

## Economic Advantages and Blending Potential

The LR report asserts that LNG remains the most cost-effective alternative fuel pathway to 2050 under current and proposed regulatory frameworks. Furthermore, the report suggests that blending LNG with bio-LNG and e-LNG could enhance this advantage, potentially allowing LNG-fueled vessels to achieve compliance surpluses in the initial years of new regulations. This aspect is critical for shipping companies operating within the EU ETS, as lower emissions can translate to reduced carbon costs. The ability to blend with biofuels offers a potential pathway to further reduce the carbon footprint and improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## Methane Slip Reduction: A Crucial Development

A significant portion of the LR report focuses on progress made in mitigating methane slip, a major concern associated with LNG as a fuel. The report indicates that high-pressure two-stroke engines are now achieving methane slip as low as 0.2 g/kWh, while low-pressure engines equipped with exhaust gas recirculation are delivering reductions exceeding 60%. This technical progress is vital for addressing the greenhouse gas (GHG) intensity of LNG and enhancing its overall environmental profile. However, the report emphasizes that current emissions factors under FuelEU Maritime and the IMO’s Lifecycle Assessment guidelines do not fully reflect these improvements. Updating these factors is essential to accurately reflect the actual emissions performance of LNG-fueled vessels and incentivize further technological advancements, as measured by data from the EMSA MRV database.

## Upstream Emissions and Fuel Certification

Beyond engine technology, the LR report highlights the importance of addressing upstream emissions and implementing robust fuel certification schemes. Cleaner LNG production, reduced methane leakage during extraction and transportation, and the widespread adoption of independently verified certification schemes are crucial for shipowners to realize the full emissions reduction potential of LNG. These considerations extend beyond the operational emissions captured by the EU MRV data and influence the overall lifecycle assessment of LNG as a transition fuel. Greater transparency and standardization in fuel certification will be essential for ensuring accurate reporting and compliance under evolving environmental regulations.

## Operational Considerations and Safety

The report also delves into the operational considerations of LNG-fueled vessels during drydocking, providing recommendations for planning, execution, and safety protocols. While not directly related to emissions, these operational aspects are critical for ensuring the safe and efficient adoption of LNG as a fuel across the maritime industry.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The continued growth of the LNG-fueled fleet, coupled with advancements in methane slip reduction and the potential for blending with renewable sources, suggests that LNG will continue to play a significant role in the maritime sector's transition towards decarbonization. However, the accuracy of emissions reporting, particularly concerning methane slip and upstream emissions, remains a critical factor. Aligning regulatory frameworks with technological advancements, promoting robust fuel certification schemes, and addressing upstream emissions are essential for ensuring that LNG genuinely contributes to reducing shipping emissions and improving [CII ratings](/cii.html).
