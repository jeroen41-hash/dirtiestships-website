The US is leading a charge to completely abandon the IMO's proposed Net-Zero Framework (NZF), threatening to derail international shipping's pathway to decarbonization. This framework, initially approved in principle at MEPC 83, aimed to establish a global fuel standard and emissions pricing mechanism, essential components for achieving net-zero emissions by 2050. The US opposition throws the future of these regulations into serious doubt.

## US Calls for Scrapping the Net-Zero Framework

According to a recent article in splash247.com, the US submission to the IMO argues that the NZF is a "flawed" approach that could transform the IMO into a "global climate bank." The US delegation believes the framework unfairly penalizes existing technologies and relies on expensive, unproven fuels. They are advocating for an "energy-all" approach, which would protect the use of LNG, nuclear, and conventional fuels. Furthermore, the US suggests the proposed March 2027 entry into force is legally impossible. This stance directly contradicts the goals of initiatives like the EU ETS and could complicate efforts to accurately assess vessel [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## Japan Seeks a Compromise

While the US is pushing for complete abandonment, other major maritime nations are attempting to find common ground. Japan, the world's third-largest shipowning nation, is proposing significant revisions to the NZF to reach a consensus. Recognizing the strong disagreements surrounding a global carbon tax, Japan suggests removing mandatory payments to the NZF. Instead of a "tax," Japan proposes a system where ships balance their GHG Fuel Intensity (GFI) deficits through Surplus Units (SUs) traded in the market. This approach could potentially offer more flexibility to shipowners compared to a strict carbon tax, but its effectiveness in driving emissions reductions remains to be seen. Japan also advocates for revising the GFI targets, claiming the current targets are unrealistic and could lead to premature and costly ship replacements. The suggested constant direct compliance targets from 2028 to 2035 aim to provide the industry with foreseeable compliance pathways.

## China Focuses on Technical Methodologies

China, the world's largest shipowning nation, is primarily concerned with the technical methodologies that will underpin future fuel regulations. Beijing is strongly opposing what it views as discriminatory accounting for e-fuels. China argues that including "embodied emissions" – the carbon footprint of manufacturing renewable energy infrastructure – solely for e-fuels is unfair. This concern highlights the complexities of lifecycle emissions accounting and the need for a standardized approach. Furthermore, China is advocating for the inclusion of onboard carbon capture and storage (OCCS) in default emission values, arguing that the technology's potential should not be stifled. This is particularly relevant as companies seek to improve their position in [company rankings](/companies.html) that are based on well-to-wake carbon accounting principles.

## The IMO's Delicate Balancing Act

IMO secretary-general Arsenio Dominguez faces a challenging task in navigating these divergent views and attempting to salvage the NZF. The IMO's goal is to "get things done" in 2026, moving from planning to concrete actions and measurable progress. However, the strong opposition from the US and the complex technical issues raised by China highlight the significant hurdles that remain. The success of any future regulations will depend on achieving a balance between environmental ambition, economic feasibility, and technological neutrality. The current situation could potentially slow down the implementation of effective measures to reduce shipping emissions, impacting the availability of up-to-date information displayed at our [emissions data](/charts.html).

## What this means for shipping emissions

The potential abandonment of the IMO's Net-Zero Framework presents a significant setback to the decarbonization of the shipping industry. Without a global fuel standard and emissions pricing mechanism, it will be more challenging to incentivize the adoption of low- and zero-emission fuels and technologies. The uncertainty surrounding future regulations could also delay investments in cleaner shipping solutions. The current situation underscores the need for continued dialogue and collaboration among IMO member states to find a pathway forward that addresses both environmental concerns and the economic realities of the shipping industry. The cost of EU ETS allowances adds further complexity, as the lack of a global framework may disproportionately affect companies operating within the EU. The data collected under EMSA's MRV regulation is vital to monitor regional effects.
