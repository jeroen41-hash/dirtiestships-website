The container shipping industry is facing a challenging start to 2024, with falling freight rates coinciding with rising operational costs, particularly those linked to the EU's expanding emissions regulations. This squeeze, as highlighted in a recent market-insights.upply.com report, is forcing container lines to significantly increase Energy Transition Surcharges (ETS) to offset the growing costs of compliance.

## ETS Surcharges Surge in Response to Expanding EU Regulations

The core issue revolves around the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) and FuelEU Maritime regulations. Shipping companies are now grappling with the financial implications of these regulations, leading to substantial increases in ETS surcharges. According to the upply.com report, these surcharges are averaging $170 per 40’ dry container and $250 per 40’ reefer container on Asia to North Europe routes during the first quarter of 2024. These increases are directly attributed to the stricter requirements imposed by the EU ETS, which will progressively include a larger percentage of shipping emissions: 40% in 2024, 75% in 2025, and 100% from 2026 onward. Furthermore, the expansion of the EU ETS to encompass methane and nitrous oxide emissions, in addition to CO2, further compounds the financial burden. This aligns with the EU's broader strategy to decarbonize maritime transport, but places immediate cost pressures on carriers.

## FuelEU Maritime and Biofuel Price Volatility

Beyond the EU ETS, FuelEU Maritime regulations are also contributing to the increased surcharges. The upply.com article mentions that shipping companies are incurring extra costs due to changing biofuel prices as they strive to meet the FuelEU Maritime requirements. This regulation mandates a gradual reduction in the greenhouse gas intensity of energy used onboard ships, pushing companies to explore alternative fuels like biofuels. However, the price volatility and availability challenges associated with biofuels add to the operational expenses, which are then passed on to customers through the ETS surcharges. The article lacks specifics on the magnitude of these costs, but they clearly represent a significant factor driving up prices.

## Impact on Container Shipping Companies and [CII ratings](/cii.html)

While the upply.com report does not explicitly name specific companies, the increased ETS surcharges will undoubtedly impact the profitability of all container shipping lines operating in and out of EU ports. These costs will likely affect companies' ability to invest in cleaner technologies and strategies to improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html). A lower CII rating, indicating poorer carbon intensity, can lead to penalties and reduced competitiveness within the EU market. It will be crucial to see how these surcharges affect overall freight rates and whether shippers are willing to absorb these additional costs or seek alternative, potentially less compliant, carriers. ## Quarterly Reviews and Carbon Price Fluctuations

The article states that ETS surcharges are reviewed quarterly, reflecting fluctuations in the price per tonne of carbon. This highlights the dynamic nature of carbon pricing and its direct influence on shipping costs. As the price of carbon increases, we can expect further adjustments to these surcharges, potentially exacerbating the financial pressure on container shipping companies. It also introduces an element of uncertainty for shippers, making it difficult to predict transportation costs in the long term. ## Spot Rates and Capacity Dynamics

The report highlights that falling spot freight rates coinciding with increased operating costs related to EU regulations creates a challenging environment for container shipping companies. Pre-Chinese New Year capacity outstripping demand has put downward pressure on revenue per container. This means companies are earning less while simultaneously facing higher expenses associated with compliance. This squeeze could potentially lead to consolidation within the industry as smaller players struggle to compete.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The rising ETS surcharges signal a significant shift in the economic landscape of container shipping. While these surcharges are intended to incentivize emission reductions, their immediate impact is to increase costs for shippers and consumers. Whether these increased costs will translate into meaningful reductions in shipping emissions remains to be seen. It will depend on how effectively companies invest in cleaner technologies, transition to alternative fuels, and optimize their operations. If companies are merely passing on the costs without making substantial changes to their emissions profiles, the EU ETS may fall short of its decarbonization goals.
