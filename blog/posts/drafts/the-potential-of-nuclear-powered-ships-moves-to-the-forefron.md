The potential for nuclear-powered merchant ships is gaining momentum as a zero-emission alternative to fossil fuels, with industry leaders planning to seriously examine nuclear propulsion at Posidonia 2026. This shift comes as the shipping industry faces increasing pressure to decarbonize under regulations like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the [CII ratings](/cii.html) scheme, making the prospect of long-term, carbon-free operation increasingly attractive.

## Nuclear Propulsion: From Concept to Commercial Reality?

CORE POWER, a developer of maritime nuclear propulsion systems, will host an Executive Briefing at Posidonia 2026 to assess the commercial viability of advanced nuclear technologies. According to Charlotte Vere, Group Head of Market Development at CORE POWER, discussions have moved beyond theoretical considerations, with engagement from governments, shipowners, banks, insurers, and ports. The seminar will explore how nuclear propulsion could enhance fleet competitiveness through long refueling intervals (5-7 years), insulation from fuel price volatility, and greater operational flexibility. The company also envisions floating nuclear platforms supplying clean energy to ports, potentially reducing reliance on shore-side power generated from fossil fuels.

## Industry Perspectives: Cautious Optimism

While some in the industry see nuclear power as the "only true green solution," others maintain a more cautious approach. Dr. George Pateras, Deputy Chairman of Contships Management, believes that fourth-generation molten salt reactors (MSRs) using thorium fuel could arrive within 10-15 years. Hanwha Ocean, a South Korean shipbuilder, notes that major European container carriers and energy companies have begun exploring nuclear-powered vessels. However, their assessment remains at an early stage, with commercial deployment potentially not feasible until the late 2030s at the earliest. Samsung Heavy Industries, which is studying MSR-powered container ships and LNG carriers, also points to the mid-2030s as a realistic timeframe. These timelines align with the development cycles for new ship technologies and the need for robust safety and regulatory frameworks.

## Regulatory and Logistical Hurdles

Several challenges must be addressed before nuclear-powered ships can become a widespread reality. Charlotte Vere highlights regulatory alignment, liability frameworks, port acceptance, and insurance structures as critical workstreams. Panos Kourkountis, Chairman of MARTECMA and Technical Director at Sea Traders S.A., stresses the importance of radioactive waste management and long-term environmental stewardship. Public perception also plays a significant role, as the term "nuclear" carries a heavy legacy. Dr. John Kokarakis, Chair of SNAME Greek Section and Technical Director at Bureau Veritas, emphasizes that commercial readiness depends on port-state acceptance, crew training pipelines, standardised reactor designs, and internationally harmonised liability conventions. The IMO is currently modernizing the 1981 Code of Safety for Nuclear Merchant Ships, a critical step towards establishing a global regulatory framework.

## Linking Nuclear to Existing Regulations and Costs

If nuclear ships become a reality, it could significantly alter the landscape of maritime emissions regulations and costs. Nuclear propulsion eliminates operational CO₂ emissions, directly improving a vessel's [CII ratings](/cii.html) and potentially avoiding substantial costs under the EU ETS. Companies that adopt nuclear power could see a major boost in [company rankings](/companies.html) based on emissions performance. While the initial investment in nuclear technology is likely to be high, the long-term operational cost savings and carbon-free operation could provide a competitive advantage. The availability of reliable, long-term emissions data from sources like the EMSA MRV could help track the impact of nuclear-powered ships as they enter the fleet.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The increasing interest in nuclear propulsion signals a growing recognition that achieving zero emissions in shipping will require radical technological shifts. While significant hurdles remain, the potential for nuclear power to eliminate operational CO₂ emissions and provide long-term cost stability makes it a compelling option for a carbon-constrained future. Whether nuclear propulsion emerges as a major player will depend on overcoming regulatory, logistical, and public perception challenges, but the conversation is undeniably shifting towards serious consideration.
