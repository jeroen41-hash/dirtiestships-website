The global shipping industry faces increasing volatility, not just from market fluctuations but from geopolitical events with cascading effects on energy supply, costs, and ultimately, carbon emissions. The recent ICIS outlook on European energy markets for H2 2026 paints a stark picture: the Iran war and the disruption to LNG flows through the Strait of Hormuz have upended expectations, driving gas prices higher and creating significant uncertainty. For shipping, this translates directly into a more challenging regulatory and operational environment, impacting everything from EU ETS compliance costs to the feasibility of achieving stringent CII ratings.

### The Geopolitical Wake: Longer Routes, Higher Burn

The prolonged closure of the Strait of Hormuz, a critical choke point for 20-30% of global LNG trade, has immediate and profound implications for shipping. ICIS experts project a potential closure extending to March 2027 under a prolonged blockade scenario, even as a base case anticipates a partial reopening by August 2026. This forces vessels, particularly LNG carriers, to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, a significantly longer journey than the Suez Canal.

Andreas Schroeder, Head of Gas Analytics at ICIS, highlights that "journey times to Europe of around four weeks around the Cape of Good Hope" would replace the "shorter 18-day-route through the Suez Canal." This near-doubling of transit time directly translates to increased fuel consumption per voyage. For vessels already operating on tight margins and under pressure to reduce their carbon footprint, these extended routes represent a substantial setback. The additional fuel burn, documented through [EMSA MRV data](/emissions-data), will invariably inflate a ship's Annual Efficiency Ratio (AER), making it harder to maintain or improve its [CII ratings](/cii.html).

### Rising Energy Costs: The ETS Impact

The disruption in energy markets is forecast to keep gas prices elevated. Even with a projected reopening of Hormuz by August, ICIS expects gas prices to remain "higher than current market levels," with a prolonged blockade pushing prices closer to the "55-60 €/MWh range." While this outlook focuses on gas, such significant upward pressure inevitably ripples through the broader energy complex, influencing the price of marine fuels like VLSFO and MGO.

Higher fuel costs directly impact a shipping company's operational expenditure. Crucially, the ICIS outlook notes that "carbon markets saw heightened volatility amid concerns over potential political intervention and the future direction of EU climate policy." This instability in the carbon market, combined with rising energy prices, creates a perfect storm for EU ETS compliance. Shipping companies are already grappling with the phased inclusion of maritime emissions into the EU ETS. Any upward swing in EUA (EU Allowance) prices due to energy market instability or policy concerns will significantly inflate the cost of emitting CO2 for voyages within or to/from the EU. This financial burden, visible in our ongoing [company rankings](/companies.html), could divert crucial investment away from decarbonization initiatives and into simply covering operational costs.

### CII Challenges on the Horizon

The dual pressures of longer routes and higher fuel prices will make it exceptionally difficult for many vessels to meet their Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) targets. CII ratings are directly linked to a ship's operational efficiency and annual emissions. Longer voyages around the Cape of Good Hope mean more fuel burned for the same cargo moved, leading to a poorer AER and thus a worse CII grade.

Companies striving to improve from D or E ratings to compliant C grades will find their efforts undermined by factors beyond their direct control. While slow steaming is a common strategy to improve CII, the economic pressure from higher fuel costs might ironically force some operators to maintain faster speeds to recoup costs or adhere to schedules, exacerbating their emissions problem. The need for Europe to secure "1.3 more LNG cargoes per day" to meet winter storage targets further illustrates the increased demand for shipping capacity and potentially faster transit times in specific segments, which could conflict with CII objectives. This complex interplay of economics, geopolitics, and environmental regulation will be a key area of analysis in future [CII ratings](/cii.html) updates.

### What this means for shipping emissions

The H2 2026 outlook for European energy markets signals a period of sustained pressure on the global shipping industry. Geopolitical instability in the Middle East will lead to longer voyages, increased fuel consumption, and consequently, higher CO2 emissions per tonne-mile for affected routes. This, coupled with elevated energy prices and volatile carbon markets, will drive up operational costs, notably for EU ETS compliance. Meeting ambitious decarbonization targets, particularly maintaining strong CII ratings, will become increasingly challenging. Companies will need to leverage advanced operational efficiencies and potentially accelerate investment in alternative fuels where feasible, even as the immediate economic landscape discourages such moves.
