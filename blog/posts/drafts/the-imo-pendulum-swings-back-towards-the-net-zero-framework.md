The global shipping industry's path to decarbonization remains fraught with uncertainty, but recent developments at the IMO's MEPC 84 suggest a potential resurgence of the Net Zero Framework (NZF). This framework, agreed upon in principle in April 2025, is crucial for setting concrete targets and implementing mechanisms to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from ships. The outcome of MEPC 84 indicates a renewed commitment from a majority of member states to use the NZF as the basis for further negotiations, a critical step after setbacks encountered at MEPC.ES2. This news is essential for DirtiestShips.com readers as it directly impacts future regulations that will influence [CII ratings](/cii.html), the financial burdens of [EU ETS carbon costs](/charts.html), and ultimately, [company emissions rankings](/companies.html).

## Shifting Sands at MEPC 84: A Closer Look

The UCL Shipping and Oceans Research Group's analysis of MEPC 84 reveals a complex interplay of shifting alliances and persistent disagreements. While a majority of 59 member states now support the NZF, this support is not unanimous. The article points out that five countries "flipped back" to supporting the NZF, while ten countries that previously abstained or took no position are now in support. Crucially, only two countries shifted from opposing the adjournment of the NZF to opposing the framework itself. This suggests that opposition is consolidating, but remains in the minority.

Alternative proposals from Argentina and Japan failed to garner significant backing, highlighting the difficulty of building consensus around drastically different approaches. The Argentinian proposal, focused solely on technical elements without a GHG price or fund, received support from 24 member states. The Japanese proposal garnered only 7 supporters. The lack of support for these alternatives underscores the NZF's position as the most viable path forward, at least for now.

## Technical vs. Market-Based Measures: The Ongoing Debate

The article highlights the tension between technical measures and market-based mechanisms in achieving decarbonization. The NZF, as originally conceived, incorporates both elements, including a potential GHG levy. The alternative proposal from Argentina, focused exclusively on technical solutions, reflects a preference by some nations to avoid carbon pricing mechanisms.

This divergence is highly relevant to the [EU ETS](/charts.html). The EU ETS imposes a carbon cost on shipping companies operating within its jurisdiction, incentivizing emissions reductions through market forces. The IMO's eventual strategy will significantly impact the effectiveness and global reach of regional regulations like the EU ETS. If the IMO adopts a weaker, technical-only approach, the EU ETS may need to be adjusted to maintain its ambition.

## Implications for Company Emissions and CII Ratings

The regulatory framework established by the IMO directly affects the operational costs and environmental performance of shipping companies. The [CII ratings](/cii.html), which assess a ship's annual operational carbon intensity, are likely to be influenced by any new regulations arising from the NZF. Stronger regulations, including carbon pricing, will incentivize companies to invest in fuel-efficient technologies and alternative fuels to improve their CII ratings.

Companies with poor [CII ratings](/cii.html) may face financial penalties, operational restrictions, and reputational damage. Therefore, the outcome of the ongoing negotiations at the IMO will have a direct impact on the [company rankings](/companies.html) tracked by DirtiestShips.com. Companies that proactively adopt decarbonization strategies are likely to perform better under stricter regulatory regimes.

## Uncertainty Remains: What's Next?

Despite the positive signals from MEPC 84, the path forward remains uncertain. The article emphasizes that "there is no certainty on the outcome to future work," and points to the upcoming intersessional meeting (ISWG GHG 22) in September as a critical juncture. The final specifications of any agreement are likely to remain contentious until an amendment is adopted, potentially at the MEPC.ES2 meeting in October 2025.

The article suggests that opposition to the NZF may now focus on achieving "modest modifications" rather than outright rejection. However, the risk remains that dissenting voices could stall or weaken the final agreement. Continued monitoring of these negotiations is crucial for understanding the evolving regulatory landscape.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The renewed support for the Net Zero Framework at MEPC 84 is a potentially positive sign for reducing shipping emissions. However, significant challenges remain in reaching a final agreement that balances ambition, equity, and practicality. The outcome of these negotiations will directly impact the regulatory burdens and investment decisions of shipping companies, ultimately shaping the industry's trajectory towards decarbonization. The next few months will be critical in determining whether the momentum towards the NZF can be sustained and translated into concrete action.
