The Union of Greek Shipowners (UGS) has taken a significant step in the ongoing battle over international shipping emissions, reaching out to Liberia and Japan to develop alternative proposals to the International Maritime Organization’s (IMO) Net-Zero Framework. This move, coupled with UGS's fervent call for the EU to roll back its own maritime regulations, underscores a growing chasm between industry concerns and the escalating pressure for decarbonization, with profound implications for global CO2 emissions and the economic future of shipping.

### The IMO's Net-Zero Framework: A Delayed Global Carbon Price

The IMO's Net-Zero Framework (NZF), envisioned to establish a mandatory greenhouse gas (GHG) price mechanism, faces significant hurdles. Its adoption was delayed until October 2025, allowing countries like Liberia and Japan to propose alternatives that would remove the mandatory GHG pricing. The UGS, representing the world's largest shipowning nation by fleet size, intends to work with these countries to refine proposals, with UGS President Melina Travlos noting that while "some parts of their proposals are positive, some parts are not feasible."

The core intent of the NZF is to internalize the cost of carbon emissions, thereby incentivizing a shift to lower-carbon bunker fuels. However, as our data tracking at DirtiestShips.com consistently shows through [emissions data](/charts.html), the transition remains economically challenging. Current delivered bunker prices for 0.5%-sulfur fuel oil in Rotterdam were $17/Gigajoule in April, significantly cheaper than LNG at $21/Gj and bio-LNG at $35/Gj. Without a robust, globally consistent carbon price, the economic incentive to choose greener (and currently more expensive) fuels is severely limited, impacting global emissions trajectories. The UGS argues that without sufficient sustainable fuel availability, a GHG cost mechanism would simply "tax ship operators" rather than genuinely drive decarbonization.

### EU ETS Under Fire: A Battle for Competitiveness

The UGS's criticism extends forcefully to the European Union, reiterating calls for the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) for shipping and FuelEU Maritime rules to be withdrawn should a global IMO framework be confirmed. Travlos stated that "ETS and FuelEU are hurting the European shipping industry’s competitiveness," highlighting a central point of contention for many operators, particularly those featured in our [company rankings](/companies.html).

Greece, despite being the largest shipowning country within the EU (with Greek-owned ships accounting for over 19% of the global fleet by UGS figures), notably abstained from the IMO's vote last October. This signals the internal EU tension, as Greece is bound by EU laws to act in unity. The UGS's strong stance against EU regulations like the ETS is rooted in concerns over market distortion and revenue allocation. UGS board member John Lyras called the ETS a "sham," arguing that the EU is collecting emission charges (estimated at over Eur7 billion, or $8.2 billion, per year from ship operators) without adequately funding maritime decarbonization. The European Commission has pledged Eur1.6 billion by 2030 for decarbonization projects and provided Eur600 million to shipping projects from the Innovation Fund since 2020 – figures that the industry perceives as disproportionate to the revenue generated.

These debates over unilateral vs. global regulation are critical. Our analysis of [CII ratings](/cii.html) and EMSA MRV data regularly shows how different regulatory environments, be they regional like the EU's or potentially global like the IMO's NZF, can create disparate pressures and compliance costs for ship operators.

### Greek Shipping's Global Influence and the Regulatory Divide

With Greek-owned ships comprising a substantial portion of the global fleet and 26% of the oil tanker fleet by deadweight tonnage, the UGS's position carries immense weight. Last year, EU port calls accounted for 13.7% of the total for the Greek-owned fleet in deadweight tonnage, underscoring the direct impact of EU regulations on this massive fleet.

The EU has indicated it would review its regulations to avoid double-counting once the IMO finalizes the NZF. While FuelEU, with its similar design to the NZF, might be phased out, many industry observers believe it's "highly unlikely" the EU would exclude shipping from the ETS, given its significant contribution to national revenues. This regulatory overlap and potential for "double-counting" creates complexity and uncertainty for operators trying to plan for decarbonization, affecting investment in new technologies and cleaner fuels.

### What this means for shipping emissions

The current landscape reveals a stark division: the industry, led by major players like Greek shipowners, seeks a harmonized, global approach from the IMO while pushing back on regional regulations seen as detrimental to competitiveness. Environmental groups and many shipping professionals, conversely, emphasize the urgency of a robust, mandatory carbon pricing mechanism to accelerate the energy transition. The ongoing delay in the IMO's Net-Zero Framework and the active lobbying against a mandatory GHG price, coupled with the UGS's resistance to the EU ETS, signifies a critical juncture for shipping's decarbonization efforts. Without clear, consistent, and effective carbon pricing, the transition to sustainable marine fuels will continue to lag, making the IMO's ambitious net-zero targets increasingly difficult to achieve. The coming discussions at the IMO and the EU's response will dictate the trajectory of global shipping emissions for the foreseeable future.
