The first half of 2026 has witnessed an extraordinary surge in the global shipping market, with the ClarkSea Index soaring by 61% year-on-year to an impressive $38,717/day, driven largely by geopolitical disruptions. While these figures herald a period of significant prosperity for many shipping companies, they simultaneously cast a long shadow over the industry's crucial decarbonization efforts. The very factors fueling this boom—extended voyages, operational inefficiencies, and strong demand—are fundamentally escalating CO2 emissions, putting renewed pressure on regulatory compliance frameworks like the EU ETS and the IMO's CII ratings at a time when the "green transition consensus" is reportedly stalled.

## Geopolitics, Disruption, and the Emissions Cost

The primary catalyst for this market upswing has been persistent geopolitical disruption, notably the 95% drop in transits through the Strait of Hormuz and ongoing Red Sea re-routing. These events, as highlighted by Clarksons Research, have forced a shift to longer-haul voyages (e.g., US-Asia), creating what the report terms "disruption upside" for various shipping sectors. Tankers, for instance, experienced their strongest rate environment on record, averaging $82,000/day, while VLGC rates peaked near $200,000/day. Container freight rates are at their highest outside of the Covid-19 pandemic.

However, this commercial success comes with a steep environmental price. Longer routes directly translate to increased fuel consumption and, consequently, higher CO2 emissions per journey. When combined with the operational inefficiencies from ships stuck outside chokepoints and re-positioning demands, the total carbon footprint of the global fleet undoubtedly swelled in 1H 2026. This trend will be starkly evident in upcoming [emissions data](/charts.html) derived from EMSA MRV submissions, revealing the true scale of this disruption-driven emissions surge. The sustained operation of an "ageing non-eco fleet," which the report notes remains a "release valve" for markets, exacerbates this problem, as these vessels typically burn more fuel per nautical mile than their modern counterparts.

## The Carbon Cost of Prosperity: EU ETS and CII Implications

For vessels trading in or out of EU ports, the robust market conditions and resulting increase in emissions have direct financial and compliance ramifications. The [EU ETS carbon costs](/news.html) will rise significantly for companies, as more voyages—often longer and faster to capitalize on high rates—mean higher carbon liabilities. While current profits might absorb these costs, the long-term pressure to decarbonize intensifies.

More critically, the surge in emissions will severely challenge vessels' [CII ratings](/cii.html). The Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) measures a ship's operational carbon efficiency. Longer voyages at potentially higher speeds, driven by market demand, will likely push many vessels towards lower, less favorable CII grades (D or E). Shipping companies must now navigate the paradox of record profits alongside a deteriorating environmental performance, risking non-compliance and reputational damage. The "ageing non-eco fleet" is particularly vulnerable here, facing even greater difficulty in meeting CII requirements without significant operational adjustments or costly retrofits.

## A Boom in New Orders, But What Kind of Green?

Amidst the booming charter markets, newbuild ordering has seen a significant uptick, up 33% on the full year 2025 run rate. The global orderbook has grown by 10% since the start of 2026, reaching 21% of the fleet by mid-year. Tankers are leading this surge, with 150 VLCCs ordered—the largest annual tally since 1973—along with record LPG and strong containership orders. This expansion in the fleet, coupled with increasing shipyard output (up 14% y-o-y, dominated by Chinese capacity), points to a future with potentially greater overall emissions if not counteracted by greener designs.

However, the Clarksons report critically notes a "Green transition consensus stalled with continued regulatory uncertainty." This is perhaps the most concerning takeaway for environmental advocates. While investment is high, the focus appears to be on expanding capacity to meet current demand rather than a decisive pivot towards zero-emission technologies. If these new orders predominantly comprise conventional fossil-fuel-powered vessels or only incrementally more efficient designs, the industry risks locking in substantial CO2 emissions for decades to come, undermining global climate goals and raising questions about the long-term sustainability of [company rankings](/companies.html) based on emission performance.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The first half of 2026 presents a stark paradox: record profits for the shipping industry driven by geopolitical instability, yet simultaneously an alarming escalation in CO2 emissions and a perceived pause in the green transition. The market forces pushing for longer voyages and higher speeds are directly counteracting decarbonization efforts, creating a significant challenge for regulatory compliance, especially with EU ETS costs and increasingly stringent CII ratings. Without a renewed commitment to green technologies and clear regulatory certainty, the current market boom risks becoming an environmental bust, making the need for transparent, data-driven platforms like DirtiestShips.com more critical than ever to track and expose the true carbon cost of global trade.
