The pressure to decarbonize shipping is intensifying, and as Australia looks to diversify its energy sources, low-carbon liquid fuels (LCLFs) are emerging as a key potential solution for hard-to-abate sectors like international shipping. With the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) now including maritime, and the [CII ratings](/cii.html) pushing for operational efficiency, the urgency to find viable alternatives to traditional bunker fuel has never been greater.

## Power-to-Liquid (PtL) and Biogenic Fuels: Two Pathways

According to a recent report from csiro.au, Australia is exploring two primary pathways for producing LCLFs: Power-to-Liquid (PtL) and biogenic fuels. PtL, also known as e-fuels, involves synthesizing fuels by combining hydrogen with captured carbon dioxide. This process can yield jet fuel, methanol, or diesel, and even ammonia – a zero-carbon fuel with significant potential for the shipping industry, as noted by Dr. Daniel Roberts of CSIRO. The second pathway focuses on biogenic fuels, which are derived from converting biomass and waste into liquid fuels. Australia's diverse feedstocks, including forestry residues, agricultural waste, and urban waste streams, present a significant opportunity in this area.

## Addressing the Hard-to-Electrify Sectors

While electric vehicles are gaining traction, the report emphasizes that electrification is unlikely to be a complete solution for heavy industry and transport, particularly international shipping. These sectors require high-energy-density fuels, making LCLFs a more practical alternative. Dr. Roberts points out that "it’s the international marine shipping fuel… where electrification is unlikely to be able to do the heavy lifting, so we're looking at ways of supporting the transition to lower carbon versions.” The article does not mention specific emission reduction percentages or targets related to these alternative fuels in shipping.

## Scaling Up Production and Reducing Costs

Both PtL and biogenic fuel production face challenges in scaling up to meet the demands of the shipping industry. The report highlights the need for "big power station-sized plants" to process thousands of tonnes of feedstock a day or utilize hundreds of megawatts of electrolyzers and carbon capture systems. Cost reduction is also a critical factor. CSIRO is focusing on developing new hydrogen production technologies, more efficient carbon capture approaches, and better systems integration to lower the overall costs of PtL fuels. For biogenic fuels, the focus is on collaborating with project proponents to ensure plants are well-designed and operated. The article cites HAMR Energy as one example of a company CSIRO is working with. Successfully scaling alternative fuel production will have a knock-on impact on [company rankings](/companies.html) as they adapt their fleets.

## Australia's Opportunity and Global Collaboration

Australia is well-positioned to capitalize on LCLF production due to its abundant renewable energy potential and diverse waste streams. The country's existing research infrastructure and collaborations with international partners, such as the Australia-India trial for using agricultural waste in steelmaking, provide a solid foundation for future development. However, the article lacks specific details on investment levels or policy support for LCLF development in Australia. It would be beneficial to see further analysis of how Australia's LCLF initiatives could integrate with global efforts to reduce shipping emissions and comply with regulations like the EU ETS. The cost of carbon under the EU ETS is a key factor driving uptake, making any viable fuel alternative increasingly attractive.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The development of LCLFs in Australia represents a significant opportunity to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from international shipping. While challenges remain in scaling up production and reducing costs, the two-pronged approach of PtL and biogenic fuels offers a promising pathway towards decarbonizing this hard-to-abate sector. Success will require ongoing research, collaboration, and supportive policies to accelerate deployment and ensure the economic viability of these alternative fuels. The potential for LCLFs to mitigate the impact of shipping on the environment is substantial, and as regulations like the EU ETS and the [CII ratings](/cii.html) scheme tighten, the pressure on shipping companies to adopt these solutions will only increase. Monitoring the progress of these initiatives and their impact on emissions data via [emissions data](/charts.html) will be crucial in assessing their effectiveness.
