The global shipping industry's decarbonization journey faces a significant navigational challenge, as recent data reveals a marked slowdown in orders for alternative-fuelled vessels. This cautious pivot by shipowners to a more diversified, 'hedged' approach to fuel investments carries profound implications for meeting ambitious emissions reduction targets, impacting future EU ETS carbon costs and the long-term viability of vessels under increasingly stringent CII ratings.

## A Measured Slowdown in Alternative Fuel Uptake

According to DNV’s Alternative Fuels Insight (AFI) platform, May saw orders for just 36 alternative-fuelled vessels, bringing the total for 2026 to 119 vessels through the end of the month. This pace is "significantly lower" than during the same period in 2025, highlighting a shift in investment momentum.

The May orders were heavily skewed towards LPG and ethane carriers, accounting for 26 vessels, followed by eight LNG-fuelled vessels (six containerships, two car carriers), and two ethanol-fuelled bulk carriers. Year-to-date, LNG remains the primary choice, representing 60 of the 119 orders, predominantly for container vessels (42) and vehicle carriers (12). LPG/ethane carriers follow closely with 50 orders. However, orders for truly zero-carbon fuels like methanol/ethanol (4), ammonia (4), and hydrogen (1) remain notably limited.

This slowdown is particularly striking given the broader context of significant investment. The World Shipping Council reported last month that the combined fleet of dual-fuel containerships and vehicle carriers has surpassed 1,200 vessels delivered or on order, representing over $180 billion in private investment. While liner shipping has historically led this charge, with 78% of containerships and 94% of vehicle carriers on order being alternative-fuel capable, the current dip in new orders suggests a re-evaluation of strategy.

## The Strategic Shift: Hedging Against Uncertainty

Jason Stefanatos, Global Decarbonization Director at DNV Maritime, attributes this evolving market dynamic to owners balancing regulatory uncertainty, fuel availability, and long-term investment risks. Instead of making a "single bet" on one future fuel, shipowners are increasingly viewing fuel choice as a "portfolio decision." This involves managing fuel optionality, timing of investment, and exposure to future regulation to navigate the decisions for long-life assets.

This strategic hedging is manifesting in several ways. While container shipping still leads alternative-fuel adoption, ordering trends are shifting. There's a noticeable move towards smaller container vessels, with fewer very large containerships—which were historically more prone to adopt alternative fuels—being ordered. Simultaneously, DNV notes increased activity in the tanker and bulker segments, indicating a broadening, albeit cautious, adoption across different ship types. The industry is effectively trying to position fleets for future emissions regulations and uncertain fuel economics by maintaining flexibility.

## Implications for EU ETS and CII Performance

This shift towards a more cautious, diversified approach has direct consequences for the industry's efforts to curb emissions and manage regulatory compliance. For platforms like DirtiestShips.com, which track actual emissions data via EU MRV and analyze [CII ratings](/cii.html) and [EU ETS](https://dirtiestships.com/ets.html) costs, the slowdown in *new* alternative-fuel orders means a slower pace of emissions reduction for the global fleet in the coming years.

While LNG offers a reduction in CO2 emissions compared to traditional heavy fuel oil, it is still a fossil fuel and thus contributes to CO2 emissions and incurs EU ETS costs. The limited orders for zero-carbon fuels such as ammonia or hydrogen are particularly concerning for the long-term trajectory towards net-zero. Without a significant uptick in vessels capable of running on these truly green fuels, the industry will continue to accrue substantial carbon costs under the EU ETS, which applies to all voyages to and from EU ports.

Furthermore, the slowdown could impede the overall improvement of fleet-wide [CII ratings](/cii.html). Vessels capable of running on lower-emission fuels generally achieve better CII scores. A slower uptake of these newbuilds means that the average fleet's operational carbon intensity may not improve as rapidly, potentially leading to a higher proportion of vessels falling into 'D' or 'E' categories, requiring stricter corrective action plans. While liner companies have been leaders, a prolonged slowdown could affect their standing in [company rankings](/companies.html) if their peers accelerate.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The cautious approach to alternative-fuel investments, while understandable given market uncertainties, poses a clear challenge to the shipping industry's decarbonization ambitions. While existing investments indicate significant progress, the current slowdown in new orders for alternative-fuel vessels, particularly those capable of running on truly zero-carbon fuels, suggests that the pace of CO2 emissions reduction might be slower than anticipated. This will inevitably prolong the industry's exposure to EU ETS costs and necessitate more concerted efforts to improve operational efficiency for existing fleets to maintain compliant [CII ratings](/cii.html). The global fleet's journey towards net-zero remains an uphill climb, and the current hedging strategy risks delaying the peak of that ascent.
