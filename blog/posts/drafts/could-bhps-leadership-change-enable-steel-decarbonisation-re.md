The global shipping industry, already grappling with stringent decarbonization mandates like the EU ETS and IMO’s CII ratings, faces a complex interplay of forces. Among these, the decarbonization strategies of major industrial players are paramount, particularly those in heavy industries like steelmaking, which directly drive demand for shipping raw materials and finished products. New analysis from the Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA), highlighted by Hellenic Shipping News, suggests a potential turning point for BHP, a mining giant whose strategic choices profoundly influence global trade flows and, by extension, shipping's carbon footprint. The impending arrival of a new CEO at BHP on July 1st presents an opportunity for a critical reset, with significant implications for the maritime sector's journey towards cleaner operations.

## BHP's Enormous Scope 3 Footprint and Shipping Implications

BHP's vast operations, particularly in metallurgical (met) coal and iron ore, generate enormous Scope 3 emissions – those indirect emissions occurring in a company's value chain. According to the IEEFA briefing note, "BHP: Time to get real about steel technology change and met coal," these emissions are projected to reach a staggering 378 million tonnes (Mt) in FY2025. While these are not direct shipping emissions, they represent the carbon intensity of the materials that fill countless cargo holds annually.

BHP's current strategy, which IEEFA describes as "lagging" its peers, prioritizes technologies like carbon capture, utilisation and storage (CCUS) that would allow continued use of its existing met coal and blast furnace iron ore products. This emphasis means continued high demand for bulk carriers transporting these carbon-intensive commodities. For shipping companies, this translates to sustained cargo volumes but also reinforces a supply chain heavily reliant on fossil fuels, making the overall transition to low-carbon logistics more challenging and potentially increasing exposure to future regulatory risks and carbon costs under schemes like the EU ETS for voyages touching European ports. ## The Pitfalls of CCUS and Shifting Trade Dynamics

IEEFA's analysis sharply criticizes BHP's heavy reliance on CCUS, noting a "distinct lack of progress" for the technology in the steel sector. There are currently no commercial-scale CCUS plants for met coal-based steelmaking anywhere in the world. This mirrors broader issues with CCUS across sectors, including disappointing capture rates, high transportation costs, and doubts over storage site availability and security – issues that have led the International Energy Agency (IEA) to downgrade its projected role in emissions reduction.

Furthermore, BHP's long-term demand projections for met coal heavily depend on India. However, IEEFA suggests this outlook is overly optimistic. India's steel sector is actively seeking to diversify its energy security, prioritizing domestic coal and reducing reliance on imports, particularly from Australia. This shift has direct implications for dry bulk shipping. A reduction in Australian met coal exports to India would reshape major trade routes, potentially leading to altered fleet deployments, increased ballast legs, or shifts in regional freight rates for Capesize and Panamax vessels that typically carry these cargoes. This dynamic underscores the interconnectedness of commodity markets and the global shipping fleet, as tracked by our [emissions data](/charts.html).

## Direct Reduced Iron (DRI): A Promising Alternative for Shipping

The IEEFA analysis points to Direct Reduced Iron (DRI)-based steelmaking as the "most promising technology for steelmaking emissions reductions." DRI technology, already mature and used at commercial scale, reduces iron ore using gas or green hydrogen, effectively eliminating the need for carbon-intensive blast furnaces and their reliance on metallurgical coal.

A significant shift towards DRI, especially "green hydrogen" DRI, would profoundly alter the shipping landscape. While BHP is involved in developing solutions for using its lower-grade iron ore in DRI, its primary focus remains on CCUS. Should the industry pivot more decisively to DRI, demand for met coal would plummet, while demand for high-grade iron ore (and potentially green hydrogen or ammonia for its transport) would increase. This could lead to a re-evaluation of vessel designs, port infrastructure for new fuels, and trade patterns, directly influencing the emissions profiles of vessels and their [CII ratings](/cii.html). Shipping companies that adapt quickly to these evolving cargo requirements stand to gain a competitive advantage in a decarbonizing supply chain.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

BHP's strategic direction on steel decarbonization carries profound implications for global shipping emissions. A continued reliance on met coal and unproven CCUS technologies means sustained demand for carbon-intensive bulk shipping, increasing pressure on carriers to meet their own decarbonization targets under the EU ETS and IMO regulations. Conversely, a genuine shift towards more realistic pathways like green hydrogen-based DRI would fundamentally alter commodity trade, potentially reducing the overall carbon footprint of shipping by eliminating the need to transport vast quantities of metallurgical coal and changing iron ore demand. As the world pushes for decarbonization, the choices made by giants like BHP will ripple through every segment of the supply chain, directly impacting the types of ships we see, the routes they take, and ultimately, the industry’s ability to achieve net-zero.
