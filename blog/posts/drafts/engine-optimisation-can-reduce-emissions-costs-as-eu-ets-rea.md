The full phase-in of the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) in 2026 is significantly impacting shipowner operational strategies, with a marked increase in focus on engine optimization to mitigate rising emissions-related costs. As emissions costs become a core element of voyage economics, rather than just a compliance hurdle, the industry is turning to data-driven solutions and proactive maintenance.

## The Rising Cost of Inefficiency

The hellenicshippingnews.com article highlights a critical financial shift due to the full implementation of the EU ETS. As of January 1, 2026, vessels operating between EU ports must surrender EU Allowances (EUAs) covering 100% of their verified greenhouse gas emissions. This eliminates the phased approach introduced in 2024.

The article cites industry reports indicating that full ETS compliance adds roughly $320 per tonne of VLSFO (Very Low Sulphur Fuel Oil) consumed on intra-EU voyages. This figure is based on prevailing EUA prices and regulated fuel emission factors. While the actual cost fluctuates based on carbon market dynamics and exchange rates, the scale of the financial exposure demands operational improvements focused on fuel efficiency and combustion quality. Even marginal inefficiencies now translate directly into noticeable financial losses. This means a poor [CII rating](/cii.html) has a higher financial impact under the full ETS.

## Engine Optimization: A Key Mitigation Strategy

German engine diagnostics specialist CM Technologies GmbH (CMT) emphasizes the importance of engine optimization as a means to control emissions-related operating costs. According to CMT, incremental losses in combustion efficiency are frequently overlooked sources of avoidable emissions. Issues such as retarded ignition timing, uneven cylinder loading, and injector degradation can develop gradually without triggering immediate alarms, yet they steadily increase fuel consumption. Under the ETS framework, each additional tonne of fuel consumed translates directly into higher EUA requirements.

The article notes that shipowners and managers are increasingly using performance data, fuel monitoring, and combustion analysis to manage fuel consumption and, consequently, emissions exposure. This shift towards data-driven decision-making aligns with the broader industry trend of leveraging technology to enhance operational efficiency and comply with increasingly stringent environmental regulations.

## Guidance from Class Societies

Major classification societies, such as DNV and ClassNK, reinforce the importance of proactive engine management. Their recent guidance indicates that managing EU ETS exposure necessitates closer operational control of fuel consumption and engine performance, coupled with robust monitoring and verification of emissions data. This guidance is driving a more proactive approach to engine optimization, with operators actively seeking to identify and rectify even minor efficiency losses before they escalate into significant emissions costs.

CMT reports growing interest in onboard combustion analysis to assess ignition timing and cylinder balance under real operating conditions, allowing for immediate adjustments. Furthermore, there's increased attention to wear-related efficiency losses under low-sulphur fuel operation, as liner and piston-ring wear can elevate friction and fuel demand even before traditional maintenance thresholds are reached. This suggests a need for more frequent and detailed engine inspections, potentially increasing operational expenditures in the short term, but with the aim of reducing overall ETS costs. ## Expanding ETS Scope and Combustion Quality

The expansion of the EU ETS scope in 2026 to include methane and nitrous oxide (N2O) further emphasizes the significance of combustion quality, according to CMT. These gases possess significantly higher global warming potentials than CO2, meaning incomplete combustion events can result in a disproportionate increase in carbon-equivalent emissions. Addressing this requires more sophisticated monitoring and control of combustion processes. The [EMSA MRV data](/news.html) will likely reflect increased reporting of these gases as the regulations are enforced.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The full phase-in of the EU ETS is forcing shipping companies to prioritize engine optimization and fuel efficiency. While the price of EUAs remains beyond an operator’s direct control, the relationship between engine performance and emissions cost has become undeniable. Maintaining efficient combustion is now a crucial aspect of managing operational risk in the carbon market. This will likely drive innovation in engine technology, fuel management systems, and data analytics, ultimately contributing to a reduction in greenhouse gas emissions from the shipping industry, and influencing the [CII ratings](/cii.html) of many vessels.
