Global shipping, a significant contributor to greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, faces a critical juncture as international efforts to decarbonize the industry encounter geopolitical headwinds. The International Maritime Organization's (IMO) proposed Net-Zero Framework (NZF), designed to establish uniform global emissions rules, has been delayed, raising concerns about regulatory fragmentation and the pace of green fuel adoption. This delay underscores the urgent need for collaborative strategies, particularly between Europe and Africa, to drive the industry towards net-zero emissions.

## The IMO's Net-Zero Framework and its Stalled Progress

The IMO's NZF represents a significant step towards decarbonizing global shipping. It aims to reduce emissions by 20-30% by 2030 and 70-80% by 2040 (from 2008 levels). The framework, applying to ships of 5,000 gross tonnage and above engaged in international trade, combines binding emissions targets with a pricing mechanism for non-compliance, introducing a global fuel standard that requires ships to reduce their annual GHG fuel intensity (GFI). The ECFR article highlights that despite initial momentum, the adoption of the NZF was derailed due to opposition from influential countries like the US and Saudi Arabia, leading to a postponement of the vote until December 2026. This delay jeopardizes the prospect of a unified global approach to emissions reduction.

## The Threat of Regulatory Fragmentation

The absence of a global framework like the NZF raises the specter of a fragmented regulatory landscape. Without a standardized approach, regional decarbonization schemes are likely to proliferate, increasing compliance costs for ship operators and potentially encouraging regulatory arbitrage. This could undermine the effectiveness of emissions reduction efforts and slow the development and adoption of green fuels.

This fragmentation can also impact the effectiveness of existing regional regulations, such as the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), which now includes shipping. Without a global framework, ships could theoretically divert routes to avoid ETS costs, potentially undermining the system's impact. The EU ETS currently adds a carbon cost to voyages within the EU and 50% of the emissions on voyages into or out of the EU. ## The Role of Green Ammonia and Green Methanol

The ECFR article emphasizes the potential of green hydrogen-based fuels, particularly green ammonia and green methanol, as viable alternatives to conventional fossil fuels. These fuels can be produced using renewable energy sources, significantly reducing the carbon footprint of shipping. However, the transition to these fuels requires substantial investment in production infrastructure and vessel retrofitting. The NZF could act as a market catalyst, providing predictable demand that will help scale production and reduce costs over time.

The availability and cost of these alternative fuels will directly impact [CII ratings](/cii.html). Vessels using low-carbon fuels will achieve higher ratings, incentivizing their adoption.

## Africa's Pivotal Role and the Need for Collaboration

The ECFR article underscores the importance of securing the support of African countries for the NZF. Africa stands to benefit from the development of new green-fuel industries, and European collaboration can play a crucial role in ensuring that African maritime sectors are not left behind in the decarbonization process. This collaboration could involve joint development of green fuels, technology transfer, and capacity building initiatives. A strong partnership between Europe and Africa could sway the vote at the IMO and pave the way for the adoption of the NZF.

What this means for shipping emissions:

The delay in adopting the IMO's Net-Zero Framework poses a significant threat to the decarbonization of global shipping. The absence of a unified global approach could lead to regulatory fragmentation and hinder the development and adoption of green fuels. Collaboration between Europe and Africa is crucial to revive the NZF and drive the industry towards a sustainable future. The pressure is on for countries to adopt global regulations that lower emissions in their shipping industries.
