The U.S. Merchant Marine, once a global leader, now represents a fraction of global shipping capacity, a situation creating strategic vulnerabilities that could impact maritime emissions regulations worldwide. While the U.S. currently lacks a significant presence in commercial shipbuilding and operation, any effort to revitalize its merchant fleet could have unforeseen consequences for the global shipping industry's push to decarbonize, especially as environmental regulations tighten via initiatives like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## A Shrinking Fleet: Numbers Don't Lie

According to a recent report, the U.S. Merchant Marine ranks as the 22nd largest globally (Mercogliano, 2025). In January 2025, only 188 U.S.-flagged ships exceeding 1,000 gross tons were active, representing a mere 0.55% of the world's fleet by deadweight tonnage (Mercogliano, 2025). This decline contrasts sharply with nations like China, which accounts for over half of global shipbuilding (Mercogliano, 2025). This information aligns with data on our [emissions data](/charts.html) page showing the U.S. as a minor player in overall fleet emissions compared to Asian and European flagged vessels.

## Policy Shifts and Potential Impacts

In April 2025, Executive Order 14269, "Restoring America's Maritime Dominance," signaled a policy shift aimed at revitalizing the U.S. maritime sector (Mercogliano, 2025). The stated goal is to rebuild domestic maritime industries and enhance national security. While the executive order doesn't explicitly address environmental concerns, any significant expansion of the U.S. fleet would inevitably impact global shipping emissions.

The reconciliation budget allocated limited resources toward maritime industrial workforce development programs ($450 million) and the lease/purchase of new ships ($600 million), (Mercogliano, 2025). The key point here is whether these investments will prioritize environmentally friendly technologies. Without such a focus, an expanded U.S. fleet could increase overall emissions, potentially impacting the [CII ratings](/cii.html) landscape and the financial burden of carbon costs under the EU ETS.

## Lessons from History: Echoes of Post-War Policy

The article highlights how post-World War II policies, such as the Ships Sale Act of 1946 and the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade (GATT), inadvertently weakened the U.S. maritime sector (Mercogliano, 2025). As the U.S. looks to rebuild, it must avoid repeating past mistakes by prioritizing sustainable practices and investing in low-carbon technologies. Failing to do so could result in a fleet that is not only economically uncompetitive but also environmentally unsustainable.

## The Bigger Picture: Strategic Vulnerability or Opportunity?

The article suggests that the decline of the U.S. Merchant Marine creates a strategic vulnerability, leaving the U.S. exposed to economic coercion (Mercogliano, 2025). While national security is a valid concern, it shouldn't come at the expense of environmental responsibility. The U.S. has an opportunity to leverage its technological prowess and environmental regulations to become a leader in green shipping, driving innovation and setting a positive example for other nations. A revitalized U.S. fleet equipped with the latest in emissions-reducing technologies could significantly contribute to global decarbonization efforts. Currently, our [company rankings](/companies.html) do not feature many U.S.-based shipping firms, suggesting a missed opportunity for leadership in sustainable maritime practices.

## What this means for shipping emissions

A resurgence of the U.S. Merchant Marine presents both risks and opportunities for global shipping emissions. Without a clear focus on sustainability, expansion could exacerbate existing environmental challenges. However, with strategic investments in green technologies and a commitment to international regulations like the EU ETS, the U.S. can transform its maritime sector into a model of environmental responsibility, driving innovation and accelerating the industry's transition to a low-carbon future. The time to act is now, ensuring that any revitalization effort aligns with global decarbonization goals.
