The global shipping industry, responsible for a significant portion of worldwide CO2 emissions, faces increasing pressure to decarbonize. Recent meetings at the International Maritime Organization (IMO) signal a renewed push towards establishing a consensus on mid-term measures to address greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from ships, offering a glimmer of hope amidst ongoing debates and complexities. This comes as the industry also contends with heightened environmental risks in critical waterways.

## IMO Restarting Net-Zero Framework Discussions

The 84th session of the IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC 84), held from April 27 to May 1, 2026, concluded with a commitment to revitalize discussions on the IMO Net-Zero Framework. This framework encompasses "mid-term measures" intended to substantially reduce GHG emissions from ships. IMO Secretary-General Arsenio Dominguez emphasized the need to rebuild trust and maintain momentum in these crucial negotiations. The article from shipmanagementinternational.com highlights the agreement to establish an intersessional Working Group tasked with resolving concerns and fostering broader agreement on a global measure before MEPC 85. This suggests a potential shift towards more concrete action, though the path forward remains uncertain.

## Addressing the "Chain of Custody"

A key component of any effective emissions reduction strategy is the ability to accurately track and verify emissions throughout the fuel supply chain. The MEPC 84 meeting included plans for a one-day expert workshop focused on "chain of custody" models. This is critical because it ensures that emissions are properly traced and attributed, avoiding loopholes and promoting transparency. This concept aligns with the EU's approach to emissions accounting and is relevant to the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), which now includes shipping. Without robust chain of custody measures, it will be difficult to accurately assess the impact of different fuels and technologies on overall emissions, as well as fairly allocate [CII ratings](/cii.html) to individual vessels.

## Environmental Risks in the Strait of Hormuz

Beyond emissions regulations, the MEPC also addressed immediate environmental risks. The Committee adopted a resolution condemning attacks on commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz and the potential for large-scale marine pollution. The resolution acknowledged the vulnerability of the Persian Gulf and surrounding waters and warned of the dangers posed by oil spills, hazardous substances, and residues resulting from attacks. This underscores the broader environmental challenges facing the shipping industry, extending beyond GHG emissions to include the risk of accidental or intentional pollution incidents.

## The Road to MEPC 85

The next few months are critical for shaping the future of global shipping emissions regulations. Two inter-sessional meetings are scheduled for September and November 2026, preceding MEPC 85 in late November/early December. During this time, Member States can submit amendments and adjustments to previously approved draft amendments. A second extraordinary session of MEPC, originally adjourned in October, is scheduled to resume on December 4, pending discussions at MEPC 85. These meetings provide opportunities to refine the proposed measures and build consensus among the IMO's diverse membership. However, the complexity of these negotiations and the differing priorities of various nations pose significant challenges to reaching a comprehensive and effective agreement. It remains to be seen if this flurry of diplomatic activity can resolve fundamental disagreements.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The renewed focus on the IMO Net-Zero Framework is a positive step, but the success of this initiative hinges on the ability of Member States to bridge their differences and agree on concrete, enforceable measures. The emphasis on "chain of custody" models is crucial for ensuring the integrity of any emissions reduction scheme. The upcoming inter-sessional meetings and MEPC 85 will be pivotal in determining whether the shipping industry can make meaningful progress towards decarbonization, or if further delays and compromises will continue to undermine efforts to address its environmental impact. The outcome of these negotiations will have a direct impact on the cost of carbon for ships trading in the EU under the EU ETS, potentially affecting [company rankings](/companies.html) and ultimately shaping the future of the industry. The data gathered by the [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) program will continue to be essential to evaluate the effectiveness of whatever measures are adopted.
