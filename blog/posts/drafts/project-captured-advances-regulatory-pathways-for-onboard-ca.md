The global shipping industry has just received a significant signal that onboard carbon capture and storage (OCCS) could become a viable and recognized pathway to decarbonization. Project CAPTURED, a pioneering initiative, has achieved dual regulatory milestones: formal recognition for CO2 captured onboard under the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) and in-principle support from the International Maritime Organization (IMO) for carbon mineralisation as permanent CO2 storage. These breakthroughs, following the world’s first demonstration of an end-to-end value chain for onboard captured and liquefied CO2 (LCO2), dramatically strengthen the commercial case for OCCS and could reshape compliance strategies for fleets operating under stringent emissions regulations.

## A New Pathway for EU ETS Compliance

The most immediate and impactful development for shipowners facing EU ETS obligations is the formal recognition of onboard captured CO2 for compliance. As detailed by hellenicshippingnews.com, this means that the verified tonnage of CO2 captured on a vessel can be directly deducted from the emissions requiring the surrender of EU Allowances (EUAs). For companies operating within the EU ETS, this represents a tangible financial incentive to invest in OCCS technology. Each tonne of captured CO2 that qualifies through permanent chemical binding, such as carbon mineralisation, translates directly into a reduction in EUA purchases, potentially saving millions for large fleets as carbon prices fluctuate. This recognition introduces a critical new dimension to emissions reduction strategies, moving beyond fuel switching and operational efficiencies to direct capture. Vessels leveraging this could significantly alter their emissions profiles as tracked in [emissions data](/charts.html).

## IMO's Nod to Permanent Carbon Storage

Beyond the EU ETS, Project CAPTURED’s data and learnings also formed the basis of a proposal to MEPC 84, which garnered in-principle support from the International Maritime Organization (IMO) for recognizing carbon mineralisation as a form of permanent CO2 storage. This global endorsement is crucial for the long-term viability and widespread adoption of OCCS. IMO recognition provides a clear signal that this technology, when linked to validated permanent storage methods like mineralisation, aligns with international decarbonization goals. While the EU ETS provides the immediate economic driver, IMO support lays the groundwork for future global regulations that could incorporate OCCS as a legitimate emissions reduction measure, paving the way for broader investment and technological development across the industry.

## Impact on CII Ratings and Company Performance

The ability to capture and permanently store CO2 onboard has direct implications for a vessel’s Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) rating. CII, which measures a ship's operational carbon efficiency, is heavily influenced by actual CO2 emissions. By reducing the net emissions output, OCCS technology can significantly improve a vessel's [CII ratings](/cii.html), helping ships avoid poor 'D' or 'E' ratings and the subsequent need for corrective action plans. For companies, widespread adoption of OCCS could transform their standing in [company rankings](/companies.html) based on emissions performance. This isn't just about regulatory compliance; it's about competitive advantage, attracting more environmentally conscious charterers, and demonstrating a proactive commitment to decarbonization. While the initial investment in OCCS can be substantial, the long-term benefits in compliance, operational flexibility, and corporate reputation are increasingly compelling.

## The Data-Driven Future of Onboard Carbon Capture

The success of Project CAPTURED underscores the critical role of data verification in scaling onboard carbon capture. The EU ETS recognition is explicitly tied to "verified tonnage" of captured CO2. This aligns perfectly with the principles established by the EU's Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system, which forms the backbone of emissions transparency. For OCCS to gain traction, robust systems for measuring, reporting, and verifying captured CO2 will be paramount. While the news article from hellenicshippingnews.com is thin on specific company or vessel details from the pilot, its achievement signals a proof-of-concept for the entire value chain—from capture to offloading and permanent storage. The challenge now shifts to developing and deploying these technologies at scale across diverse ship types, ensuring that the entire lifecycle of captured CO2 is meticulously tracked and accounted for to guarantee environmental integrity.

### What this means for shipping emissions

The regulatory advancements achieved by Project CAPTURED mark a pivotal moment for shipping's decarbonization journey. By officially recognizing onboard carbon capture as a valid pathway for EU ETS compliance and gaining IMO's in-principle support for permanent storage, a significant barrier to OCCS adoption has been removed. This breakthrough offers shipowners a powerful new tool to mitigate emissions, manage EU ETS costs, and improve their CII ratings, providing a much-needed alternative to conventional decarbonization strategies. While scaling and infrastructure development remain crucial next steps, the era of verified, onboard CO2 capture and permanent storage has officially begun, promising to accelerate the industry's transition towards net-zero emissions.
