The future of carbon pricing for the international shipping sector remains uncertain but alive, following discussions at the International Maritime Organization's (IMO) Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC84) this week. While a majority of nations voiced support for the Net-Zero Framework (NZF) – a proposal that includes carbon pricing – significant opposition persists, particularly from oil-producing nations and the US. The outcome of these negotiations will have profound implications for shipping emissions, the cost of maritime transport, and the industry's ability to meet its decarbonization goals.

## The Battle Lines: Support vs. Opposition

The MEPC84 meeting highlighted a clear division among member states regarding the NZF. According to [hellenicshippingnews.com](http://hellenicshippingnews.com), 55 countries backed the NZF, emphasizing the necessity of its carbon pricing and funding mechanisms. This group included the EU, the UK, Brazil, and several island nations particularly vulnerable to climate change. Conversely, 51 countries favored reopening the framework for substantial alterations, including the removal of carbon pricing and the weakening of carbon intensity limits on fuels. This opposition bloc featured the US, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and other nations with vested interests in fossil fuel production. These disagreements reflect the complex geopolitical landscape surrounding climate policy, with economic interests often clashing with environmental imperatives.

## The Net-Zero Framework: A Contested Path Forward

The NZF, a proposed landmark climate regulation, aims to introduce the world’s first carbon pricing mechanism for international shipping. If adopted, it would require ships to pay a price for their carbon emissions, incentivizing the adoption of cleaner fuels and more efficient technologies. The framework also includes limits on the carbon intensity of fuels, pushing the industry away from fossil fuels. The article states that the NZF will be the basis for further discussions this autumn, but it will be considered alongside other proposals that seek to weaken or eliminate carbon pricing altogether. These alternative proposals include a suggestion from Japan to remove carbon pricing and from Panama/Liberia to weaken carbon intensity limits. The stakes are high, as the final framework will significantly shape the future of shipping emissions and the pace of decarbonization.

## Carbon Pricing and the EU ETS: A Regional Precedent

While the IMO debates a global carbon price, the European Union has already implemented its own Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) for shipping. Since January 2024, ships calling at EU ports have been required to purchase allowances for their CO2 emissions, creating a carbon cost for these voyages. This regional approach provides a practical example of how carbon pricing can work in the maritime sector, though its effectiveness depends on robust enforcement and a sufficiently high carbon price. ## The Urgency of Decarbonization: Fuel Costs and Climate Targets

The pressure to decarbonize the shipping industry is mounting, driven by both environmental concerns and economic realities. The [hellenicshippingnews.com](http://hellenicshippingnews.com) article highlights that bunker fuel prices have doubled due to the war in the Middle East, adding urgency to efforts to improve efficiency and transition to alternative fuels. The IMO has set targets to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from shipping by at least 50% by 2050, compared to 2008 levels, but many experts believe that more ambitious action is needed to align with the Paris Agreement goals. Without strong policies like carbon pricing, the industry risks falling short of its decarbonization targets. ## What this means for shipping emissions

The IMO's negotiations on a global carbon price for shipping are at a critical juncture. The outcome will determine the pace and scale of decarbonization efforts in the maritime sector. While the survival of the Net-Zero Framework is a positive sign, significant challenges remain. The industry needs a clear regulatory signal to incentivize investments in cleaner fuels and technologies. A weak or watered-down framework risks prolonging the reliance on fossil fuels and undermining the industry's ability to meet its climate targets. As discussions continue this autumn, it's crucial that member states prioritize ambitious climate action and resist efforts to weaken or delay the implementation of effective carbon pricing mechanisms.
