The Strait of Hormuz, a critical artery for global oil and gas shipments, is undergoing a significant shift with potentially far-reaching consequences for shipping emissions. Iran is formalizing a transit-toll regime, creating a "bifurcated Strait" with implications for vessel compliance and fleet composition that could influence the industry's carbon footprint. This development raises concerns about increased costs, operational inefficiencies, and the potential for a surge in "dark fleet" activity, all of which impact the maritime sector's ability to meet decarbonization targets and comply with regulations like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS).

## Iran Implements Hormuz Transit Toll

According to hellenicshippingnews.com, Iran is establishing the Persian Gulf Strait Authority (PGSA) to administer a transit-toll regime. Vessels will be required to apply and submit detailed information, including ownership, cargo, and routing, before receiving a transit permit. While no official tariff has been published, reports suggest per-transit payments of up to $2 million, potentially payable in Chinese yuan or even Bitcoin to IRGC-linked wallets. This move marks a strategic shift from kinetic disruption to administrative control, allowing Iran to systematically screen and track vessels. This new toll could significantly impact operational costs, especially for smaller operators and those carrying lower-value cargoes.

## Impact on Vessel Compliance and Fleet Composition

The introduction of transit tolls is likely to accelerate the "bifurcation" of Hormuz traffic. As the article notes, "dark and gray fleets and BRICS-aligned tonnage are positioned to absorb the premiums, while Western-aligned tonnage remains effectively frozen out or dependent on naval escort." Vessels willing to operate outside traditional regulatory frameworks, often referred to as "dark fleets," may be more inclined to pay the tolls to avoid scrutiny and maintain operational flexibility.

This shift could have a negative impact on shipping emissions. Dark fleets, which often disable Automatic Identification Systems (AIS) and operate with less oversight, are less likely to invest in fuel-efficient technologies or adhere to emission reduction standards. Increased activity by these fleets could undermine efforts to improve the industry's [CII ratings](/cii.html) and reduce its overall carbon footprint. The lack of transparency associated with dark fleet operations also makes it difficult to accurately track their emissions and hold them accountable for non-compliance with environmental regulations.

## Potential Increase in "Dark Fleet" Activity and Evasion

The increased cost of Hormuz transits, coupled with the potential for sanctions-related complications, could incentivize more shipowners to engage in deceptive practices. This includes manipulating AIS data, falsifying cargo declarations, and employing complex ownership structures to obscure their activities. Such practices make it difficult to track and regulate emissions, potentially leading to an increase in unreported greenhouse gas emissions. For example, vessels may intentionally slow-steam outside the Strait to reduce fuel consumption (and therefore emissions), but then speed up within the Strait to minimize time spent in the tolled area. This kind of erratic operation is far from optimal from an efficiency perspective.

## EU ETS and Carbon Costs

The situation in the Strait of Hormuz adds another layer of complexity to the already challenging task of complying with the EU ETS. Companies whose vessels call at EU ports are now required to purchase carbon credits for a portion of their emissions, and the cost of these credits can be substantial. The new transit tolls could further strain the finances of companies already struggling to comply with the ETS, potentially leading them to seek ways to evade the regulations. The potential acceptance of cryptocurrency payments to IRGC-linked wallets complicates the picture and makes transparency harder to achieve. This creates difficulties when calculating a company's carbon footprint for [company rankings](/companies.html) based on EMSA MRV data and other sources.

## What this means for shipping emissions

Iran's move to formalize a transit-toll regime in the Strait of Hormuz poses a significant challenge to the global effort to reduce shipping emissions. The potential for increased dark fleet activity, evasion of regulations, and operational inefficiencies could undermine efforts to improve the industry's environmental performance and achieve its decarbonization targets. It also adds additional complexity to the operational risk assessment for companies, which is now intertwined with geopolitics and sanctions. This could affect insurance premiums, financing costs, and overall competitiveness, pushing the adoption of alternative routes and creating an uneven playing field, with potentially dire consequences for the industry's ability to meet its climate commitments.
