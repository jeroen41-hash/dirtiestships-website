The Global Biofuels Alliance (GBA) is pushing for the rapid deployment of biofuels in both the aviation and marine sectors, a move that could significantly impact shipping emissions and the industry's compliance with increasingly stringent regulations. With the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) now including maritime transport and [CII ratings](/cii.html) driving operational efficiency, the pressure to decarbonize is mounting. The GBA's focus on Sustainable Aviation Fuel (SAF) corridors and methanol as a marine fuel offers potential pathways, but faces challenges in terms of standardization, commercial viability, and technological readiness.

## SAF Corridors: Harmonizing Policy for Aviation

The GBA is actively pursuing the establishment of SAF corridors connecting major global airports. According to the source article, GBA Director Joshua Wycliffe stated that negotiations are underway with governments in the Netherlands and Singapore to create corridors between airports such as Amsterdam-Delhi, Amsterdam-Mumbai, Singapore-Mumbai, and Singapore-Delhi. The initiative aims to address policy fragmentation in the SAF market by creating standardized frameworks for SAF volumes, blending standards, and certification requirements. This standardization is crucial, as highlighted by Wycliffe, to incentivize investment in SAF technology and infrastructure. Without binding national policy mandates, the economic incentive to adopt SAF remains weak. The corridor model seeks to create a replicable template that can be expanded to other routes, potentially positioning India as a key "global bunkering geography" given its refining capacity and strategic location.

## Methanol's Near-Term Potential for Marine

While SAF faces hurdles, the GBA sees methanol as a more commercially viable near-term solution for the marine sector. Technical Director Dr. Santanu Gupta emphasized that commercial methanol marine engines have been developed and are moving into production, with over 200 ships potentially adopting methanol propulsion. This aligns with growing industry interest and pilot projects exploring methanol as a fuel to meet emissions reduction targets. Gupta highlighted the CO2-to-methanol pathway as particularly promising, especially given the existence of carbon pricing mechanisms, such as the EU ETS, which can help absorb the cost differential. This is especially important for the shipping industry as the EU ETS is now applied to international shipping, including voyages calling at EU ports. Ships that receive a poor [CII rating](/cii.html) may face commercial pressure and higher costs, incentivizing the adoption of alternative fuels like methanol. The article doesn't offer specifics on the number of ships ordered or under construction that are capable of running on methanol, however.

## 2G Ethanol and the Alcohol-to-Jet Pathway

The article provides insights into India's second-generation (2G) ethanol program, noting that all Indian oil marketing companies have built 2G ethanol plants. However, the technology faces challenges related to the high silica content of Indian paddy straw, which affects the pre-treatment process. Despite these challenges, the GBA is positioning 2G ethanol as a SAF feedstock via the alcohol-to-jet pathway. Gupta noted that while 2G ethanol is currently not competitive with first-generation ethanol for road transport blending, its potential as a SAF feedstock, combined with the co-production of renewable diesel, could make it economically viable, assuming a price of 2.5 times the jet fuel rate can be achieved for SAF.

## Carbon Pricing and Cost Absorption

Both methanol and SAF pathways face economic hurdles. The GBA highlights the importance of carbon pricing mechanisms in absorbing the cost differential between biofuels and traditional fossil fuels. The EU ETS, now extended to maritime, puts a price on CO2 emissions from ships calling at EU ports. This carbon cost can potentially make biofuels more competitive, especially for ships operating within the EU. Ships subject to the EU ETS are required to surrender allowances for their emissions, adding a significant cost. The higher the carbon price, the greater the incentive to switch to lower-emission fuels like biofuels. However, the article lacks detailed analysis of the specific impact of current carbon prices on the economic viability of methanol and SAF in the marine sector. It also doesn't address the operational challenges of biofuels, such as availability, storage, and handling.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The Global Biofuels Alliance's initiatives to promote SAF corridors and methanol adoption in the marine sector represent a potential pathway for shipping to reduce its CO2 emissions. While SAF adoption faces policy and technological hurdles, methanol offers a more immediate alternative, especially in light of the EU ETS. The success of these initiatives will depend on factors such as the harmonization of policies, the development of cost-effective technologies, and the effective implementation of carbon pricing mechanisms. Ultimately, the transition to biofuels in shipping requires a coordinated effort involving governments, industry stakeholders, and technology providers. The impact on overall fleet emissions tracked by DirtiestShips.com will depend on the speed and scale of biofuel adoption, impacting vessel [CII ratings](/cii.html) and potentially influencing [company rankings](/companies.html).
