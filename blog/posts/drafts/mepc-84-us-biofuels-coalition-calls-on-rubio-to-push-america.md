The push for biofuels in shipping, fueled by both environmental concerns and economic incentives, is heating up, particularly in the US. A coalition of biofuel producers is actively lobbying for the inclusion of American-made biofuels in international maritime regulations, directly challenging the EU's approach to decarbonization. This move, detailed in a recent *shipandbunker.com* article, has the potential to significantly impact the uptake of alternative fuels and reshape the [CII ratings](/cii.html) landscape for vessels trading globally.

## Biofuel Industry Seeks Level Playing Field at IMO

The American Biofuels Maritime Initiative (ABMI), representing a consortium of US biofuel and farm groups, is urging Secretary of State Marco Rubio to advocate for American biofuels at the International Maritime Organization's (IMO) MEPC 84 session. Their primary goal is to establish a "technology-neutral" framework that recognizes biofuels as a viable option for decarbonizing the maritime sector. This push directly opposes the EU's FuelEU Maritime regulation, which ABMI claims "effectively prohibits the use of American-made biofuels" (*shipandbunker.com*). The coalition also advocates phasing out "harmful" regional rules, clearly referencing the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS).

## Challenging EU Regulations: FuelEU and ETS

The criticism of FuelEU Maritime centers on its potential to create a "patchwork system" that increases costs and reduces predictability for international shipping. This regulation sets targets for reducing the greenhouse gas intensity of energy used on board ships calling at EU ports. ABMI argues that FuelEU Maritime favors certain fuel types over others, disadvantaging American biofuels. Similarly, the US delegation has previously called for a mandatory phaseout of the [EU ETS](/) for shipping. The EU ETS imposes a carbon price on emissions from ships operating within the EU, and critics argue that it places an unfair burden on the shipping industry. We track these [carbon costs](/) closely, and any policy shift would have knock-on effects for shipowners.

## Quantifying the Potential Biofuel Market

Currently, biofuels account for a mere 0.3% of global maritime energy. ABMI, citing Renewable Fuels Association (RFA) analysis, believes that biofuels could supply 5% of the global maritime fuel market. This translates to a significant 4–5 billion gallons of new demand, which could, in turn, boost demand for American farm products. The *shipandbunker.com* article notes Vanguard Renewables estimates that LNG could supply 50% of the marine fuel mix by 2050, with bio-LNG contributing a further 13%. However, the article lacks a specific timeframe for achieving the 5% biofuel target, making it difficult to assess its feasibility. This highlights the need for more concrete data and analysis to understand the potential impact of biofuels on shipping emissions. Our platform will continue to monitor [emissions data](/charts.html) to track the actual uptake of biofuels in the sector.

## Economic Drivers and Oversupply Concerns

The ABMI's lobbying efforts are also driven by economic factors. The letter to Secretary Rubio highlights the struggles of US corn farmers, who are facing a fourth consecutive year of operating losses. The coalition sees the maritime sector as a potential "relief valve" for domestic oversupply, with the EPA determining that the renewable CNG/LNG market is currently demand-constrained rather than production-constrained (*shipandbunker.com*). This emphasizes the complex interplay between environmental regulations, agricultural policy, and the shipping industry.

## Implications for Company Emissions Rankings

The increased use of biofuels could significantly impact the [company rankings](/companies.html) on DirtiestShips.com. If a company utilizes biofuels, their CO2 emissions would likely decrease, potentially improving their ranking. However, the lifecycle emissions of biofuels are a subject of ongoing debate, and it is crucial to consider the sustainability of biofuel production when assessing their environmental benefits. Furthermore, the [CII ratings](/cii.html) of ships using biofuels could also improve, as these ratings are based on a ship's annual operational carbon intensity. However, variations in biofuel sourcing and quality, not captured by simple emissions data, could skew results.

What this means for shipping emissions: The push for biofuels represents a significant challenge to the EU's regulatory approach and a potential shift in the landscape of alternative fuels for shipping. If the US biofuel industry succeeds in gaining broader acceptance for its products, it could lead to a more diverse and potentially less costly pathway to decarbonization. However, the environmental benefits of biofuels need to be carefully assessed, and the focus should remain on achieving genuine emissions reductions across the entire lifecycle of fuels used in the maritime sector.
