## Navigating the EU ETS and FuelEU Maritime: A Refresher

The core takeaway from ClassNK's announcement, as reported by *The Maritime Executive*, is the release of updated guidance on two critical EU regulations: the EU ETS for shipping and FuelEU Maritime. Both regulations apply to ships above 5,000 gross tonnage calling at EU ports. The EU ETS, which went into effect January 1, 2024, mandates that shipowners purchase and surrender emission allowances (EUAs) for a portion of their emissions generated on voyages to, from, and within the EU. FuelEU Maritime, active since January 1, 2025, focuses on the fuel itself, setting targets for the gradual reduction of greenhouse gas (GHG) intensity of energy used onboard ships. These regulations represent a significant shift in how shipping operates within the EU, creating both challenges and opportunities for decarbonization.

## Methane Slip Considerations: An Important Detail

The ClassNK FAQs address a particularly crucial aspect of LNG-fueled vessels: methane slip. Methane slip refers to the uncombusted methane that escapes from LNG engines. Methane is a far more potent greenhouse gas than carbon dioxide, albeit with a shorter lifespan in the atmosphere. The FAQs explain the procedures for using measured methane slip emission factors in place of default values for ships using LNG as fuel. This is significant because default values may overestimate the actual emissions from certain LNG engines, potentially penalizing operators who have invested in more efficient technologies. The European Commission (EC) issued guidelines on the reporting and verification of actual Tank-to-Wake methane slip emission factors in October 2025, a detail also referenced in the ClassNK update. Getting the data right is critical for accurate ETS compliance.

## The Broader Regulatory Context and Data Transparency

While the article focuses on ClassNK's guidance, it's important to place this within the broader context of EU maritime regulations and data transparency. The EU's Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) regulation, overseen by EMSA, forms the foundation for emissions reporting under the EU ETS. Our site uses this EMSA MRV data to track CO2 emissions from the global shipping industry, enabling us to provide [company rankings](/companies.html) based on their environmental performance. The [CII ratings](/cii.html) system, while separate from the EU ETS, further incentivizes emissions reductions by assigning ships a rating from A to E based on their operational carbon intensity. The EU ETS adds a direct financial incentive to improve efficiency and reduce fuel consumption. Understanding these interconnected regulations and leveraging accurate data is key for shipowners navigating the decarbonization landscape.

## EU ETS Costs and Their Impact

The cost of EU Allowances (EUAs) is a significant factor impacting shipping companies' bottom lines. These costs are directly linked to the amount of emissions a company is responsible for. As we analyze on [emissions data](/charts.html), vessel types and routes can greatly affect costs. Container ships on long-haul voyages, for example, will incur greater EUA expenses than smaller vessels operating solely within the EU. With the price of EUAs subject to market fluctuations, shipowners face financial uncertainty and must consider strategies for hedging their exposure. The pressure is on to invest in technologies and operational practices that reduce emissions and, consequently, lower EUA costs.
