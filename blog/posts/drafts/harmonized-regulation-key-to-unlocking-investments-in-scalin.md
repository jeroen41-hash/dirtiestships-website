Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage (OCCS) is gaining traction as a potential decarbonization pathway for shipping, but a new report highlights the critical need for harmonized regulation to unlock investment and scale the technology. Commissioned by major shipping lines CMA CGM, Evergreen, Hapag-Lloyd, Maersk, and MSC, the DNV report underscores the importance of a clear legislative framework to support the development of OCCS and the necessary infrastructure. This comes as the industry grapples with the IMO's delayed adoption of net-zero targets and increasing pressure from regional regulations like the EU ETS.

## OCCS: A Promising Solution Hampered by Regulatory Uncertainty

The DNV report, initiated with support from the Port of Rotterdam Authority, focuses on three key areas: the physical value chain for liquid CO₂ (LCO₂), CO₂ abatement potential and economic opportunities, and the regulatory framework. The concept involves capturing CO₂ onboard vessels, liquefying it, and transferring it to LCO₂ carriers for transport to storage or utilization sites. This approach aims to address the challenge of decarbonizing existing vessels and potentially offer a pathway for ships using conventional fuels to comply with increasingly stringent emissions regulations. The report's emphasis on regulatory clarity highlights a significant hurdle to wider adoption. As we track in our [news feed](/news.html), the shipping industry is facing a patchwork of regulations globally.

## Asia-North Europe Trade Lane as a Testing Ground

The five shipping companies propose using the Asia-North Europe trade lane as a pilot environment for an OCCS corridor, according to the article. This route is attractive due to its high emissions, predictable schedules, and the presence of CO₂ storage projects in Rotterdam and Singapore. This mirrors trends we see in the industry, as major carriers seek to leverage operational efficiencies on key routes to pilot and deploy new technologies. However, the report identifies the regulatory landscape as the primary bottleneck. While the IMO is developing guidelines for OCCS, EU regulations are incomplete, and legal ambiguities surround cross-border CO₂ transport and its classification as either waste or a raw material. These uncertainties affect not only OCCS projects but also the [CII ratings](/cii.html) of vessels operating on these routes if CO2 capture can be included as a performance gain.

## Evergreen's Pilot Project in Rotterdam: A Step Forward

The article cites a successful pilot project involving Evergreen's 14,000 TEU container ship, *Ever Top*, equipped with an SMDERI CO₂ capture installation. The vessel unloaded 20 tons of liquid CO₂ in Rotterdam, following a similar delivery in Shanghai. This project demonstrates the technical feasibility of OCCS, but the report emphasizes the need for scalability across the entire value chain, including LCO₂ transport vessels, storage terminals, and utilization pathways. Companies like Value Maritime, Wärtsila, and SMDERI are actively involved in developing and deploying capture systems, indicating growing industry interest. The question is how quickly and cost-effectively these solutions can scale to make a dent in overall emissions. ## Regulatory Gaps and the Need for Harmonization

The DNV report underscores the lack of a comprehensive regulatory framework as a major impediment to OCCS adoption. IMO guidelines are still under development, and EU regulations do not yet provide a complete framework. Legal uncertainties surrounding cross-border CO₂ transport and the classification of CO₂ further complicate matters. Investments in capture installations, transport capacity, and permanent storage facilities are contingent upon supportive regulations being introduced in a timely manner. These points highlight the critical need for international cooperation and harmonization to create a level playing field and encourage investment in OCCS. The Port of Rotterdam Authority's efforts to develop CO₂ storage and reuse chains within its industrial complex are a positive step, but a broader, more coordinated approach is required.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The shipping industry's exploration of OCCS highlights its growing commitment to decarbonization, but the technology's widespread adoption hinges on addressing regulatory uncertainties. Without a clear and harmonized legal framework, investments in OCCS infrastructure and technology will be hesitant, hindering the industry's ability to meet increasingly stringent emissions targets. The pilot project on the Asia-North Europe trade lane offers a valuable opportunity to test and refine OCCS technology, but its success depends on resolving the regulatory challenges and fostering collaboration across the entire value chain.
