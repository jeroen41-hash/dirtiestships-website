Geopolitical instability is shining a spotlight on the fragility of the global energy system, potentially accelerating the shift towards alternative fuels and reshaping the shipping industry's role in energy transportation. The concentration of oil and gas production in specific regions, particularly the Middle East, creates significant risks for global energy security, as highlighted by recent events in the region (splash247.com). This fragility is prompting a re-evaluation of the energy transition, framing it not only as a climate imperative but also as a crucial trade resilience strategy.

## Hormuz Dependence Exposes Systemic Risks

The article in splash247.com points out that approximately one-fifth of global oil trade transits the Strait of Hormuz. Disruptions in this critical chokepoint can have immediate and measurable effects on shipping, including increased insurance premiums and freight rates, as vessels reroute to avoid conflict zones. With 600-700 tankers, including VLCCs carrying crude, passing through the strait daily, representing approximately 20% of global oil demand, the potential for significant disruption is clear. This reliance underscores the need for diversification in energy sources and supply chains. This impacts our data directly, showing potential for sudden drops in CII ratings as ships divert and increase emissions. The EU ETS carbon costs for voyages via longer routes will also jump considerably.

## Hydrogen's Potential to Reduce Concentration Risk

Unlike oil and gas, hydrogen production is not geographically constrained by geological reserves. Green hydrogen, derived from renewable resources, offers the potential for more distributed production hubs across multiple continents. This shift could reduce the concentration risk associated with the current fossil fuel-based system. While trade would not disappear entirely, the distances could be shorter, such as North Africa supplying Europe. The splash247.com article emphasizes that instead of reacting to geopolitical flashpoints in oil-exporting regions, risk becomes more focused on renewable buildout timelines, policy certainty, and infrastructure deployment.

## Shipping's Dual Role and the Fuel Transition

The shipping industry is uniquely positioned in the energy transition, acting as both a consumer and a mover of hydrocarbons. This dual role places it at the heart of the transition conversation. While the transition to alternative fuels like green ammonia is gaining traction, shipowners remain cautious, balancing regulatory compliance against capital risk and technology maturity. Large-scale green ammonia projects are clustering in familiar regions, but electrolyser supply chains and the need for critical minerals introduce new dependencies. Diversifying marine fuel supply is crucial, especially considering the volatility of fossil fuel prices and the increased costs associated with navigating geopolitical hotspots. ## Lessons from History and the Path Forward

Past energy crises, such as the 1973 oil embargo, have spurred investment in energy efficiency and alternative sources, but the urgency often fades when prices stabilize. The 2022 European gas crisis led to accelerated renewable deployment, but also a resurgence in coal and new LNG supply deals, creating lock-in to fossil fuel infrastructure. However, the falling costs of renewable energy and electrolysis, attracting unprecedented private capital, suggest that this time could be different. Countries heavily reliant on Gulf energy flows are also among the fastest movers in building hydrogen import infrastructure. This suggests that the drivers for change are more resilient than in previous cycles. ## What this means for shipping emissions

Geopolitical instability is a catalyst for accelerating the energy transition and reshaping the shipping industry's role in global energy markets. The vulnerabilities exposed by reliance on concentrated fossil fuel production highlight the need for diversified energy sources and supply chains. While the transition to hydrogen and its derivatives presents its own challenges, it offers the potential to reduce concentration risk and create a more resilient energy system. For the shipping industry, this means embracing alternative fuels, adapting to new trade routes, and managing the evolving risks associated with capital investment and regulatory compliance. By focusing on security and resilience, policymakers can create an environment that fosters the transition towards a cleaner and more sustainable energy future, ultimately reducing shipping emissions.
