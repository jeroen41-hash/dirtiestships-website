Caribbean Nations Seek Unified Approach to Maritime Decarbonisation

A push for greater regional coordination on maritime decarbonisation is gaining momentum in the Caribbean, highlighting the crucial need for unified strategies to meet IMO emissions targets. With shipping facing increasingly stringent regulations, including the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's [CII ratings](/cii.html), a fragmented approach risks leaving smaller nations behind.

## Regional Collaboration Key to Green Shipping Transition

The call for a coordinated strategy emerged from an IMO roundtable held in Port of Spain, Trinidad and Tobago, under the GreenVoyage2050 programme (according to reporting by shipandbunker.com). The meeting underscored the consensus that successful decarbonisation hinges on collaborative policies, knowledge sharing, and readily financeable projects, rather than isolated efforts. This aligns with the broader understanding that global shipping's complex decarbonisation challenges require international and regional cooperation.

The article highlights draft recommendations focusing on harmonizing national policies, building capacity, and enhancing access to finance for clean fuels and port infrastructure. These are essential components, especially for smaller island nations with limited resources. The harmonisation of policies across the region could reduce the administrative burden for shipowners, potentially lowering the cost of compliance with regulations like the EU ETS if they operate in the area.

## Overcoming Barriers to Clean Fuel Adoption

Discussions at the IMO roundtable also touched on critical aspects like fuel pathways, infrastructure requirements, and obstacles to the adoption of alternative fuels. The Jamaica alternative fuels study serves as a valuable regional example, but broader understanding of specific regional barriers will be needed. Infrastructure development is a major stumbling block. The high upfront costs associated with building bunkering facilities for alternative fuels such as ammonia and methanol are considerable, and a coordinated regional approach could allow governments to share these costs or attract international investment more effectively.

Delegates toured the ammonia-fueled vessel Fortescue Green Pioneer. While showcasing the potential of alternative fuels, such visits also highlight the infrastructure gaps that need to be addressed before widespread adoption becomes feasible.

## Mobilising Finance for Green Maritime Projects

The second day of the event concentrated on improving project bankability and mobilizing finance for green maritime projects. Development banks presented financing tools and risk-sharing approaches. This focus on finance is paramount. The transition to cleaner fuels and technologies requires significant capital investment. Without readily available and accessible funding, particularly for developing nations, the pace of decarbonisation will inevitably lag behind. The [company rankings](/companies.html) on DirtiestShips.com show that companies investing in future-proof assets now may enjoy a financial advantage in the long run, particularly with the EU ETS expanding to shipping.

It is currently difficult to assess the potential financial impact of a harmonized Caribbean approach. However, collectively negotiating financing terms or establishing a regional green fund could prove more attractive to investors than individual nations pursuing projects independently.

## The Role of IMO and Data Transparency

The IMO's role in setting a global direction for GHG reduction is acknowledged in the article. However, Dr. Jose Matheickal emphasized that implementation occurs at the country and regional level. This reinforces the need for regional strategies tailored to specific contexts.

The EU MRV data, used to calculate [CII ratings](/cii.html) and assess EU ETS costs, provides valuable insight into ship emissions. However, equivalent, publicly accessible data for the Caribbean region is largely absent. Increased data transparency regarding ship traffic and emissions within the region would be vital for monitoring progress towards decarbonisation goals and informing policy decisions. The region could benefit from implementing a system comparable to EMSA MRV for its maritime traffic.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The Caribbean's drive for a coordinated maritime decarbonisation strategy represents a crucial step towards achieving global emissions reduction targets. By pooling resources, sharing knowledge, and attracting investment, Caribbean nations can collectively accelerate the transition to cleaner shipping. However, the success of this initiative hinges on concrete policy implementation, readily available financing, and increased data transparency – all of which require sustained commitment from both regional governments and international stakeholders. The future emissions profile of shipping in the Caribbean, and its effect on the wider industry, will be worth watching closely.
