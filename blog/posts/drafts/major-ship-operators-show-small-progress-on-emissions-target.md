The latest analysis from the Sea Cargo Charter paints a concerning picture for global shipping's decarbonization efforts, revealing that some of the largest operators and charterers are making only incremental progress in reducing their emissions intensity. Despite increasing regulatory pressure and a growing industry focus on sustainability, the collective performance of these key players continues to fall significantly short of the International Maritime Organization's (IMO) minimum trajectory for greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction. This highlights a critical disconnect between ambition and action, directly impacting the industry's ability to meet critical climate targets and exposing companies to escalating carbon costs and reputational risks.

## A Marginal Shift in Decarbonization Trajectory

The 2026 Sea Cargo Charter Annual Disclosure Report, covering 32 signatories responsible for 14% of global seaborne wet and dry bulk trades, indicates a persistent struggle to align with IMO targets. For 2025, these companies collectively missed the IMO’s minimum GHG emissions reduction trajectory by a substantial 11.6%. While this represents a slight improvement from the 12.2% misalignment reported for 2024, the progress is marginal, underscoring the formidable challenge ahead. The IMO’s targets mandate a reduction in lifecycle GHG from international shipping by at least 20% by 2030, 70% by 2040 (against 2008 levels), with the ultimate goal of achieving net zero close to 2050. The current pace suggests these ambitious milestones remain increasingly out of reach without more drastic interventions.

The report did note a positive trend in individual carbon intensity reductions: 20 out of 29 signatories that also reported 2024 data managed to reduce their carbon emission intensity in 2025, an improvement over the 19 out of 33 that achieved cuts in 2024. This suggests that while some companies are improving, not all are on a consistent downward path. ## Biofuels and Efficiency: The Current Levers

The primary drivers behind what little progress has been observed are the adoption of biofuels and energy-saving devices, alongside operational efficiency measures. According to the report, biofuels "currently appear to represent the most mature alternative fuel pathway," with wind-assist technologies also gaining momentum. Operational adjustments such as speed optimization, weather routing, and live performance monitoring were also cited by many signatories as effective in reducing emissions in 2025.

However, the cost implications of these alternative fuels are significant. The Platts bunker cost calculator highlights a stark difference: April’s average delivered bunker prices in Singapore saw 0.5%-sulfur fuel oil at $19/Gigajoule, while B24 (24% used cooking oil methyl ester blend) was $24/Gj, and 100% sustainable methanol soared to $49/Gj. This price disparity acts as a substantial barrier to wider adoption, making it challenging for operators to commit to costlier, cleaner fuels without stronger financial incentives or regulatory mandates. Emerging solutions like synthetic fuels and onboard carbon capture are acknowledged but remain in earlier stages of consideration.

## Regulatory Vacuum Hampers Progress

A critical impediment to accelerated decarbonization is the persistent "regulatory uncertainty" and the delays in adopting the IMO’s Net-Zero Framework. This framework was intended to introduce a cost on maritime GHG from 2028, creating a global incentive for a low-carbon bunker transition. As James Lewis, chair of the Sea Cargo Charter and global head of operations at Cargill Ocean Transportation, noted, "the IMO’s climate alignment goals will become progressively further out of reach without the tangible incentives and level playing field that a global regulatory framework would deliver."

This global regulatory vacuum contrasts sharply with regional initiatives like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), which has already begun placing a direct carbon cost on voyages to and from EU ports. While the Sea Cargo Charter report does not delve into ETS specifics, the lack of a global equivalent means that operators without exposure to regional regulations face fewer immediate financial pressures to transition away from fossil fuels, contributing to an uneven playing field. Our analysis of [EU MRV data](/emissions-data) and ETS cost implications demonstrates the very real financial impact on vessels operating in and out of European waters, driving a different level of urgency than what is seen globally.

By requiring signatories to disclose their GHG emissions annually using a common framework, the charter aims to foster accountability. ## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The Sea Cargo Charter's latest disclosure reveals an industry caught between ambition and inertia. While some operators are making individual strides in carbon intensity reduction, the collective progress is too slow to meet critical IMO targets. The reliance on biofuels and operational efficiencies offers only incremental gains, while the higher costs of cleaner fuels and the delay in a global carbon pricing mechanism stifle more transformative action. Without a unified, globally enforced regulatory framework that places a meaningful cost on emissions, shipping's decarbonization trajectory will continue to drift, leaving the industry vulnerable to stricter regional regulations and the escalating risks of climate change.
