COSCO's potential $2 billion order for up to 12 LNG dual-fuel container vessels signals a notable, albeit incremental, shift toward lower-emission fuels within the Chinese shipping giant's fleet. As the industry grapples with increasingly stringent environmental regulations like the EU ETS and the IMO's CII, this move represents a calculated step to mitigate future carbon costs and improve [CII ratings](/cii.html). However, the reliance on LNG, a fossil fuel, also highlights the complexities and ongoing debates surrounding transitional fuels in the decarbonization of shipping.

## COSCO's LNG Investment: A Breakdown

According to Splash247.com, COSCO is in advanced talks with CSSC’s Hudong-Zhonghua Shipbuilding for a series of 14,000 TEU neo-panamax LNG dual-fuel container vessels. The potential order of up to 12 ships could cost between $170 million and $190 million per vessel, totaling between $2 billion and $2.3 billion. These vessels are expected to be deployed by Orient Overseas Container Line (OOCL), COSCO’s Hong Kong-based liner arm. This follows an earlier order this year for around $2.7 billion worth of new tonnage, including 12 LNG dual-fuel 18,000 TEU vessels at Jiangnan Shipyard. While a delivery timeline is not yet available, these orders represent a substantial investment in LNG-powered vessels, alongside the group’s exploration of methanol as a marine fuel.

## LNG vs. Methanol: A Diversified Approach

COSCO appears to be hedging its bets in the alternative fuel landscape. While committing to LNG, the company has also invested in methanol-capable designs, notably OOCL’s 2025 order for 14 methanol dual-fuel 18,500 TEU boxships. This diversified approach suggests an awareness of the evolving regulatory landscape and the potential limitations of LNG as a long-term solution. Methanol, particularly green methanol produced from renewable sources, offers a pathway to significantly lower lifecycle emissions. However, the infrastructure and availability of green methanol remain significant hurdles. The recent chartering of six 13,000 TEU ships by OOCL from Seaspan, with deliveries from late 2026, further complicates the picture, as these vessels are presumably conventionally fueled.

## Fleet Expansion and Market Position

The potential Hudong order would further expand COSCO's already substantial newbuilding pipeline. COSCO, including OOCL, currently ranks as the world’s fourth-largest liner operator, with approximately 555 vessels and a capacity of around 3.6 million TEU. If this deal goes through, COSCO could have over 120 ships on order, totaling over 1.4 million TEU. ## EU ETS Implications and Carbon Costs

The EU ETS, which now includes maritime shipping, places a price on CO2 emissions from voyages to, from, and within the European Economic Area. As COSCO expands its fleet, particularly with vessels serving European routes, the company will face increasing carbon costs. While LNG dual-fuel engines can reduce CO2 emissions compared to conventional fuels, they are not zero-emission. Furthermore, methane slip, the release of unburned methane (a potent greenhouse gas) during LNG combustion, can offset some of the CO2 reduction benefits. Therefore, COSCO's LNG investments will likely result in only a modest improvement in their EU ETS carbon footprint compared to using conventional fuels, while future methanol-powered vessels may deliver larger reductions. ## What this means for shipping emissions

COSCO's move towards LNG dual-fuel vessels reflects a pragmatic approach to meeting current and near-future emissions regulations. While not a silver bullet, LNG can offer some CO2 reductions, especially if methane slip is minimized. However, the long-term decarbonization of shipping requires a shift towards truly zero-emission fuels like green methanol or ammonia. COSCO's diversified approach, encompassing both LNG and methanol, suggests an awareness of this need for a long-term strategy. The impact on overall shipping emissions will depend on the scale of adoption of these fuels and the stringency of future environmental regulations.
