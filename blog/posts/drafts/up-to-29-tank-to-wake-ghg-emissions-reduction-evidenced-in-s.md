The shipping industry faces intense pressure to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, driven by regulations like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's Carbon Intensity Indicator ([CII ratings](/cii.html)). A recent Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) published by the Society for Gas as a Marine Fuel (SGMF) claims that using Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) as a marine fuel can achieve significant reductions in tank-to-wake GHG emissions compared to traditional marine fuels like Marine Gas Oil (MGO). This report, while potentially positive, requires careful scrutiny to understand its implications for the overall decarbonization effort and its impact on shipping companies navigating these complex regulations.

## Claimed Emissions Reductions: A Closer Look

According to the SGMF report, LNG can achieve up to 29% reduction in tank-to-wake GHG emissions compared to MGO 0.1%. This is an improvement of up to 13% compared to SGMF's previous LCA conducted in 2022. The report also claims a well-to-wake GHG emissions reduction of up to 25%, supported by a well-to-tank emissions reduction of up to 9% compared to their first LNG LCA study published in 2019. These figures, if accurate and widely achievable, could influence decisions regarding fuel choices in the short to medium term.

However, the report's claims need to be viewed cautiously. Tank-to-wake emissions only represent a portion of the total lifecycle emissions. Furthermore, the article lacks specific details about the methodology used in the LCA, the types of LNG engines assessed, and the potential for methane slip, a potent greenhouse gas. The report mentions that the emissions calculations are based on the performance of a wide range of engine types including medium-speed 4-stroke and low-speed-2-stroke engines. Understanding the relative performance of these engine types is key to truly understanding potential gains. The article states that SGMF’s studies are critically peer reviewed by industry experts and leading academics, which should lend credibility to their findings.

## The Role of LCAs in Fuel Selection

The SGMF report underscores the importance of comprehensive and transparent Life Cycle Assessments (LCAs) in guiding fuel selection decisions. As Mark Bell, Managing Director of SGMF, noted, "Shipowners making decisions on future fuel pathways must rely on independent, fact based full Well-to-Wake data of all the fuels." This sentiment is echoed by Emma Scheiris, Deputy Director-Environment at INTERTANKO, who emphasized the need for LCAs that "can apply consistently across all fuels and reflect actual supply pathways."

The challenge lies in ensuring that these LCAs are truly independent and account for all relevant factors, including methane slip from LNG engines, the energy intensity of LNG production and transportation, and the potential for future improvements in both LNG and alternative fuel technologies. The upcoming LCAs from SGMF on methanol and ammonia, expected in the coming years, will provide further data points for comparison.

## Implications for CII Ratings and EU ETS

Even with potential GHG emissions reductions, LNG faces challenges within the framework of regulations like the IMO's CII and the EU ETS. While LNG can offer lower CO2 emissions compared to conventional fuels, methane slip can offset these gains, potentially impacting a vessel's [CII ratings](/cii.html). Vessels with poor CII ratings may face operational restrictions or be forced to adopt corrective measures, adding to operational costs.

Under the EU ETS, shipping companies are required to purchase allowances for their CO2 emissions. The cost of these allowances ([EU ETS carbon costs]) depends on the carbon intensity of the fuel used. If LNG's well-to-wake GHG emissions are not significantly lower than those of conventional fuels, ship operators may not see substantial cost savings under the EU ETS. Furthermore, the EU ETS is likely to expand to include other GHGs beyond CO2 in the future, which would further penalize fuels with high methane slip rates. This will affect [company emissions rankings](/companies.html) when the time comes to assess their performance under the regulation.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The SGMF report provides evidence for potential GHG emissions reductions from LNG as a marine fuel, but its claims require careful consideration within the broader context of lifecycle emissions and regulatory frameworks. While LNG may offer a transitional pathway to decarbonization, its long-term viability depends on addressing methane slip and demonstrating substantial well-to-wake GHG emissions reductions compared to alternative fuels like ammonia, methanol, and hydrogen. Shipping companies need to carefully evaluate all available data, including independent LCAs, to make informed decisions about fuel choices that align with both environmental regulations and business objectives. The true impact of LNG on shipping emissions will depend on the industry's ability to mitigate methane slip and improve the efficiency of the entire LNG supply chain.
