The IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) 84th session advanced a range of decisions beyond the highly publicized Net Zero Framework, directly impacting ship operations and potentially influencing the industry's overall emissions profile. While not directly targeting CO2 emissions in the same way as decarbonization efforts, these changes related to ballast water management, plastic pellet carriage, underwater radiated noise, and a new Emission Control Area (ECA) in the North Atlantic will shape operational requirements and indirectly contribute to broader environmental goals.

## Ballast Water Management Amendments

A significant outcome of MEPC 84 was the advancement of amendments to the Ballast Water Management (BWM) Convention. These amendments, expected to be adopted at MEPC 85 in 2026, focus on strengthening implementation of the existing convention rather than introducing entirely new concepts. Key areas of revision include contingency measures, ballast water management plan (BWMP) content and approval, crew familiarization, system maintenance, and record-keeping. The aim is to improve consistency, transparency, and practical application of the Convention.

Alongside the amendments, the Committee adopted revised 2026 Guidelines for ballast water management and development of Ballast Water Management Plans (G4). These guidelines are designed to align with the amended Convention requirements, offering clearer direction on ship-specific plan development and approval. The revised guidelines will take effect upon the entry into force of the Convention amendments, replacing the existing guidelines.

The article doesn't provide a direct link to emissions, but efficient ballast water management can optimize vessel operations, indirectly reducing fuel consumption and emissions. Proper system maintenance and consistent application of the BWM Convention could lead to slight efficiency gains, contributing to a better [CII rating](/cii.html) for affected vessels.

## Plastic Pellet Carriage Remains Contentious

The article highlights the ongoing debate surrounding the carriage of plastic pellets in freight containers. Member states have yet to agree on which IMO Convention (MARPOL, SOLAS, or both) should mandate a code on plastic pellet carriage. Despite the lack of consensus, the Committee instructed the Pollution Prevention and Response Sub-Committee (PPR) to develop a draft code. This code aims to provide operational clarity and environmental protection, potentially mirroring the requirements of the International Maritime Dangerous Goods (IMDG) code.

While seemingly unrelated to direct emissions, the environmental impact of plastic pellet loss at sea is significant. Stricter regulations and improved handling procedures could indirectly contribute to a more sustainable shipping industry. Addressing plastic pellet spills aligns with broader environmental stewardship goals, which are increasingly factored into company rankings and investor decisions. The [company rankings](/companies.html) page could consider a company's record on such environmental incidents when assessing overall performance.

## Underwater Radiated Noise (URN) Experience Building Phase Extended

MEPC 84 approved an extension of the Experience Building Phase on Underwater Radiated Noise (URN) until 2028. This extension was supported by BIMCO, citing the early stage of adoption of the 2023 revised guidelines and the need for more reliable data. The industry requires more time to understand effective and feasible URN mitigation measures.

Although URN doesn't directly impact CO2 emissions, it's an important environmental consideration. Reducing URN can contribute to a healthier marine ecosystem. Investing in technologies and operational practices that minimize URN could indirectly improve a company's environmental profile, potentially influencing its attractiveness to investors and stakeholders. Further data on noise reduction strategies should be added to [emissions data](/charts.html).

## North Atlantic Emission Control Area (ECA)

The news article makes mention of a new Emission Control Area (ECA) in the North Atlantic. Although not detailed in the article, this is a key point. ECAs limit the amount of sulfur oxides (SOx), particulate matter (PM) and nitrogen oxides (NOx) that ships can emit. ECAs force ships to burn cleaner fuels, install scrubbers, or use alternative technologies to comply with stricter emissions standards.

For ships operating in the North Atlantic ECA, this will necessitate using more expensive, lower-sulfur fuel or fitting emission abatement technologies. This will add to the cost of operation, and potentially influence the adoption of alternative fuels and technologies. The economic impact will be exacerbated by the recent EU ETS regulations, especially as costs rise.

## What this means for shipping emissions

MEPC 84 addressed a range of issues that, while not always directly linked to CO2 emissions reduction, collectively contribute to a more sustainable and environmentally responsible shipping industry. The amendments to the Ballast Water Management Convention, the ongoing discussions on plastic pellet carriage, and the extension of the URN Experience Building Phase all reflect the IMO's commitment to addressing the broader environmental impacts of shipping. The new ECA in the North Atlantic will directly impact SOx and PM emissions. These initiatives, combined with the ongoing push for decarbonization through measures like the EU ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html), are shaping the future of shipping and its environmental footprint.
