The shipping industry's scramble for decarbonization solutions is intensifying, and the newly launched Global Liquid Hydrogen Alliance (Alliance) signals a significant push towards liquid hydrogen (LH2) as a viable zero-emission fuel. With the IMO's Net Zero Framework gaining momentum and a global carbon price for shipping on the horizon, this initiative aims to accelerate the deployment of LH2 by addressing key logistical and financial hurdles.

## The Promise of Liquid Hydrogen: A Scalable Solution?

The Alliance, formed by the Zero Emissions Ship Technology Association (ZESTAs), aims to address the current fragmentation in the hydrogen project landscape. While over 600 hydrogen projects are linked to Europe with significant committed investment, Final Investment Decisions (FIDs) are stalling. According to ZESTAs, the gap isn't ambition but rather architecture. Unlike other hydrogen initiatives focusing on derivatives or blended fuels, the Alliance will concentrate exclusively on pure green hydrogen and LH2. The article cites industry reports projecting the liquid hydrogen market to grow from $9 billion today to $54 billion by 2037, with liquefaction capacity more than quadrupling. The Alliance aims to ensure LH2 is at the forefront of this growth.

However, whether this growth is truly attainable and sustainable hinges on factors not fully addressed in the article. ## Key Objectives of the Global Liquid Hydrogen Alliance

The Alliance has outlined four key priorities. First, building a "global ground truth" by creating a neutral, verifiable evidence base around LH2 technology, safety, logistics, costs, and performance. Second, positioning LH2 as a primary energy carrier for shipping, particularly for long-range applications. Third, accelerating market creation and international alignment through demand aggregation, offtaker coordination, standards development, and aligned policy engagement across the IMO, Europe, and key producing regions. Finally, turning policy momentum into deployment by identifying how LH2 can be rolled out through shipping corridors, port infrastructure, vessel integration, and supply chain partnerships.

These are ambitious goals, and the Alliance will need to navigate complex regulatory landscapes and technological challenges. For example, aligning LH2 standards across the IMO and regional bodies will be critical to ensure seamless global operations.

## Liquid Hydrogen: Advantages and Challenges

According to the article, LH2 is uniquely suited for long-range, high-energy-density maritime applications due to its scalability, renewability, and avoidance of issues associated with other alternative fuels such as ammonia, methanol, or biofuels. The article claims LH2 technology isn't emerging as it has an industrial track record spanning over six decades, with a global market valued at approximately US$45 billion and global liquefaction capacity of around 600 tonnes per day. Containerised LH2 transport is commercially operational today, using standard 40-foot cryogenic containers transportable by road, rail, and ship under existing regulatory frameworks, with no new infrastructure category required.

However, the article acknowledges a "deployment gap" that is geographic and coordination-related, not technical. Europe, for example, accounts for roughly one-third of global hydrogen demand but has limited liquefaction capacity. This suggests that substantial investment in infrastructure and supply chain development is needed to realize the full potential of LH2. As vessels adopt LH2, the [EU ETS carbon costs](/ets.html) of voyages will be a key economic factor in its viability.

## Regulatory Context and the IMO's Role

The launch of the Alliance comes at a crucial time, with the IMO's Net Zero Framework setting the stage for a global carbon price for shipping. The article states that the framework is the "agreed basis for future negotiations," signalling a clear regulatory direction. This provides a strong incentive for companies to invest in zero-emission technologies like LH2.

However, the pace and stringency of IMO regulations remain a point of contention. The [EMSA MRV data](/emissions.html) tracked on DirtiestShips.com will be crucial in monitoring the industry's progress towards decarbonization and assessing the effectiveness of measures like the EU ETS and the IMO's framework.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The formation of the Global Liquid Hydrogen Alliance represents a concerted effort to accelerate the adoption of LH2 as a zero-emission fuel for shipping. While challenges remain in terms of infrastructure development, regulatory alignment, and cost competitiveness, the Alliance's focus on building a neutral evidence base and fostering collaboration across the value chain could prove pivotal in driving down shipping emissions. The success of the Alliance and LH2 adoption will depend on ongoing investment, technological advancements, and supportive policy frameworks that incentivize the transition to cleaner fuels.
