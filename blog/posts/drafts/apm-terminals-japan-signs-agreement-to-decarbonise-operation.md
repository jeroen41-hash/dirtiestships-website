APM Terminals' recent agreement to power its Yokohama operations with renewable energy highlights a growing trend in the maritime sector: proactive decarbonization measures driven by both corporate sustainability goals and increasing regulatory pressure. While shipping itself faces growing scrutiny under the EU ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html), ports are also feeling the heat to clean up their operations, and this PPA signals a direct response to that pressure.

## Yokohama Terminal to Reduce Emissions by 2.6 ktCO2e Annually

The agreement, a 20-year Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) with FPS Inc., will see APM Terminals Yokohama source renewable electricity from newly developed solar facilities in the Tokyo region (hellenicshippingnews.com). The impact is projected to be a reduction of approximately 2.6 ktCO2e (kilotonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent) per year in Scope 2 GHG emissions. This represents a significant shift from fossil-based grid electricity to renewable solar power. Scope 2 emissions are those generated indirectly from purchased electricity. The solar facilities have a total installed capacity of 5.5 MW and started delivering power on April 1, 2026.

## Linking to Maersk's Decarbonization Targets

This initiative is closely aligned with Maersk's broader decarbonization pathway. The company aims to source 100% renewable electricity by 2030 and achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions across its entire value chain by 2040 (hellenicshippingnews.com). As a Maersk Group company, APM Terminals' efforts directly contribute to these ambitious targets. ## The Role of Physical PPAs in Decarbonization

APM Terminals and Maersk are strong advocates for physical PPAs as a means to decarbonize electricity consumption. The article emphasizes the importance of adding new renewable capacity to the national grid, ensuring that corporate climate action translates into tangible, long-term emissions reductions and supports wider grid decarbonization (hellenicshippingnews.com). This approach helps mitigate reliance on market-based offsets, which are sometimes criticized for lacking additionality. Also PPAs help protect against electricity price volatility.

## Implications for Port Decarbonization

While the article focuses on APM Terminals' actions, it's important to view this development in the broader context of port decarbonization. Ports are increasingly under pressure to reduce their emissions, not only from vessel operations, but also from terminal operations, equipment, and landside transportation. This PPA represents a concrete step towards reducing the carbon footprint of a major port terminal, setting an example for others to follow. While the article provides a high-level overview, detailed emissions data for port operations is not currently tracked in EU MRV, though pressure is mounting to expand the scope of emissions monitoring.

## EU ETS and the Drive for Sustainability

The urgency for measures like this PPA is fueled, in part, by regulatory pressures, including the extension of the EU ETS to maritime shipping. While the direct impact of the EU ETS falls on shipping companies, the ripple effects are being felt throughout the entire maritime supply chain. Ports that can offer lower-emission services and infrastructure will become more attractive to shipping companies seeking to minimize their carbon costs under the EU ETS. The price of carbon credits within the EU ETS creates the economic incentives necessary to accelerate investment in renewable energy and other decarbonization technologies. As ships are also rated by the [CII ratings](/cii.html) scheme, ports with lower emissions can contribute to improved ratings for ships that call at their facilities.

**What this means for shipping emissions:** This agreement, while focused on a single terminal, exemplifies a broader trend towards decarbonization within the maritime industry. As regulatory pressures increase and corporate sustainability goals become more ambitious, we can expect to see more ports and terminals adopting renewable energy solutions and investing in other technologies to reduce their carbon footprint. This is crucial for achieving meaningful reductions in overall shipping emissions.
