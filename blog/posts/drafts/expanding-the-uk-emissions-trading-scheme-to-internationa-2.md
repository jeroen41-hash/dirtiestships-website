The UK Emissions Trading Scheme (ETS), designed to drive decarbonization across key sectors, currently leaves a significant portion of shipping emissions untouched. While the planned 2026 expansion to include domestic shipping is a step forward, a new analysis highlights the urgent need for a broader scope to truly impact maritime emissions and avoid loopholes that could undermine the scheme's effectiveness.

## The Limited Scope of the Current UK ETS

The UK ETS, operational since 2021, operates on a "cap and trade" principle, requiring companies to surrender one allowance for every tonne of CO2 equivalent (CO2e) emitted. However, maritime shipping was initially excluded. The planned 2026 expansion will only capture emissions from vessels of 5,000 gross tonnage (GT) or more while at berth, at anchor, or traveling between UK ports. According to a recent analysis from the International Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT), this only addresses a small fraction of the problem.

In 2023, ships engaged in UK-related voyages emitted 16.4 million tonnes (Mt) of CO2e. Of this, vessels of 5,000 GT or more were responsible for nearly 80%, largely stemming from voyages between the UK and international ports. The 2026 UK ETS scope, focusing on in-port and domestic voyages of larger vessels, only covers an estimated 2.5 Mt, or a mere 15% of the total [emissions data](/charts.html). This leaves a substantial 12.1 Mt of CO2e from international voyages unaddressed, the largest single source of emissions related to UK shipping.

## Potential Gains From Expanding the UK ETS

The ICCT analysis makes a compelling case for expanding the UK ETS to include a portion of international voyages and vessels smaller than 5,000 GT. By covering 50% of international voyages from vessels over 5,000 GT, the UK could more than triple its emissions coverage. Including vessels under 5,000 GT could quadruple the current scope.

Financially, this translates to a significant increase in revenue generated by the scheme. While the current scope is projected to generate £138–£173 million annually, expanding to include international voyages and smaller vessels could increase this to £570–£710 million. This additional revenue could then be reinvested into supporting further maritime decarbonization efforts.

## Addressing Leakage and Gaming Risks

One of the critical concerns highlighted in the analysis is the potential for "leakage" and "gaming" if the UK ETS remains narrowly focused. Without broader coverage, operators may be incentivized to divert their routes to non-covered ports or utilize smaller vessels to circumvent the 5,000 GT threshold, effectively undermining the scheme's intended impact. Such actions could affect a company's standing in our [company rankings](/companies.html) if they choose to engage in such behavior.

This issue is particularly relevant in the context of the [CII ratings](/cii.html), which assess the operational carbon intensity of ships. Operators seeking to improve their CII ratings might be tempted to exploit loopholes in the ETS, potentially leading to unintended consequences. Similarly, without consistent standards enforced through mechanisms like the EMSA MRV data collection system, any regional ETS risks simply displacing emissions elsewhere.

## EU ETS Linkage and Future Considerations

The announcement of negotiations between the European Commission and the UK government to link the EU ETS and UK ETS is a positive sign. Aligning allowances and carbon prices could significantly expand the overall coverage and create a more robust framework for decarbonizing shipping in the region. This convergence is essential to avoid market distortions and ensure a level playing field for operators.

However, even with a linked EU-UK ETS, a substantial portion of international shipping emissions would still remain outside the scope of regulation. The ICCT report suggests the UK ETS Authority should remain vigilant and proactively address these gaps.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The current UK ETS proposal only scratches the surface of shipping's emissions problem. While linking with the EU ETS offers promise, expanding the scheme's scope to include international voyages and smaller vessels is crucial. This would significantly increase emissions coverage, generate substantial revenue for decarbonization initiatives, and mitigate the risks of leakage and gaming, leading to a more effective and impactful effort to reduce shipping's carbon footprint.
