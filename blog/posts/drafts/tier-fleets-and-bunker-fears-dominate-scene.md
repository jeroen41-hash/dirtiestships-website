The dry bulk shipping sector faces a complex landscape of rising demand, geopolitical instability, and an increasingly stringent regulatory environment aimed at decarbonization, creating a "two-tier fleet" and raising serious concerns about bunker fuel availability and the feasibility of current green transition strategies. This challenging outlook was a central theme at the recent Geneva Dry conference, where industry experts debated the key issues impacting the sector.

## Dry Bulk Demand Remains Strong, But Clouds Gather

Despite global economic uncertainties, dry bulk trade has shown resilience. According to Kpler data presented at the conference, seaborne dry bulk trade grew by just over 2% in the first four months of 2024, reaching approximately 1.7 billion tonnes. Panamax, Supramax, and Handysize TC rate averages are also up significantly year-on-year, at 56%, 41%, and 34% respectively [splash247.com]. This growth, however, is threatened by geopolitical risks, such as the potential closure of the Strait of Hormuz, which disrupts commodity supply chains and impacts fertilizer exports from the Middle East, affecting planting decisions globally. Coal markets are also in flux, with Indonesia potentially shifting production strategies and creating new demand patterns. These shifts could influence vessel utilization and, consequently, emissions profiles reflected in [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## The Rise of the Two-Tier Fleet

A significant concern raised at Geneva Dry was the growing bifurcation of the global dry bulk fleet due to an increasing compliance burden. John Xylas, CEO of Ariston Navigation and chairman of INTERCARGO, highlighted that the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), FuelEU Maritime, [CII ratings](/cii.html), EEXI, tightening port state control, and stricter charterer vetting are creating a divide between well-managed and less well-managed vessels. This isn't necessarily about vessel age or size but rather the quality of management. Charterers like Anglo American are increasingly prioritizing higher standards and actively shaping the market to favor compliant vessels. This trend will likely exacerbate the cost differential between high and low-rated ships under the CII, increasing the operational expenses of vessels with poor energy efficiency ratings and their associated [EU ETS carbon costs](https://www.google.com/search?q=EU+ETS+carbon+costs). Vessels with lower ratings are likely to be disadvantaged as more charterers factor in [company emissions rankings](/companies.html).

## Bunker Fuel Availability and Price Volatility

The availability and cost of bunker fuel are also major concerns for the dry bulk sector. The panel at Geneva Dry noted that while the initial price spike following the Hormuz crisis was sentiment-driven, underlying fundamentals are now catching up. Refineries prioritizing distillate production will squeeze fuel oil supply for shipping, while increased use of fuel oil for power generation further tightens the market. This demand squeeze suggests that bunker prices are unlikely to return to pre-war levels anytime soon. This price volatility directly impacts voyage costs and influences decisions around slow steaming, which can affect [CII ratings](/cii.html) by reducing a vessel's annual fuel consumption, and also the overall costs associated with [EU ETS carbon costs](https://www.google.com/search?q=EU+ETS+carbon+costs).

## Decarbonization Realities and Policy Challenges

The panel also addressed the challenges of decarbonization in the shipping industry. There was skepticism about the near-term feasibility of hydrogen, ammonia, and green methanol at the scale required by the industry. One panelist suggested current decarbonization policies risk simply taxing the industry without effectively cleaning the environment. While acknowledging the need for ambition, another panelist emphasized the importance of pragmatism and collaboration, citing China's renewable energy investments and green corridors as potential building blocks. The discussion highlighted the tension between ambitious regulatory targets and the practical realities of technology readiness and infrastructure development. Data reported via [EMSA MRV data](https://www.emsa.europa.eu/emissions-monitoring-reporting-verification-mrv.html) will become even more critical as the industry tracks its progress against regulatory goals.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The dry bulk sector is navigating a turbulent period. Strong demand is offset by geopolitical risks and regulatory pressures. The emergence of a two-tiered fleet driven by compliance requirements will likely lead to a divergence in operational costs and market access. Concerns over bunker fuel availability and price volatility add further complexity. While the industry is committed to decarbonization, skepticism remains about the feasibility of current strategies and the effectiveness of existing regulations. Addressing these challenges requires a balanced approach that combines ambitious targets with pragmatic solutions and realistic timelines. The impacts on overall shipping emissions are potentially substantial, particularly given the dry bulk sector's significant contribution to global trade and its carbon footprint.
