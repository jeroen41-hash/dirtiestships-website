The global shipping industry, facing mounting pressure to decarbonize, has reaffirmed its unified support for the International Maritime Organization (IMO) as the primary regulator for reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions. This unified stance comes ahead of the crucial MEPC 84 meeting, where IMO member states are expected to make critical decisions on the path forward for shipping's decarbonization. The industry's statement underscores its commitment to the IMO's 2023 GHG Strategy, while simultaneously cautioning against regional regulations that could lead to double penalties for the same emissions, something that will affect companies ranked on [company rankings](/companies.html).

## Industry Backs Global Regulation, Warns Against Regional Fragmentation

The seven major shipping organizations – BIMCO, CLIA, ICS, INTERCARGO, INTERFERRY, INTERTANKO, and WSC – representing the vast majority of the global commercial shipping fleet, jointly issued the statement reported by hellenicshippingnews.com. The core message is a strong preference for globally consistent regulations delivered through the IMO, rather than a patchwork of regional or national schemes. The industry argues that the IMO has a proven track record of delivering effective regulations for maritime safety, security, and environmental protection. They emphasized the risk of "double or multiple penalties" imposed on ships if subject to overlapping regulations, especially with schemes like the EU ETS already impacting shipping within European waters. This could disproportionately affect ships with lower [CII ratings](/cii.html) that are already penalized in some ports.

## The Push for Fuel Neutrality and Data-Driven Decisions

The joint statement emphasizes the need for IMO regulations to be fuel and technology-neutral. This means avoiding regulations that favor specific fuel types or technologies and instead focusing on outcomes – the actual reduction of GHG emissions. The industry highlights the importance of considering the availability, safety, and scalability of alternative fuels when developing new regulations. While supporting a range of solutions, the statement includes transitional fuels like LNG and LPG, alongside alternative fuels like e-fuels, biomethanol, and ammonia, as well as technologies like wind power and onboard carbon capture. Data-driven decision-making is also a core demand, presumably relying on sources like the EMSA MRV data which is used to generate [emissions data](/charts.html) on this site. The fuel lifecycle assessment is critical to determining the real-world carbon emissions for alternative fuels, a factor which has yet to be completed by the IMO.

## Enforcement and Regulatory Certainty

Beyond the technical aspects of GHG reduction measures, the shipping industry stresses the importance of practicable enforcement of new regulations. Effective enforcement is seen as crucial for ensuring a level playing field across the industry and for securing the intended reductions in GHG emissions. They seek regulatory certainty from the IMO to encourage investment in alternative fuels and technologies. A clear and predictable regulatory framework is essential to encourage energy producers to ramp up production and supply of these new fuels, which are currently limited in availability and scale.

## Connecting to the EU ETS and Carbon Costs

The article does not explicitly mention the EU ETS, however the industry's concern over overlapping regulations is a clear reference to the existing EU scheme. The EU ETS, which applies to ships calling at EU ports, imposes a carbon price on their emissions. The cost of carbon allowances under the EU ETS can be substantial, particularly for older and less efficient ships. The industry is worried about similar regional schemes emerging in other parts of the world, which would further complicate compliance and potentially lead to financial penalties being imposed multiple times for the same emissions. This could also impact companies differently based on how efficiently they manage their routes and which ports they serve.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The shipping industry's unified support for the IMO is a clear message to policymakers: the industry wants a globally coordinated approach to decarbonization. While the commitment to reducing GHG emissions is evident, the industry is keen to avoid a fragmented regulatory landscape that could increase costs and hinder progress. The success of the IMO's strategy will depend on its ability to develop practical, effective, and enforceable regulations that are fuel-neutral and take into account the availability and safety of alternative fuels. The MEPC 84 meeting represents a critical opportunity for the IMO to demonstrate its leadership in driving decarbonization in the shipping industry and is the focus of news analysis in our [news feed](/news.html).
