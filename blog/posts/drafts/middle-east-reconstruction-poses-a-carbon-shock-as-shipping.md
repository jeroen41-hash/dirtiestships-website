The looming reconstruction efforts in the Middle East, particularly in Gaza and Lebanon, present a significant and largely unaddressed carbon emissions challenge, potentially dwarfing the emissions generated by global shipping over several weeks. This stark contrast highlights a critical imbalance: the shipping industry faces increasing pressure to decarbonize through regulations and carbon pricing mechanisms, while the carbon footprint of conflict and reconstruction remains largely unaccounted for.

## The Scale of the Reconstruction Emissions Problem

A recent study published in *One Earth* (cited in gcaptain.com) estimates that rebuilding the damage from the first four months of the conflict in Gaza alone could generate between 46.8 million and 60 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent. This figure surpasses the annual emissions of over 135 nations and rivals the emissions of countries like Sweden and Portugal. The destruction in Southern Lebanon, with an estimated 3,600 homes destroyed, adds another 881,433 tonnes of CO2 equivalent to the reconstruction burden. The article points out the World Bank estimated damages to Gaza's infrastructure at $18.5 billion in the early months of the conflict.

While the *gcaptain.com* article does not specify the exact timeframe, it states these emissions could "rival weeks of global shipping emissions." For context, the OECD estimates that global maritime transport emitted approximately 973 million tonnes of CO2 in 2024. This suggests that the Gaza reconstruction emissions could potentially equal 2-3 weeks of total shipping emissions.

## Cement and Steel: The Carbon Culprits

The article emphasizes the carbon-intensive nature of cement and steel production as key drivers of reconstruction emissions. Cement manufacturing is responsible for approximately 7-8% of global CO2 emissions, with much of this tied to the chemical process of calcination, which releases CO2 directly from limestone. The article notes that steelmaking, crucial for rebar, is also a significant industrial emitter. These materials are essential for rebuilding infrastructure, including apartment blocks, hospitals, schools, and transport networks.

## Shipping's Carbon Accountability vs. Reconstruction's Blind Spot

The core issue raised by the article is the disparity in carbon accountability. The shipping industry is increasingly subject to stringent environmental regulations, including the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's tightening regulatory framework. Ships are taxed and penalized for every tonne of CO2 emitted, particularly when calling at EU ports. This can greatly affect a ship's [CII rating](/cii.html) as well as the operational costs.

From 2026, full EU ETS liability will apply to ships. This means that shipping companies calling at EU ports will need to purchase carbon allowances for their emissions, adding a significant financial burden. Our [company rankings](/companies.html) highlight those shipping companies most exposed to the impact of the EU ETS carbon costs.

Conversely, the article notes that the nations responsible for the destruction and subsequent reconstruction face no equivalent carbon obligations. This creates a situation where the shipping industry, tasked with transporting reconstruction materials, is penalized for facilitating a process driven by factors entirely outside its control.

## Data Gaps and Further Analysis Needed

While the article highlights a critical issue, specific data on the types of ships involved in transporting reconstruction materials and the companies responsible for these shipments is lacking. Analyzing EMSA MRV data (as used on our site to generate [emissions data](/charts.html)) for specific routes and ship types could provide more granular insights into the actual impact of reconstruction on shipping emissions and [CII ratings](/cii.html). Further research is also needed to evaluate alternative, lower-carbon building materials and construction methods that could mitigate the environmental impact of reconstruction.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The Middle East reconstruction carbon challenge presents a complex dilemma for the shipping industry and its regulators. While efforts to decarbonize shipping are essential, the article underscores the need for a broader perspective on carbon accountability that addresses the emissions associated with conflict and reconstruction. If these emissions are not addressed, they could significantly offset the gains made through shipping efficiency improvements and the adoption of alternative fuels. The industry may also be unfairly penalised for work that benefits communities in need. The issue demands that policymakers consider a more holistic approach to climate action that incorporates the carbon footprint of conflict and reconstruction into global emissions reduction strategies.
