The International Maritime Organization's (IMO) proposed Net-Zero Framework (NZF), a cornerstone of the industry's decarbonization efforts, faces a critical juncture. A new report estimates the NZF could generate $11-12 billion annually, contingent on its adoption and design, potentially impacting the speed and equity of shipping's green transition. This significant revenue stream could either accelerate the shift to low and zero-carbon fuels or become mired in governance complexities, impacting everything from [CII ratings](/cii.html) to [company emissions rankings](/companies.html).

## The Promise of Billions: Fueling Decarbonization

The $11-12 billion revenue projection, according to shipandbunker.com, hinges on the IMO adopting the NZF in its current form ("IMO Net-Zero Framework Could Generate $11-12 Billion in Revenues"). This sum represents a substantial pool of capital earmarked for two primary objectives: incentivizing the adoption of alternative fuels and establishing a global climate fund. The latter is particularly significant, aimed at mitigating the potential negative consequences of decarbonization on developing nations and ensuring a "just and equitable transition." Without such a fund, poorer nations could bear a disproportionate economic burden as the shipping industry moves towards cleaner fuels, potentially undermining global support for decarbonization efforts. The current EU ETS system already shows some of these challenges.

## Governance: The Devil in the Details

The report highlights that the success of the NZF hinges on its governance structure. The design of the fund—specifically its "impact areas, governance, recipients, and delivery mechanisms"—will determine its effectiveness and broad acceptance. A critical decision involves whether the IMO maintains full control over the fund or collaborates with existing climate finance institutions. The former offers greater control but could be slower to implement, while the latter allows for faster rollout but raises concerns about alignment with the IMO's specific goals for the maritime sector. The potential for uneven distribution of funds, favoring countries with "stronger project pipelines," is another significant concern. A "blended approach," as the report suggests, might be necessary to balance scalability with fairness, ensuring that resources are directed where they are most needed to develop zero-emission solutions, regardless of national economic strength.

## The US-Saudi Stumbling Block and the Road Ahead

The article highlights the challenge of gaining consensus. Despite reaching an agreement in principle in April, a final vote on the NZF was postponed in October 2025 due to opposition from a bloc led by the US and Saudi Arabia. This delay underscores the deep divisions within the IMO regarding the speed and scope of decarbonization measures. The coming year will be crucial, as member states decide whether to adopt the framework as is or to compromise on a less ambitious version. This vote directly impacts the cost effectiveness for ships trading in and out of Europe due to the interaction with the EU's ETS. It also impacts the long term [CII ratings](/cii.html) for vessels still using fossil fuels.

## Connecting the Dots: EU ETS and a Global Levy

While the article focuses on the IMO's proposed framework, it's essential to view this in the context of existing regional regulations, particularly the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). The EU ETS already places a price on carbon emissions from ships calling at European ports, effectively creating a regional carbon levy. The IMO's NZF, if implemented, could establish a global carbon pricing mechanism, potentially harmonizing efforts and avoiding fragmentation of the market. However, the interaction between the two systems needs careful consideration. A global levy could complement the EU ETS or, depending on its design, potentially undermine its effectiveness. The increased costs of running vessels will further push ship owners to look at improving vessel efficiency. Data for this, from EU MRV reporting, can be viewed via [emissions data](/charts.html) on this site.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The IMO's Net-Zero Framework represents a potentially transformative mechanism for accelerating the decarbonization of the shipping industry. The prospect of generating $11-12 billion annually offers a significant boost to the development and adoption of alternative fuels, as well as support for a just transition. However, the success of the NZF hinges on overcoming political hurdles, establishing a robust and equitable governance structure, and ensuring that its implementation aligns with existing regional regulations like the EU ETS. The coming year will be pivotal in determining whether the NZF lives up to its potential or becomes another example of well-intentioned but ultimately ineffective climate policy. This will have material impacts to the costs facing shipping companies which, in turn, will have to be factored into their [company rankings](/companies.html).
