The shipping industry's pursuit of decarbonization is at a critical juncture, with increasing scrutiny on the viability of transitional fuels. A new study warns that investments in LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas) and methanol-fueled vessels may create "dead ends" that hinder, rather than help, the long-term shift to zero-emission alternatives like green ammonia. This has significant implications for shipping companies navigating the complexities of the EU ETS and striving to improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## LNG and Methanol: Stepping Stones or Dead Ends?

The research paper, submitted to Environmental Innovation and Societal Transitions, introduces an analytical framework to evaluate whether near-term fuel choices truly pave the way for a zero-emission future. It differentiates between "stepping stones," which build capacity for sustainable solutions, and "dead ends," which lock in fossil-based infrastructure, creating stranded assets. According to Dr. Pinar Langer from the UCL Energy Institute, confusing short-term emissions reduction with long-term progress poses a real danger.

The study leverages a mixed-methods approach, including 25 interviews with industry professionals, document analysis, patent data analysis, and econometric analysis of 7,355 vessels from the Clarksons World Fleet Register. This broad approach provides a robust assessment of the potential pitfalls associated with LNG and methanol adoption. The key concern highlighted is that while these fuels may offer some immediate emissions reductions, they can also lead to capital lock-in and the entrenchment of fossil fuel infrastructure, thereby diverting resources and attention from truly sustainable alternatives.

## The Risk of Capital Lock-in

The study identifies a significant risk of capital lock-in associated with LNG and, especially, methanol. This lock-in extends to multiple areas: material and financial resources, technological knowledge, regulatory institutions, and industry expectations. The analysis shows that while LNG and methanol can generate some knowledge spillovers and regulatory learning applicable to ammonia, these benefits are outweighed by the risk of stranded assets and the continued dependence on fossil fuel supply chains.

This is particularly relevant for companies that are heavily investing in LNG-fueled vessels to meet current emission targets or improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html). These companies may find themselves at a disadvantage if regulations tighten further, favoring truly zero-emission fuels. The stranded asset risk also impacts the cost of compliance with the EU ETS. While LNG offers a reduction in CO2 emissions compared to traditional fuels, it still incurs carbon costs under the scheme, and as the ETS tightens, these costs will increase, potentially making LNG less economically attractive in the long run. ## Policy and Design are Key

The research paper suggests that LNG could potentially serve as a stepping stone, but only if stringent policies are put in place to drive the deployment of "ammonia-ready" ships, port infrastructure, and bunkering systems. Without these safeguards, LNG investments risk becoming stranded assets that compete with, rather than enable, green ammonia. Methanol is viewed as offering even weaker stepping stone characteristics, potentially diverting capital and political attention away from ammonia without providing substantial system benefits.

This highlights the importance of forward-looking policies and infrastructure planning. Governments and regulatory bodies need to create a clear pathway for the transition to zero-emission fuels, providing incentives for the development of ammonia infrastructure and disincentives for investments that perpetuate fossil fuel dependence. ## What this means for shipping emissions

The study raises serious concerns about the long-term effectiveness of LNG and methanol as decarbonization strategies for the shipping industry. While these fuels may offer some near-term emissions reductions, they also carry a significant risk of locking in fossil fuel infrastructure and diverting resources from truly sustainable alternatives. Shipping companies need to carefully consider the long-term implications of their fuel choices, factoring in the evolving regulatory landscape and the increasing likelihood of stringent emissions targets. A shift towards "ammonia-ready" designs and a focus on developing the necessary infrastructure for green ammonia are crucial to ensure a sustainable and decarbonized shipping industry.
