The relentless pressure to decarbonize global supply chains is driving unprecedented collaboration within the maritime industry. As regulators push for greater transparency and accountability through frameworks like the EU MRV and the looming costs of the EU ETS, shipping companies are being forced to innovate beyond mere operational efficiency. A new partnership between Taiwanese carrier Yang Ming Marine Transport Corporation and port operator PSA International highlights this critical shift, aiming to integrate sea and land-based solutions to accelerate emission reductions across the entire logistics chain. This initiative offers a glimpse into how major players are attempting to navigate the complex landscape of carbon accountability, from ship to shore.

## Yang Ming's Decarbonization Trajectory

Yang Ming has reportedly made significant strides in reducing its carbon footprint, announcing a 63.32% cut in its carbon intensity by the end of 2025 compared with 2008 levels. While carbon intensity reductions (emissions per unit of transport work) are distinct from absolute emission cuts, such a substantial improvement is crucial for a company's environmental performance metrics. This level of intensity reduction directly impacts the company's individual vessel [CII ratings](/cii.html), pushing them towards higher (better) grades. Sustained efforts in energy efficiency, vessel optimization, and potentially fuel transitions contribute to these figures, which are ultimately reflected in the EU MRV data submitted for their fleet operating within European waters.

Beyond its own operational intensity, Yang Ming is extending its decarbonization commitment to its customers through its new EcoSea+ service. This service is explicitly designed to help clients reduce their Scope 3 transportation emissions, addressing the indirect emissions generated throughout the value chain. This move signals a recognition that freight customers increasingly demand verifiable green logistics solutions to meet their own corporate sustainability targets, creating a market imperative for carriers to offer low-carbon transport options.

## Integrating Sea and Land for End-to-End Solutions

The core of this announcement lies in the strategic partnership with PSA International. Acknowledging that "no single company can achieve supply chain decarbonization alone," Yang Ming and PSA are combining their respective strengths to offer a "sea-land joint carbon inset package." As stated by Kevin Lee, President of Yang Ming, this involves leveraging Yang Ming's "maritime fuel initiatives" with PSA's "electrified terminal capabilities."

PSA's role is critical in addressing the often-overlooked emissions from port operations and inland logistics. Their "Node to Network strategy" aims to integrate port and supply chain capabilities to support greener terminal and inland networks. This means tackling emissions from cargo handling equipment, drayage, and warehousing – areas traditionally outside the direct purview of a shipping line but integral to the overall supply chain's carbon footprint. The collaboration therefore moves beyond the vessel's chimney, aiming to mitigate emissions from the moment cargo leaves its origin until it reaches its final destination.

## Transparency and Verification: The Book-and-Claim Framework

A significant aspect of this partnership is its focus on transparency and verifiable emission reductions. The collaboration intends to implement a "digital Book-and-Claim framework" and a "joint maritime-land based inset token package." This mechanism is designed to provide cargo stakeholders with a "transparent and accountable sea-land pathway" to achieve their decarbonization targets.

The Book-and-Claim system allows for the decoupling of the environmental benefit from the physical flow of goods. For instance, a shipper can "claim" the emissions reduction achieved by a green fuel used on a different leg of the journey, or from electrified terminal operations, even if their specific cargo wasn't transported via that exact green pathway. The emphasis on "verifiable approach to mitigate transparent emissions" is crucial here. As the EU ETS expands and more stringent regulations come into play, the ability to accurately measure, verify, and report these reductions will become paramount, especially for mitigating carbon costs and demonstrating compliance to increasingly demanding [company rankings](/company.html) criteria. While the article doesn't detail the verification process, it implies a robust system for tracking and attributing these emission reductions across the supply chain.

## Implications for Carbon Costs and Compliance

For carriers like Yang Ming, aggressive decarbonization initiatives directly impact their exposure to the EU ETS. While the EU ETS primarily targets direct emissions (Scope 1) from vessels, demonstrating a strong commitment to reducing carbon intensity and actively participating in broader supply chain decarbonization efforts positions a company favorably. Such partnerships and verifiable green offerings could potentially differentiate carriers in a competitive market, attracting clients who are themselves under pressure to report on their Scope 3 emissions.

The emphasis on measurement, verification, and a transparent framework also aligns with the principles underpinning EU MRV data collection. The more precisely companies can track and report their emissions reductions across the entire journey, the more robust their claims become in a regulatory environment that increasingly demands granular data. This holistic approach signals an understanding that future regulations and market demands will likely extend beyond the single vessel, encompassing the entire transport chain.

## What this means for shipping emissions

This partnership between Yang Ming and PSA International signifies a growing recognition that decarbonizing global shipping requires a systemic, collaborative approach spanning the entire logistics ecosystem. Moving beyond isolated vessel efficiency improvements, the focus on integrated sea-land solutions, verifiable emission reductions, and transparent frameworks like Book-and-Claim will be critical for achieving meaningful supply chain decarbonization. As regulatory pressures intensify and carbon costs from schemes like the EU ETS continue to rise, such alliances are not just strategic advantages, but increasingly, necessities for companies aiming to meet global climate targets and maintain their competitive edge in a rapidly evolving, emission-conscious market.
