The future of low-emission shipping fuels hangs in the balance as European biochemical projects face delays and a US-based e-methanol project secures a crucial offtake agreement, highlighting both the challenges and opportunities in decarbonizing the maritime sector. While European bio-attributed chemical projects stall due to cost pressures and weak demand, a US e-methanol plant gains momentum thanks to the EU's FuelEU Maritime regulation, showcasing the crucial role of policy in driving investment in alternative fuels.

## European Bio-Chemical Projects Face Headwinds

Several European projects aimed at producing bio-attributed chemicals are experiencing significant delays, threatening the region's ambition to become a leader in sustainable materials. According to Argus Media, projects by companies like Vioneo, Syclus, and Futerro are being pushed back to 2029-2030 due to "weak demand, cost pressures, and, at times, a lack of feedstock." Vioneo, for example, is moving its planned 300,000 t/yr methanol-to-polymers plant from Antwerp, Belgium, to China, citing a "faster route to market." This relocation underscores the competitive disadvantage faced by European producers due to higher energy costs and feedstock prices compared to other regions. This mirrors concerns raised by the EU Policy Affairs manager from European Bioplastics in November 2025, who noted that while much of the bioplastics technology was developed in Europe, industrialization often takes place elsewhere because of lower energy costs and investment opportunities. The current operational projects for bio-attributed chemicals and bio-attributed plastics total over 1.5mn t/yr of capacity, compared with planned projects in Europe totalling 741,000 t/yr by 2030, Argus projections show. The delays directly impact the availability of sustainable feedstocks crucial for the production of low-carbon fuels like bio-methanol, which are relevant for the shipping industry's transition.

## FuelEU Maritime Drives E-Methanol Investment

In contrast to the struggles in the European bio-chemical sector, Irish project developer ET Fuels has secured a significant win with a long-term offtake agreement to supply e-methanol to UK-based shipping company RFOcean from 2030. The supply will come from ET Fuels' Rattlesnake Gap plant in Texas, which will produce 120,000 t/yr of e-methanol. This deal is explicitly driven by the EU's FuelEU Maritime regulation, which requires shipping companies to gradually reduce the carbon intensity of their bunker fuels. RFOcean's chief executive, Fredrik Rye-Florentz, stated that "compliant fuel will be scarce," and that locking in supply now at fixed prices provides certainty under FuelEU Maritime. The agreement allows ET Fuels to proceed with securing financing for the Rattlesnake Gap project, demonstrating the direct impact of regulation on investment decisions.

## The Role of Policy and Incentives

The contrasting fortunes of European bio-chemical projects and the US e-methanol plant highlight the critical role of policy and incentives in driving the uptake of sustainable fuels. The Argus Media article points out that "stable regulations unlock investment." The FuelEU Maritime regulation, with its targets for reducing carbon intensity, creates a demand signal that encourages companies like RFOcean to commit to long-term e-methanol supply agreements. This agreement also highlights how the multiplier applied for the use of non-carbon fuels from 2030-34 under FuelEU Maritime offers "a big incentive" to shipping companies to secure e-fuels. Without similar supportive policies and incentives in Europe, the bio-chemical sector risks falling behind, potentially hindering the availability of sustainable feedstocks for the maritime industry.

## Implications for Shipping Companies and [CII ratings](/cii.html)

The availability and cost of alternative fuels directly impact shipping companies' ability to comply with emissions regulations and improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html). Vessels using e-methanol or bio-methanol can significantly reduce their well-to-wake greenhouse gas emissions, contributing to a better CII rating and potentially reducing their exposure to carbon pricing mechanisms like the EU ETS. As the EU ETS expands to include shipping, companies with lower carbon footprints will face lower compliance costs. Securing long-term supply agreements for sustainable fuels, as RFOcean has done, can provide a competitive advantage by ensuring access to compliant fuels at predictable prices. Companies that delay investing in alternative fuels risk facing higher fuel costs and potentially lower [company rankings](/companies.html) on sustainability metrics. The [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) will continue to provide crucial transparency on fleet emissions and fuel consumption, allowing stakeholders to track the industry's progress towards decarbonization.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The delays in European bio-chemical projects and the success of the US e-methanol plant underscores the need for consistent and supportive policies to drive the transition to low-emission shipping fuels. While technological innovation is important, clear regulatory frameworks and financial incentives are essential to de-risk investments in alternative fuel production and ensure a stable supply of sustainable fuels for the maritime industry. Without a level playing field and robust policy support, Europe risks losing its competitive edge in the race to decarbonize shipping, potentially hindering the industry's ability to meet its emissions reduction targets.
