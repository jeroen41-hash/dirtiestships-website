The European Community Shipowners’ Associations (ECSA) has raised significant concerns about the applicability of the EU Monitoring, Reporting and Verification (MRV) regulation and the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) to the unique operational profile of the offshore shipping industry. With the EU ETS set to extend to offshore vessels above 5,000 GT from January 1, 2027, ECSA is urging the European Commission to address what it perceives as fundamental flaws in the current regulatory framework or, failing that, to temporarily suspend the inclusion of the offshore sector in the EU ETS. This could have implications for the carbon costs and [CII ratings](/cii.html) of companies operating in this segment.

## Incomplete Vessel Type Definitions

A core grievance highlighted by ECSA is the inadequacy of the current list of vessel types used to define the scope of the offshore sector. The statement released by ECSA on Safety4Sea.com explicitly states that the list is "incomplete and ambiguous." This poses a challenge for accurate emissions accounting and [CII ratings](/cii.html) calculations, as it becomes difficult to definitively classify vessels and their associated emissions under the existing regulations. The article does not provide specific examples of ambiguous classifications, but the ambiguity introduces the potential for inconsistent enforcement across different member states, undermining the level playing field the EU ETS aims to create.

## Ambiguity Surrounding "Port of Call" Definitions

Another critical issue raised by ECSA centers on the definition of a "port of call" within the context of offshore operations. The current definition, according to ECSA, does not adequately reflect the realities of offshore activities, particularly concerning the movement of cargo, crew, and Industrial and Special Personnels (ISP). The organization specifically calls out the use of UN/LOCODE in this context. In offshore operations, vessels frequently interact with offshore platforms or installations, which don't neatly align with traditional port definitions. This discrepancy can lead to uncertainties in determining which voyages fall under the scope of the EU ETS, further complicating emissions reporting and potentially distorting competition. ## Calls for Harmonization and International Alignment

ECSA emphasizes the urgent need for further clarification of key definitions impacting the offshore sector. With contracts already being negotiated that extend beyond 2030, the organization stresses the importance of clear and harmonized definitions to prevent misinterpretation, reduce the risk of uneven enforcement, and protect the competitiveness of European offshore operators. Ideally, ECSA believes these issues are best addressed at the international level through the International Maritime Organization (IMO). This preference aligns with the broader shipping industry’s general desire for global regulations to avoid regional disparities and potential competitive disadvantages. Given the EU's leadership in maritime decarbonization, it remains to be seen whether the IMO will move quickly enough to satisfy the concerns of European shipowners.

## ECSA's Proposed Solutions and the "Stop the Clock" Request

ECSA proposes a set of solutions, including defining the scope of the offshore sector by its activities, complemented by a "negative" list of exempted vessels. The organization also suggests exploring the concept of a "virtual" port within the regulatory framework to better capture the unique operational characteristics of offshore vessels. Failing the adoption of these solutions by 2026, ECSA is advocating for the Commission to temporarily suspend the inclusion of the offshore segment under the EU ETS until the Directive's revision, planned for the second half of 2026, is complete. This "stop the clock" request underscores the severity of ECSA's concerns about the potential negative impacts of the current EU ETS framework on the European offshore industry. Realistically, a suspension of the ETS is unlikely, but the threat of it could provide political leverage to force the EU to make important changes.

## What this means for shipping emissions

ECSA's statement underscores the challenges of applying broad-based emissions regulations like the EU ETS to specialized sectors like offshore shipping. The ambiguities in definitions and the mismatch between regulatory frameworks and operational realities could lead to unintended consequences, including competitive distortions and increased compliance costs for European operators. If ECSA's concerns are not adequately addressed, the effectiveness of the EU ETS in reducing emissions from the offshore sector could be compromised.
