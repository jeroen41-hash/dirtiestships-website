The shipping industry is under increasing pressure to decarbonize, and new market mechanisms are emerging to help companies meet their emissions reduction targets. The launch of Carboninsets' digital marketplace for Environmental Attribute Certificates (EACs) represents one such development, aiming to facilitate investment in and adoption of cleaner fuels and technologies across the transport sector, including shipping. But will this make a dent in the colossal carbon footprint of maritime transport, or merely provide another layer of complexity – and potential greenwashing?

## What are Environmental Attribute Certificates (EACs)?

EACs, in this context, represent verified emissions reductions achieved through specific projects or activities, such as the use of alternative fuels or the adoption of energy-efficient technologies. Think of them as tradable "proof" that a certain amount of carbon emissions has been avoided. The Carboninsets marketplace focuses on multimodal transport – air, road, marine, rail, inland shipping – and all decarbonization technologies. According to hellenicshippingnews.com, the marketplace aims to provide real-time visibility on the availability and pricing of EACs, facilitating global transactions. The key concept underpinning EACs is "book and claim," where a company can purchase EACs to offset emissions even if it's not directly using the lower-emission fuel or technology, supporting their wider adoption.

## Transparency and Verification: Key to Credibility

The success of any EAC marketplace hinges on the credibility of the certificates themselves. Carboninsets emphasizes the transparency and verification processes, stating that all EACs are externally verified by accredited auditors and issued onto the 123Carbon Registry, adhering to standards like the Smart Freight Centre’s Market Based Measures (MBM) framework and ISO 22095. This is crucial to prevent double counting and ensure that the claimed emissions reductions are real and quantifiable. The article highlights that Carboninsets has already onboarded 30+ issuers representing 500,000 EACs in the 123Carbon Registry. While the article doesn’t break down these EACs by transport mode, the inclusion of marine transport means shipping companies can potentially use the platform. The question remains how these EACs interact with, or are impacted by, shipping's evolving regulatory environment.

## EACs vs. Regulatory Compliance: EU ETS and CII

The Carboninsets marketplace doesn't directly address regulatory compliance, such as the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) for shipping or the [CII ratings](/cii.html) scheme. The EU ETS requires shipowners to surrender allowances for each tonne of CO2 emitted on voyages to, from, and within the EU. [Our company rankings](/companies.html) already show the varying levels of preparedness different companies have for these costs. EACs, on the other hand, are a voluntary mechanism for companies to offset their carbon footprint beyond regulatory requirements. A shipping company subject to the EU ETS could theoretically purchase EACs to offset emissions not covered by the ETS or to demonstrate a commitment to decarbonization beyond compliance. Similarly, while a vessel's [CII rating](/cii.html) reflects its operational efficiency, EACs could be used to offset emissions from voyages that result in a lower rating. The interplay and potential synergies (or conflicts) between these different mechanisms will be important to watch. It should be noted that the article does not clarify whether using EACs can improve a ship's CII rating.

## Potential Benefits and Challenges for Shipping

For shipping companies, the Carboninsets marketplace could offer several potential benefits. It provides access to a wider range of decarbonization options, potentially at a lower cost than directly investing in alternative fuels or technologies. It allows companies to support the development of green corridors and other innovative projects. Furthermore, it offers a way to transparently demonstrate their commitment to sustainability to customers and stakeholders. However, challenges remain. The price of EACs will fluctuate depending on supply and demand. The market needs to avoid becoming a tool for greenwashing and ensure that the emissions reductions claimed by EACs are genuinely additional and verifiable. Furthermore, while the marketplace claims to bring together supply and demand, it's unclear whether there will be enough supply of high-quality EACs to meet the demand from the shipping industry, which has a massive carbon footprint. Access to [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) could provide a baseline to compare actual emissions to offsets.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The Carboninsets marketplace is a potentially useful tool, but not a silver bullet, for shipping decarbonization. It offers an additional avenue for companies to invest in emissions reductions, but its success will depend on transparency, verification, and the availability of high-quality EACs. It's crucial that EACs are used as a complement to, rather than a substitute for, direct emissions reductions through operational efficiency improvements and the adoption of alternative fuels. The role of EACs within the broader regulatory landscape, particularly in relation to the EU ETS and CII ratings, needs to be carefully considered to ensure that they contribute to genuine decarbonization and avoid unintended consequences. More detail is needed on how this marketplace interacts with existing emissions reporting requirements.
