The fluctuating price of marine biofuels, as seen in recent movements across major bunkering hubs, underscores the complex economics behind shipping's decarbonization efforts. With the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) now fully integrated into shipping costs and the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) dictating operational efficiency, the adoption of low-carbon fuels like B30 blends is becoming a critical strategic imperative for carriers aiming to reduce their reported emissions and avoid escalating compliance penalties. The dynamics observed in ports from Rotterdam to Singapore highlight both the promise and the challenges of integrating biofuels into the global shipping fuel mix, directly impacting fleet-wide carbon footprints tracked via EU MRV data.

## Biofuel Price Volatility Across European Hubs

Recent data reveals significant shifts in biofuel bunker prices, particularly for B30 blends – a mixture of 30% biodiesel with 70% conventional very low sulphur fuel oil (VLSFO) or low sulphur marine gas oil (LSMGO). In Rotterdam, the B30-VLSFO (POMEME) blend saw a slight increase of $9/mt, even as the underlying POMEME barge price dropped sharply by $64/mt. This decoupling suggests external factors at play, crucial for understanding supply and demand dynamics in major bunkering hubs. Conversely, conventional VLSFO in Rotterdam dipped by $5/mt, widening the premium for the biofuel blend.

Other European ports also experienced notable changes. Gibraltar's B30-VLSFO (UCOME) rose by $20/mt, largely mirroring a $23/mt increase in conventional VLSFO. More dramatically, Lisbon's B30-LSMGO price surged by $76/mt, even as conventional LSMGO fell by $58/mt, leading to a substantial widening of the biofuel premium by $134/mt in a single week, reaching $210/mt. These discrepancies highlight varying regional supply chain costs and demand pressures for advanced biofuels, making strategic bunkering decisions increasingly complex for fleet operators.

## The Role of Rebates and Market Dynamics

A key factor influencing biofuel pricing in Dutch ports is the ZRE A ticket system. These certificates, generated by suppliers of low-carbon advanced biofuel blends like B30-VLSFO (POMEME), can be sold to conventional fuel suppliers, effectively subsidizing the blend price. However, the price of these ZRE A certificates has fallen from €120/mtCO2e ($138/mtCO2e) to €110/mtCO2e ($127/mtCO2e) over the week. This drop could signal an oversupplied biofuel bunker market or, alternatively, stronger direct demand for the physical biofuel itself, even without robust certificate prices. This dynamic partially explains why the sharp decrease in POMEME barge prices failed to translate into a corresponding reduction in the biofuel blend price in Rotterdam. Such national-level incentive schemes, while beneficial, introduce an additional layer of complexity to global bunker procurement strategies. For shipping companies, understanding these local market mechanisms is crucial for optimizing fuel costs and, by extension, overall EU ETS compliance expenses.

## Singapore's Biofuel Market and Global Adoption Trials

Singapore, a global bunkering giant, also saw its B30-VLSFO price increase by $30/mt, largely supported by a $31/mt rise in conventional VLSFO. The price of China UCOME cargo, a key feedstock for Singapore's B30-VLSFO, also climbed by $15/mt, further contributing to the upward price pressure. This indicates a robust demand and interconnectedness within the Asian biofuel supply chain.

Beyond pricing, the industry is witnessing concrete steps towards wider biofuel adoption. Sunoil Biodiesel's transition of its inland barge, Birjo II, to B100 (100% biofuel) for transporting renewable fuels demonstrates operational commitment. More significantly for ocean-going shipping, Japanese firm Nippon Yusen Kabushiki Kaisha (NYK Line) has initiated a year-long trial of continuous B100 use on one of its car carriers. This pilot, if successful, could provide invaluable data on long-term performance and emissions reductions. Similarly, the Global Centre for Maritime Decarbonisation (GCMD) in Singapore tested B100 derived from tallow and used cooking oil methyl ester (UCOME) on a BHP-chartered bulk carrier, the Berge Lyngor. These trials are vital for validating biofuel performance and gaining certification, ultimately impacting how such fuels contribute to [CII ratings](/cii.html) and reduce reported emissions in [company rankings](/companies.html).

## Strategic Implications for Compliance and Emissions

The evolving biofuel market has profound implications for shipping companies navigating strict environmental regulations. For vessels operating under the EU MRV regulation, using certified sustainable biofuels, even as a 30% blend (B30), can significantly reduce reported CO2 emissions. This directly improves a ship's CII rating, helping operators avoid the "D" or "E" ratings that trigger corrective action plans. Furthermore, for voyages falling under the EU ETS, verified biofuel consumption results in lower attributable emissions, thereby reducing the number of costly EU Allowances (EUAs) a company needs to surrender. The strategic adoption of biofuels, supported by robust MRV data, is therefore not just an environmental choice but a financial imperative. The recent price movements, coupled with an increasing number of B100 trials, indicate a growing momentum for biofuels as a practical bridge fuel on the path to zero-carbon shipping.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The current state of the biofuel bunker market highlights the complex interplay of feedstock availability, regional pricing dynamics, and regulatory incentives in driving shipping's decarbonization. While price volatility remains a challenge, the continued investment in trials and the structural support from schemes like the EU ETS and CII mean that biofuels will play an increasingly vital role. For operators, understanding these market nuances and leveraging certified biofuel options will be crucial for managing compliance costs, improving fleet [CII ratings](/cii.html), and transparently reducing overall emissions as reported through [emissions data](/charts.html) and the EU MRV framework. The shipping industry must remain agile, adapting bunkering strategies to maximize emissions reductions while navigating the fluctuating costs of sustainable fuels.
