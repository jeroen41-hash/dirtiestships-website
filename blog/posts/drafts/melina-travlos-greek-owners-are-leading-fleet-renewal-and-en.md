The global shipping industry finds itself at a critical juncture, navigating an increasingly complex web of decarbonisation regulations while simultaneously grappling with unprecedented geopolitical pressures. Against this backdrop, remarks from influential figures like Melina Travlos, President of the Union of Greek Shipowners (UGS), carry significant weight, particularly concerning the sector's commitment to environmental performance and its stance on regional versus global regulatory frameworks. Her recent address at Posidonia 2026, as reported by hellenicshippingnews.com, offered a potent blend of assertions regarding Greek shipping's leadership and sharp criticism of current EU climate policies, directly impacting the data we track at DirtiestShips.com.

## Greek Fleet Renewal: A Double-Edged Sword for Emissions?

Ms. Travlos proudly highlighted that Greek shipping boasts its "largest orderbook in 25 years," comprising 931 new vessels. She asserted this investment underscores their leadership in "fleet renewal and environmental performance." From an emissions perspective, the addition of new, more fuel-efficient vessels generally bodes well for improving the overall [CII ratings](/cii.html) of the fleet and reducing CO2 emissions per tonne-mile. Modern ships are designed with enhanced hydrodynamic performance, optimized engines, and often incorporate ready-for-retrofit or already installed alternative fuel technologies. Such large-scale renewal could significantly influence the Greek-owned fleet's position in future [company rankings](/companies.html) for emissions intensity.

However, the raw number of new vessels alone doesn't tell the whole story for aggregate emissions. While individual newbuilds are undoubtedly more efficient than the older ships they might replace, the article does not specify the nature of these orders – are they direct replacements, or do they represent a significant expansion of overall capacity? If new vessels primarily add to existing capacity without substantial scrapping of older, less efficient tonnage, the net effect on total CO2 emissions could be less pronounced than suggested by "environmental performance" claims. Our [emissions data](/charts.html) consistently shows that total emissions are a function of both efficiency and activity levels.

## Decarbonisation: "Unattainable Targets" and "Revenue Collection"

A core tension emerged in Ms. Travlos's comments on decarbonisation, where she starkly criticised the EU's targets as "unattainable" and distorting the IMO's 2050 objectives. This stance reflects a common sentiment among some shipowners who advocate for globally harmonised regulations over regional measures like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) for shipping. She further described the Net Zero framework as a "revenue-collection mechanism for the IMO" with funds potentially diverted from shipping-related decarbonisation efforts. This is a pointed critique of the financial mechanisms designed to drive the green transition, such as the EU ETS where shipping companies now face direct carbon costs based on their EU MRV verified emissions.

The EU ETS, implemented in January 2024, mandates that shipping companies surrender allowances for their CO2 emissions from voyages within the EU and half of those to/from EU ports. Our analysis at DirtiestShips.com clearly illustrates how these carbon costs directly impact operational expenses, incentivizing the adoption of cleaner technologies and fuels. While Ms. Travlos's "revenue collection" label might frame the ETS negatively, its primary stated goal is to internalize the cost of emissions, thereby stimulating investment in decarbonisation. Greece's abstention from the October 2025 vote on the relevant regulation further underscores the deep divisions within the industry regarding the pace and methods of achieving Net Zero.

## The Shadow Fleet and Distorted Competition

Ms. Travlos also brought attention to the "shadow fleet," arguing that sanctions without global reach "inevitably distort competition" and unfairly target Greek shipping. This phenomenon, involving vessels operating outside standard regulatory frameworks often for sanctioned cargo, poses a significant challenge to transparent emissions tracking and accountability. These ships frequently operate with older, less maintained vessels, potentially burning higher-sulphur fuels and certainly not participating in robust monitoring systems like EU MRV or striving for compliant [CII ratings](/cii.html).

The existence of such a fleet undermines global efforts to reduce shipping emissions by creating a segment of the industry that effectively escapes environmental scrutiny and compliance costs. For DirtiestShips.com, the shadow fleet represents a critical data gap. Emissions from these vessels are difficult to quantify and integrate into comprehensive global totals, making it harder to assess the true environmental footprint of the shipping sector and to hold all operators accountable for their impact. It also creates an uneven playing field for compliant operators who invest in cleaner technologies and pay for their emissions under schemes like the EU ETS.

## What this means for shipping emissions

Melina Travlos's remarks paint a complex picture for global shipping emissions. On one hand, the significant Greek orderbook of 931 new vessels offers a promise of fleet modernization and improved average efficiency, potentially lowering individual ship emissions and improving CII scores. However, the overall impact on total CO2 emissions will depend on the extent to which these new vessels replace older, dirtier ones rather than simply expanding capacity. On the other hand, the strong criticism of EU decarbonisation targets and the characterisation of carbon pricing mechanisms as "revenue collection" highlight ongoing resistance to stringent environmental regulation. Coupled with the challenge of the shadow fleet operating outside reporting and compliance, the path to decarbonisation remains fraught with both opportunity and significant hurdles for achieving verifiable, industry-wide emissions reductions.
