The Alaskan cruise industry, facing increasing scrutiny over its environmental impact, is experimenting with Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) as a potential transition fuel. The Star Princess, operated by Princess Cruises, recently became the first oceangoing vessel to be refueled with LNG in Seattle, marking a significant, albeit contested, step towards decarbonization. This move comes as cruise lines navigate the pressure to meet ambitious greenhouse gas emission reduction targets while balancing cost and logistical challenges.

## LNG Bunkering in Seattle: A Step Forward?

The refueling of the Star Princess involved a specialized LNG bunkering vessel from Seaspan Energy, highlighting the emerging infrastructure needed to support alternative fuels. According to the alaskabeacon.com article, the Port of Seattle is cautiously optimistic, viewing LNG as a "transition fuel" that can significantly reduce air pollutants like diesel particulates, sulfur oxides, and nitrogen oxides. Port of Seattle Commissioner Fred Felleman noted the visible absence of smoke during the LNG trial, a stark contrast to traditional heavy fuel oil emissions. The major cruise lines in the Alaska market, through their trade association, have committed to net-zero greenhouse gas emissions from ship operations by 2050.

While LNG offers a reduction in some pollutants, its greenhouse gas benefits are hotly debated. Critics like Seattle Cruise Control argue that a "well-to-wake" analysis of LNG's carbon footprint reveals it may be no better, or even worse, than traditional marine fuels due to methane slip during production and combustion. The article highlights the lack of planned infrastructure investment by the Port of Seattle, signaling the port's focus on alternatives beyond LNG.

## LNG vs. Other Alternatives: Cost and Availability

The article touches on the challenges of finding cost-competitive and readily available alternative fuels. While options like green methanol, renewable natural gas, ammonia, and drop-in synthetic fuels are being considered, LNG is currently the most readily available option for many shipping companies. This highlights a key tension: the desire for long-term decarbonization solutions versus the immediate pressure to reduce emissions and improve air quality in port communities.

As noted in the Wall Street Journal opinion column referenced in the alaskabeacon.com article, alternative fuels like LNG and hydrogen require significantly larger tank sizes than traditional oil, posing a challenge for cruise ships that monetize every square foot of space. This space constraint further complicates the transition to cleaner fuels.

## The Regulatory Landscape and the Cruise Industry

The shift to LNG and other alternative fuels is happening within a complex regulatory landscape. The EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) now includes shipping, placing a direct cost on CO2 emissions. Cruise lines operating in European waters will be subject to these costs, incentivizing emissions reductions. This could be a factor in Princess Cruise's decision to explore LNG in Alaskan cruises as it may give them an advantage when the ships enter EU ETS waters.

Furthermore, the [CII ratings](/cii.html) system, which assesses the carbon intensity of ships, adds another layer of pressure. Vessels with poor CII ratings may face operational restrictions, incentivizing investment in efficiency improvements and alternative fuels. LNG, while not a zero-emission fuel, can improve a ship's CII rating compared to heavy fuel oil. ## Potential Impact on Shipping Emissions

The experiment with LNG refueling in Seattle is a microcosm of the broader challenges and opportunities facing the shipping industry. While LNG may offer short-term air quality benefits and a potential improvement in CII ratings, its long-term climate impact remains uncertain. The transition to truly zero-emission fuels will require significant investment in research, infrastructure, and new vessel designs. This situation may also influence the company's environmental policies to improve [CII ratings](/cii.html).

What this means for shipping emissions: The use of LNG by cruise ships represents a modest reduction in some pollutants, but it is not a definitive solution to the industry's carbon footprint. The industry must continue to invest in and explore truly sustainable fuels and technologies to meet its long-term decarbonization goals and the more immediate pressures coming from regulations such as the EU ETS.
