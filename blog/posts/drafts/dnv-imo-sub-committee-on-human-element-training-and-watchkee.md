The IMO's recent HTW 12 session signals a crucial step towards decarbonizing shipping, focusing on seafarer training for alternative fuels. As the industry grapples with stricter emissions regulations like the EU ETS and the pressure to improve [CII ratings](/cii.html), ensuring a skilled workforce capable of handling new technologies is paramount. The meeting addressed vital gaps in the STCW Convention and Code to facilitate the safe adoption of fuels like ammonia and methanol, which are crucial to meeting IMO 2030 and 2050 targets.

## Alternative Fuel Training Takes Center Stage

A key outcome of HTW 12 was the agreement on draft interim training guidelines for seafarers working on ships using methyl/ethyl alcohol and ammonia as fuel. This is significant because the lack of standardized training has been a major obstacle to the widespread adoption of these fuels. While the [emissions data](/charts.html) clearly indicates a slow uptake of alternative fuels thus far, these guidelines provide a framework for maritime academies and shipping companies to develop comprehensive training programs. The development of specific guidelines for each fuel and technology, expected to continue until 2029, suggests a long-term commitment to supporting the safe use of new energy sources. The work builds upon generic interim guidelines approved by MSC 110 in 2025.

## Overhauling the STCW Convention and Code

Beyond alternative fuels, HTW 12 advanced a comprehensive review of the STCW Convention and Code, the globally recognized standard for seafarer training and certification. This review aims to address inconsistencies, incorporate new technologies, and ensure the competency of seafarers in a rapidly changing industry. Discussions focused on critical updates to Chapter II (Master and deck department) and Chapter III (Engine department). Key questions being considered include the extent of simulator training, the relevance of celestial navigation, and the adequacy of ECDIS training. The review also addresses emerging concerns like cultural awareness, mental health, and digital skills. An intersessional Working Group meeting in March 2026 will further progress this work.

## Addressing Fatigue and Enhancing Oversight

The meeting also tackled the persistent problem of seafarer fatigue, a contributing factor to maritime accidents and inefficiencies. HTW 12 initiated a review of the effectiveness of current regulations on hours of work and rest. A Correspondence Group will delve deeper into the causes of fatigue and the adequacy of the existing regulatory framework, reporting back to HTW 13 in 2027. Parallel to this, HTW 12 worked on enhancing the STCW oversight system and the IMO Member State Audit Scheme (IMSAS), aiming to improve the effectiveness and robustness of the "White List" of countries meeting STCW standards. This includes aligning STCW and IMSAS reporting to ensure consistent and reliable data.

## Model Training Courses Updated

The Sub-Committee validated updated versions of several model training courses, including courses on Personal Safety and Social Responsibilities, Chemical Tanker Cargo and Ballast Handling Simulator, and Oil Tanker Cargo and Ballast Handling Simulator. The updated Personal Safety course now includes a competence regarding the prevention of and response to violence and harassment, reflecting growing awareness of these issues within the maritime industry. The tanker courses identified overlaps with other model courses, indicating a need for future integration to streamline training.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The outcomes of HTW 12 are crucial for the shipping industry's efforts to reduce its carbon footprint. While the meeting didn't directly address specific CO2 reduction targets or the EU ETS carbon costs, the focus on alternative fuel training directly impacts the industry's ability to meet those targets. Companies investing in alternative fuel vessels need qualified seafarers to operate them safely and efficiently. The updated STCW Convention and Code, coupled with enhanced oversight, will ensure a more competent and adaptable workforce. Ultimately, the HTW 12 session represents a vital step towards a cleaner and more sustainable future for shipping, although the success will depend on the speed of alternative fuel adoption and the effective implementation of the training programs.
