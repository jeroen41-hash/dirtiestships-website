The global shipping industry, responsible for approximately 3% of worldwide carbon emissions and facing an urgent IMO target of net-zero by or around 2050, is in a desperate search for scalable decarbonization solutions. While alternative fuels like ammonia and methanol dominate current discussions, a new report highlights nuclear propulsion as a potent, zero-emission contender, albeit one fraught with non-technical challenges, particularly concerning port calls and public acceptance.

## The Promise of Nuclear: Zero-Emission Propulsion

The "Enabling Nuclear-Powered Feeder Ships" report, a joint development project, delves into the feasibility of nuclear-powered vessels calling at European Union ports. The environmental appeal of nuclear power is undeniable: complete elimination of direct CO2 emissions from propulsion, a significant advantage in the context of ever-tightening regulations. Core Power CEO Mikal Bøe emphasizes, "Greenhouse gas emissions from the existing shipping fleet have become unsustainable," positioning nuclear as a vital avenue to explore. With the shipping industry consuming around 350 million tonnes of fossil fuel annually, a shift to nuclear could dramatically alter its emissions profile and contribute significantly to reaching the IMO's ambitious greenhouse gas emission reduction targets by or around 2050.

## The Regulatory and Social Hurdle

Crucially, the report finds that "the principal barriers to nuclear ship port calls are not technical, but relate instead to local and international regulatory alignment, governance, risk management integration and public acceptance." This distinction is critical. While the technology for small modular reactors (SMRs) is advancing, the societal and political infrastructure required to support their deployment lags far behind. The report used a hypothetical Maersk container ship calling at the Port of Rotterdam to examine these issues, with Harbour Master René de Vries stressing the need for ports to "understand how emerging energy and shipping technologies may interact with future port operations." AP Moller-Maersk's Head of Fleet Technology, Ole Graa Jakobsen, further clarified that their involvement represents "further understanding of what would be required," not a commitment to the technology itself, underscoring the complexities involved. The IMO is revising its Safety Code for Nuclear Ships, and the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) is launching its ATLAS initiative in August, signalling international recognition of the need for structured pathways.

## Economic & Compliance Implications (CII, EU ETS)

For a data-driven platform like DirtiestShips.com, the implications for [CII ratings](/cii.html) and EU ETS carbon costs are profound. A nuclear-powered vessel, by definition, would achieve an exemplary A-grade CII rating, as its operational greenhouse gas emissions for propulsion would be zero. This would dramatically improve a company's average fleet performance, impacting their overall [company rankings](/companies.html). Furthermore, such ships would be entirely exempt from the increasingly burdensome EU ETS carbon costs on their intra-EU voyages, eliminating a significant operational expense that fossil-fueled vessels now incur. The financial incentive is enormous, directly linking the exploration of nuclear power to the economic realities faced by carriers today. However, these benefits remain theoretical until the regulatory and public acceptance hurdles are cleared.

## The Path Forward: Collaboration and Clarity

The report's conclusion serves as a roadmap: "ports should proactively prepare for their arrival," focusing on legal, safety, and operational measures, alongside cooperation with nuclear authorities. Lloyd's Register's Meg Albrecht highlights that this work "contributes to a broader understanding of the regulatory and operational considerations." Further research and transparent communication on radiation safety, reactor incidents, and security threats are essential to manage public perception "by engineering logic rather than public or promoter misconceptions." Each port will need local assessments to shape broader national, regional, and international guidance. The lack of established global standards for civilian nuclear-powered merchant ships means that today’s EU MRV data does not yet capture such vessels, underscoring how far removed this solution is from current operational realities.

### What this means for shipping emissions

Nuclear propulsion presents one of the most compelling pathways to achieve the shipping industry's net-zero targets, offering truly zero-emission operational profiles that would revolutionize [emissions data](/charts.html). However, the immediate challenge is not technological but rather a complex web of regulatory harmonization, governance frameworks, robust risk management, and — critically — gaining the "trusted confidence of port cities and their populations." While companies like Maersk are exploring the potential, wide-scale adoption hinges on collaborative, international efforts to build a universally accepted and transparent framework for nuclear-powered merchant ships. Until then, nuclear remains a powerful theoretical solution, awaiting the practical infrastructure that will allow its potential to be realized.
