The US government is intensifying its opposition to a global carbon tax on shipping, threatening international efforts to curb emissions from a sector responsible for roughly 3% of global climate pollution, according to a recent report by E&E News. This stance could significantly impact the trajectory of decarbonization within the maritime industry and raises questions about the future of global regulatory frameworks like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## US Opposition to the Net-Zero Framework

The core of the US opposition lies in its rejection of the IMO's Net-Zero Framework (NZF), a package designed to incentivize the adoption of cleaner shipping fuels and achieve net-zero emissions by 2050. The Trump administration, according to the E&E News report, is drafting a diplomatic memo warning countries against adopting any carbon tax on shipping pollution. The US "is 'strongly opposed' to a fee on ships’ climate pollution, and 'will not tolerate' the creation of a fund that uses carbon revenue for programs aimed at lowering the industry’s emissions." The memo, coordinated by the State Department and Department of Energy, aims to dissuade member nations of the IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee from supporting the NZF at its upcoming meeting.

## The Contentious Carbon Tax and Alternative Proposals

The NZF proposes a carbon-intensity standard that would become increasingly strict over time, pushing the industry away from fossil fuels and toward lower-emission alternatives. Ships failing to meet the standard would be subject to a fee, with the generated revenue earmarked for supporting the transition to greener fuels and assisting developing countries. The US views such a tax as an unacceptable economic burden, advocating instead for an "environment of energy abundance" rather than restricting energy types. This position is outlined in a State Department document reviewed by E&E News, which demands that the IMO not set an "arbitrary target" to "reverse-engineer compliance," a likely reference to the IMO’s 2023 emission reduction strategy targeting net zero by 2050.

Liberia has presented an alternative proposal to the IMO that avoids a direct carbon fee. Instead, Liberia suggests emissions reductions should be assessed against "market uptake of low-carbon marine fuels." This approach would link the fuel transition to market availability rather than proactive incentives for fuel switching. Liberia argues the NZF's current fuel standard is "overly restrictive" and reduces operators' flexibility.

## Implications for the EU ETS

The US stance also extends to regional initiatives like the EU ETS, which currently includes carbon pollution from large ships. The US demands that such initiatives be terminated. This presents a challenge for the EU, as any modification to its ETS addressing shipping would require a future IMO measure to match or exceed its stringency, according to Agathe Peigney of Transport and Environment. The inclusion of shipping in the EU ETS has already begun to impose significant carbon costs on shipping companies, a factor closely tracked on [company rankings](/companies.html) on DirtiestShips.com. This action by the US adds another layer of complexity and uncertainty to this area.

## Potential Impact on CII Ratings and Decarbonization Efforts

The IMO's [CII ratings](/cii.html) system assigns ships a rating from A to E based on their operational carbon intensity. While not directly a carbon tax, the CII aims to drive improvements in energy efficiency and emissions reductions. The US opposition to mandated targets and financial penalties may undermine the effectiveness of the CII system if countries are hesitant to enforce stricter regulations. Furthermore, the US push against penalties on LNG, a transition fuel, could slow the adoption of genuinely zero-emission technologies. Our [emissions data](/charts.html) reveals a clear connection between ship efficiency ratings and actual CO2 output, so anything that undermines this is cause for concern.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The US government's opposition to a global carbon tax on shipping poses a significant threat to international efforts to decarbonize the maritime industry. The move creates uncertainty for shipping companies already grappling with the EU ETS and striving to improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html). It also potentially undermines the IMO's ability to implement effective measures to meet its 2050 net-zero target. With the industry facing increasing pressure to reduce its environmental footprint, the lack of a unified global approach could significantly hinder progress and delay the necessary transition to cleaner fuels and technologies.
