The global shipping industry is witnessing a significant surge in the adoption of liquefied natural gas (LNG) as a transitional fuel, a pathway touted by proponents as a key decarbonization strategy. Recent data from the first half of 2026, highlighted by industry coalition SEA-LNG, indicates robust growth in LNG bunkering volumes and an expanding orderbook for LNG-fuelled vessels, even amidst geopolitical and regulatory uncertainties. This trend presents a complex picture for emissions reduction, raising critical questions for platforms like DirtiestShips.com about the true climate impact when considering factors like methane slip, EU ETS costs, and future [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## Surging Methane Pathway: Fueling Growth, Fueling Debate

The numbers are compelling: LNG bunkering volumes are experiencing a notable uptick in major ports. Singapore, a global maritime hub, reported a 48% increase in LNG bunkering in Q1 2026 compared to Q1 2025, reaching 150,000 tonnes. The combined ports of Rotterdam and Antwerp-Bruges saw a 44% increase over the same period, with Antwerp-Bruges alone recording an astonishing 214% surge. This growth underscores a strong commitment from segments of the industry to the methane pathway.

Furthermore, LNG dual-fuel vessels now represent almost 90% of the entire alternative-fuel vessel orderbook globally, a significant rise from 66% year-to-date. DNV data indicates 60 LNG dual-fuel vessels were ordered in the first five months of 2026, spanning diverse segments including containerships, car and truck carriers, oil tankers, and cruise ships. This widespread adoption reflects a perceived long-term value and investment stability, as stated by SEA-LNG Chairman Peter Keller, despite "IMO’s regulatory delays" potentially slowing other avenues.

## Biomethane's Promise: A Greener Shade of Methane?

Alongside conventional LNG, there's a burgeoning interest in liquefied biomethane (LBM). The Port of Rotterdam recorded a more than six-fold increase in LBM blend bunkering in 2025 compared to 2024, with 15,260 cbm bunkered in Q1 2026 alone—nearly matching the total for the entirety of 2025. This momentum is echoed by suppliers, with SEA-LNG member Gasum reporting biomethane growing from 0.8% to 12.3% of its maritime gas volumes between 2024 and 2025. Similarly, Titan Clean Fuels saw LBM rise from 6.5% to 19.1% of its total delivered volume in the same period.

LBM, derived from organic waste, offers a pathway to reduce lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions more significantly than fossil LNG. Its increasing uptake signals a positive, albeit still small-scale, shift towards truly renewable marine fuels. However, its current volume remains a fraction of the overall LNG bunkering, meaning its immediate impact on aggregate shipping emissions is limited.

## The Elephant in the Engine Room: Methane Slip and Emissions Reality

While SEA-LNG COO Steve Esau claims "Emissions are coming down" with the methane pathway, this assertion warrants rigorous scrutiny, especially from a platform dedicated to transparent emissions data. The EU MRV system primarily tracks CO2 emissions, providing a clear picture of fuel consumption. However, for LNG, the crucial factor often overlooked in industry-positive statements is methane slip – the release of uncombusted methane into the atmosphere from engines.

Methane is a potent greenhouse gas, with a global warming potential (GWP) significantly higher than CO2 over a 20-year timeframe (typically cited as 80-86 times greater). Even small amounts of methane slip can erode, or even negate, the CO2 reduction benefits of switching from traditional heavy fuel oil. While modern LNG engines are improving, methane slip remains a persistent challenge, influencing the true "well-to-wake" emissions profile.

For vessels navigating the EU ETS and CII regulatory landscape, this distinction is critical. Reduced CO2 emissions from LNG *will* lower immediate EU ETS costs and *may* improve a ship's [CII ratings](/cii.html) in the short term. However, as regulatory frameworks mature and potentially incorporate broader GHG assessments, including methane, the long-term viability of LNG as a "decarbonization" solution without near-zero methane slip becomes questionable. Companies investing heavily in LNG must be prepared for evolving standards that could penalize lifecycle methane emissions.

## Navigating Regulatory Winds and Investment Decisions

The article highlights the resilience of the methane pathway despite "regulatory uncertainty." This is a double-edged sword. While industry investment continues, the lack of definitive, globally harmonized regulations on methane emissions from shipping creates a risk for companies. They are betting on a pathway that currently offers some CO2 benefits but faces a significant scientific and regulatory challenge regarding methane.

The continued growth across various ship types, from containerships operated by major carriers to cruise ships, shows a strategic decision by companies to adopt LNG. This is visible in our [company rankings](/companies.html), where investment in alternative fuels is a growing differentiator. However, the path to genuinely clean shipping requires more than a simple fuel switch; it demands innovation in engine technology to eliminate methane slip and a rapid scale-up of truly zero-emission fuels.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The robust growth of LNG and LBM bunkering and the expanding orderbook for LNG-fuelled vessels undeniably represent a significant shift in the maritime fuel landscape. It signals an industry in transition, moving away from conventional fossil fuels. However, for a true and transparent assessment of shipping's decarbonization efforts, this "methane pathway" must be viewed through a comprehensive lens that fully accounts for methane slip and its potent global warming impact. While LNG offers an incremental step in CO2 reduction and can provide immediate benefits under current EU ETS and [CII ratings](/cii.html), it is not a silver bullet for achieving net-zero emissions. The ultimate success of this pathway hinges on technological advancements that eliminate methane slip and the rapid expansion of sustainable biomethane and synthetic methane production.
