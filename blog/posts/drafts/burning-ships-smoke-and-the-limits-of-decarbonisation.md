The shipping industry's laser focus on incremental emissions reductions, driven by regulations like the EU ETS, stands in stark contrast to the unquantified and unaddressed CO2 releases stemming from armed conflicts impacting maritime trade routes. While shipowners meticulously track fuel consumption to comply with emissions targets and maintain [CII ratings](/cii.html), the environmental impact of burning ships and bombed fuel depots dwarf these efforts, highlighting a significant gap in the global approach to climate accounting.

## The Decimal Place Dilemma

A recent article on Splash247.com highlights the frustrating minutiae of EU ETS compliance. The author recounts spending considerable time resolving discrepancies of mere decimal places in emissions reporting for a single vessel. This hyper-focus on granular data collection underscores the regulatory burden placed on shipping companies. It is a process designed by very capable people, but for those who must implement it, it often feels unnecessarily complicated — and for me personally, it is a nightmare (Splash247.com). Shipowners are investing in technologies like LED lighting, hull coatings, and engine optimization, all in pursuit of marginal gains in fuel efficiency. These efforts are crucial for maintaining favorable [CII ratings](/cii.html) and mitigating EU ETS carbon costs. However, the author points out that factors like voyage planning, port congestion, and cargo delays can easily negate these carefully achieved savings.

## Conflict Emissions: A Blind Spot

The author then draws a sharp contrast between this meticulous approach and the lack of accountability for emissions resulting from conflict. The Red Sea crisis and the Russia-Ukraine war have demonstrated the vulnerability of merchant shipping, with over 25 vessels hit in the recent conflict (and close to 100 if we combine the Red Sea and Russia–Ukraine situation). A burning handysize vessel, carrying approximately 1,500 tonnes of fuel, can release emissions in a matter of hours that undo months of fuel-saving initiatives. The bombing of fuel depots, as witnessed in Tehran, adds another layer to this environmental cost, with estimates suggesting millions of barrels of oil lost and nearly 1.9 million tonnes of CO2 released (Splash247.com).

## The Scale of Unaccounted Emissions

The conflict in Ukraine offers a stark illustration of the broader impact. Estimates suggest around 230 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent emissions so far — more than several European countries combined. These emissions encompass not only the burning of infrastructure and fuel but also the environmental costs associated with rebuilding efforts. Global military activity as a whole is estimated to contribute around 5.5% of total emissions. Yet, according to the source article, this significant carbon footprint largely remains outside of climate reporting frameworks. This discrepancy raises fundamental questions about the scope and effectiveness of current climate policies.

## The Cost of Compliance vs. the Cost of Conflict

The article highlights the growing financial burden associated with shipping's decarbonization efforts. With EU ETS carbon costs ranging from €60–100 per tonne of CO₂, this adds roughly $2–10 per tonne of cargo in bulk shipping. As the industry transitions to greener fuels, which can cost two to four times more than conventional fuels, freight costs are projected to increase by $10–40 per tonne (Splash247.com). These costs are ultimately passed on to consumers, contributing to inflationary pressures and disproportionately impacting developing economies. While these costs are factored into the price of goods and services, the emissions stemming from conflict remain unpriced and unaccounted for, effectively creating a double standard in climate accounting. The costs of the EU ETS are visible in our [company rankings](/companies.html) as fuel costs rise.

## A Question of Priorities

The author concludes by emphasizing that this is not an argument against decarbonization. It is a necessary process that must continue. However, the current approach overlooks the significant environmental impact of conflict-related emissions. While the shipping industry meticulously measures emissions down to the gram, millions of tonnes of CO2 released during armed conflicts are effectively ignored. This disparity underscores a fundamental imbalance in the global approach to climate change, where emissions from compliance are scrutinized while emissions from conflict are overlooked.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The current situation highlights the urgent need for a more holistic approach to climate accounting. While the shipping industry must continue its efforts to reduce emissions through technological innovation and regulatory compliance, the environmental impact of conflict cannot be ignored. This requires greater transparency and accountability in reporting emissions from military activities and armed conflicts, as well as a broader discussion about the role of international cooperation in mitigating these environmental risks. Until these issues are addressed, the shipping industry's decarbonization efforts will remain a partial solution to a much larger global challenge. The impact of geopolitical events could be something to track on our [emissions data](/charts.html) page going forward.
