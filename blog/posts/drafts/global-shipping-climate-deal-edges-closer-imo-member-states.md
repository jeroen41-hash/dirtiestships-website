The International Maritime Organization (IMO) is under increasing pressure to finalize its Net-Zero Framework (NZF) for shipping emissions, with stakeholders urging member states to resist attempts to weaken or delay its adoption. The framework, aimed at aligning the shipping sector with the goals of the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy, faces a critical test at this week's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC 84) meeting. The outcome will significantly impact the trajectory of shipping's decarbonization efforts and the [company rankings](/companies.html) that track progress.

## Key Components of the Net-Zero Framework

The IMO's NZF comprises two core elements: a global fuel standard (GFS) and a GHG pricing mechanism. The GFS mandates a gradual reduction in the greenhouse gas intensity of ship fuels, measured across their lifecycle. This would push the industry towards cleaner alternatives and incentivize the adoption of zero-emission technologies. Concurrently, a mechanism for pricing GHG emissions aims to further encourage emissions reductions by creating a financial disincentive for using polluting fuels. The revenue generated could then be used to support the energy transition, particularly in developing nations. This is crucial for ensuring a just transition, preventing small island developing states and least developed countries from being disproportionately burdened by the costs of decarbonization, as highlighted by the Clean Shipping Coalition.

## Challenges and Opportunities at MEPC 84

According to hellenicshippingnews.com, the MEPC 84 meeting presents three key opportunities to advance the decarbonization agenda. First, strengthening and enforcing the Carbon Intensity Indicator ([CII ratings](/cii.html)), the IMO's existing energy efficiency measure, is paramount. While the CII aims to improve the operational efficiency of ships, its current design has faced criticism for potentially leading to unintended consequences, such as incentivizing slow steaming without necessarily reducing overall emissions. Secondly, ensuring the NZF remains robust and free from loopholes is crucial to avoid undermining its effectiveness. Finally, establishing comprehensive Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) guidelines that incorporate sustainability criteria, biodiversity impacts, human rights, and accurate emissions factors for alternative fuels is essential. This is particularly important for methane-based fuels and biogenic fuels, where indirect land use change can significantly affect their overall GHG footprint.

## The Role of the EU and Geopolitical Instability

The article highlights the importance of the EU maintaining a united front in supporting a strong NZF. With the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) already in place for maritime emissions, the bloc's commitment to decarbonization could significantly influence the IMO's decisions. The EU ETS, which now applies to ships calling at EU ports, imposes a carbon cost on emissions, further incentivizing the adoption of cleaner technologies and fuels. The article notes that recent geopolitical instability may create the "illusion" that shipping relies on fossil fuels. However, this is a false narrative, the Clean Shipping Coalition argues, and shipping must decarbonize despite geopolitical factors.

## The Importance of Pricing GHG Emissions

John Maggs, the Clean Shipping Coalition’s Representative at the IMO, emphasizes that pricing GHG emissions is "the linchpin for enforceability" and essential for bridging the price gap between fossil fuels and zero-emission energy. A clear and predictable price signal incentivizes industry actors to invest in cleaner alternatives and provides the funds necessary to support the energy transition, ensuring that developing nations are not left behind. The [emissions data](/charts.html) on our site demonstrates how carbon pricing can shift incentives and drive innovation in the shipping sector.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The decisions made at MEPC 84 will be pivotal in shaping the future of shipping emissions. A strong and enforceable Net-Zero Framework, coupled with a robust [CII ratings](/cii.html) system and accurate LCA guidelines, is essential for achieving the IMO's GHG reduction targets. The pressure is on IMO member states to resist attempts to weaken or delay progress and to ensure a fair and equitable transition to a cleaner shipping industry. The stakes are high, with the future of our oceans and climate hanging in the balance.
