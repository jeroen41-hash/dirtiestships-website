The IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) is attempting to regain momentum on shipping emissions reductions, amidst rising environmental concerns. The MEPC 84 session, held in London, saw progress on establishing frameworks for net-zero emissions and addressing pollution risks, particularly in sensitive regions like the Strait of Hormuz. However, the path forward remains complex, requiring significant intersessional work and rebuilding of trust among member states.

## IMO Net-Zero Framework: A Long Road Ahead

A central focus of MEPC 84 was the "IMO Net-Zero Framework," designed to guide the reduction of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from ships. Nearly 100 delegations participated in discussions, highlighting the breadth of perspectives on how to achieve ambitious emissions targets. The Committee has created an intersessional Working Group to resolve concerns and foster consensus on a global measure. This group will meet twice before MEPC 85, scheduled for late November and early December 2026. These discussions are crucial, especially as they directly impact the feasibility of achieving the IMO's revised GHG reduction targets. The current discussions around mid-term measures will have a significant impact on [CII ratings](/cii.html) for ships across the global fleet. The MEPC is also planning a workshop on "chain of custody" models for fuels, emphasizing the importance of tracking and verifying emissions throughout the supply chain. This focus on transparency will be vital for ensuring the effectiveness of any emissions reduction measures.

## Emissions Control Area in the North-East Atlantic

The adoption of a new Emission Control Area (ECA) in the North-East Atlantic represents a concrete step towards reducing air pollution from ships. This ECA, set to take effect in 2028, will enforce stricter limits on nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulphur oxides (SOx), and particulate matter (PM) within the exclusive economic zones and territorial seas of several European nations, including Greenland, Iceland, the Faroes, Ireland, the UK, France, Spain, and Portugal. Within the ECA, ships will be required to use fuel with a sulphur content of no more than 0.10%. This regulation should lead to a notable decrease in harmful air pollutants, benefiting both human health and the environment. While this regional effort is commendable, its limited geographic scope highlights the need for broader, globally consistent regulations to address shipping emissions effectively. The impact of this ECA will be reflected in future EMSA MRV data, providing a quantifiable measure of its effectiveness.

## Addressing Marine Plastic Litter and Other Pollution

Beyond emissions, MEPC 84 addressed a range of other environmental concerns, including marine plastic litter and underwater radiated noise (URN). The adoption of the 2026 Strategy and Action Plan to Address Marine Plastic Litter from Ships reaffirms the IMO's commitment to zero plastic waste discharges by 2030. This plan focuses on improving port reception facilities, strengthening regulatory compliance, and increasing public awareness. In terms of URN, the Committee agreed in principle to extend the experience-building phase until the end of 2028, and to commission a study on URN emissions. While these initiatives demonstrate a holistic approach to marine environmental protection, the effectiveness of the plastic litter strategy will depend on robust implementation and enforcement. The URN study, similarly, needs to yield concrete policy recommendations to mitigate the impacts of shipping noise on marine life.

## Strait of Hormuz: Navigating Environmental Risks

The MEPC also addressed the environmental risks associated with attacks on commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz. The Committee adopted a resolution condemning these attacks and recognizing the vulnerability of the Persian Gulf region to large-scale marine pollution. The attacks, involving missiles, drones, fires, and explosions, could potentially release significant amounts of oil, hazardous substances, and residues into the marine environment. The Committee requested the Secretary-General to monitor environmental impacts and report to the next IMO Council session. While this resolution highlights the IMO's awareness of the environmental threats posed by geopolitical instability, it remains to be seen what concrete actions will be taken to mitigate these risks and hold accountable those responsible for environmental damage.

## What this means for shipping emissions

MEPC 84 demonstrated a renewed commitment to addressing shipping emissions and other environmental impacts, yet significant challenges remain. The success of the IMO Net-Zero Framework hinges on building consensus and developing effective global measures. While regional initiatives like the North-East Atlantic ECA are valuable, a coordinated global approach is essential to achieve substantial and lasting reductions in shipping emissions. The discussions regarding mid-term measures, including potential carbon pricing mechanisms, will ultimately determine the future of [company rankings](/companies.html) and the financial viability of various decarbonization strategies. The IMO's ongoing efforts to combat marine plastic litter and reduce underwater radiated noise further underscore the need for a comprehensive and proactive approach to marine environmental protection.
