The International Maritime Organization (IMO) has concluded its latest Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC 84) meeting with a renewed commitment to developing a framework for achieving net-zero greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from international shipping. This framework, if effectively implemented, will significantly impact the global shipping industry's carbon footprint and directly influence the [CII ratings](/cii.html) of individual vessels, as well as the overall emissions profile reflected in our [company rankings](/companies.html).

## IMO's Net-Zero Framework Gains Traction

The MEPC 84 meeting, held from April 27th to May 1st, saw nearly 100 delegations participate in discussions focused on adopting "mid-term measures" to reduce GHG emissions. These measures are intended to form the backbone of the IMO's Net-Zero Framework. While a consensus on the specific measures remains elusive, the committee agreed to establish an intersessional Working Group to address concerns and seek broader agreement ahead of the next MEPC session (MEPC 85) scheduled for November 30th to December 3rd. Member states will be able to submit further revisions to previously approved draft amendments. (Source: marinelink.com)

The IMO has scheduled two inter-sessional meetings (September 1-4 and November 23-27) to continue negotiations. Additionally, a one-day expert workshop will focus on "chain of custody" models for tracking fuel origin and movement. This is particularly relevant for verifying emissions reductions achieved through the use of alternative fuels and ensuring accurate reporting under the EU's Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) system, data which informs much of the analysis on DirtiestShips.com.

## Navigating Divergent Views

The path to a globally accepted regulatory framework for shipping emissions is proving challenging. According to the International Chamber of Shipping (ICS), many member states are hesitant to adopt a global framework without further adjustments. This highlights the complexities of balancing the environmental ambitions of some nations with the economic realities and developmental concerns of others.

The ICS Secretary General Thomas A Kazakos noted the need for governments to adopt a "comprehensive fit-for-purpose global framework as soon as possible to enable the industry to further accelerate its rapid transition to alternative energy sources." (Source: marinelink.com) This underscores the industry's desire for clear regulatory signals to guide investment decisions in low- and zero-emission technologies. The uncertainty surrounding the future regulatory landscape adds to the risk involved in adopting costly new technologies and fuels.

## US Delay Tactics and EU Resolve

According to T&E, the recent MEPC meeting saw pressure and delay tactics from the US, while the EU and other ambitious countries refused to abandon the Framework (Source: marinelink.com). It is not specified in the source article what these delay tactics entailed, but it highlights the divergent approaches to climate regulation between different global powers.

The EU's commitment to pushing forward with emissions reductions, particularly its implementation of the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), contrasts with the US position. The EU ETS, which now includes shipping, directly incentivizes emissions reductions by placing a carbon cost on each tonne of CO2 emitted. Ships calling at EU ports must purchase carbon credits, making fuel efficiency and the adoption of alternative fuels economically attractive. This can be seen in our [emissions data](/charts.html) and how it correlates with EU port traffic.

## Chain of Custody and Fuel Verification

The IMO's focus on "chain of custody" models for tracking fuel origin and movement is crucial. Without robust tracking and verification mechanisms, it will be difficult to accurately assess the environmental benefits of alternative fuels. This is where the EU MRV data that we use to track emissions becomes crucial. The EU MRV system, along with complementary initiatives like FuelEU Maritime, rely on detailed reporting of fuel consumption and emissions data. Ensuring the accuracy and reliability of this data is essential for the success of any global regulatory framework.

What this means for shipping emissions:

The IMO's ongoing efforts to develop a Net-Zero Framework represent a critical step towards decarbonizing the global shipping industry. While progress has been made, significant challenges remain in achieving consensus on specific measures and ensuring effective implementation. The upcoming intersessional meetings and expert workshop will be crucial in bridging the gaps between member states and establishing a robust framework that drives meaningful emissions reductions. The success of these negotiations will directly impact the future of shipping emissions and the industry's ability to meet its climate goals. Ultimately, the regulatory landscape will dictate the pace and scale of the transition to low- and zero-emission technologies and fuels.
