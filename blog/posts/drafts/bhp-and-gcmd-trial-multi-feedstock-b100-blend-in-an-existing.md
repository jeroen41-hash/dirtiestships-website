As global shipping faces escalating pressure to drastically cut its carbon footprint and navigate a complex web of environmental regulations, the development and deployment of viable alternative fuels are more critical than ever. A recent pilot project by BHP and the Global Centre for Maritime Decarbonisation (GCMD) marks a significant step, trialling a multi-feedstock B100 biofuel blend on a commercial bulk carrier. This initiative, focusing on the real-world operational integration of diverse biofuel sources, directly addresses the urgent need for scalable, lower-emissions fuel pathways, with profound implications for vessel emissions performance, regulatory compliance, and future carbon costs within the industry.

### The Promise of Diversified Biofuels

The pilot project, utilizing the BHP-chartered bulk carrier *Berge Lyngor* (owned and operated by Berge Bulk) for iron ore transport from Western Australia to China, explores the efficacy of a B100 bio-blend. Crucially, this blend consists of 50% tallow-derived biodiesel from HAMR Energy and 50% used cooking oil methyl ester (UCOME) from Mitsui & Co Energy Trading Singapore (METS). The diversification of feedstocks is a key takeaway. Current biofuel reliance heavily skews towards used cooking oil, whose supply is nearing its projected limits. By successfully integrating waste animal fats, this trial aims to demonstrate a viable expansion of the sustainable feedstock base for marine biofuels, offering shipowners and operators greater flexibility in fuel procurement based on cost, availability, and lifecycle emissions.

The trial specifically seeks to assess potential operational challenges associated with mixed-feedstock biofuels, such as corrosion from oxidation and fuel system clogging due to wax formation. Insights gathered on fuel quality, handling, traceability, and onboard vessel performance will be vital in de-risking wider adoption.

### Direct Impact on Vessel Performance and Emissions Reporting

The most striking quantifiable outcome highlighted by the pilot is the potential for a "well-to-wake" greenhouse gas emissions reduction of approximately 79 per cent per voyage compared to sailing on very low sulphur fuel oil (VLSFO) when the *Berge Lyngor* operates on this B100 bio-blend. This level of emissions abatement has immediate and substantial implications for individual vessel performance and company-wide emissions profiles.

For shipowners, achieving such a significant reduction in operational emissions directly translates to improved Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) ratings. A 79% reduction could propel a vessel from a struggling D or E rating, risking penalties and operational restrictions, to a top-tier A or B rating. Our [CII ratings tool](/cii.html) demonstrates how marginal changes in operational efficiency can shift a vessel's performance category; a near-80% reduction represents a transformative leap. Furthermore, the pilot's emphasis on robust tracing and verification of the biofuel blend's integrity is critical for accurate emissions reporting, aligning directly with the stringent data requirements of EU MRV regulations and ensuring the credibility of reported reductions. This traceability will be paramount as diverse biofuel feedstocks, each with varying lifecycle GHG footprints, become more common.

### Implications for Regulatory Compliance and Carbon Costs

While the *Berge Lyngor*'s current route (Australia to China) does not fall under the direct scope of the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), the emissions reduction demonstrated has profound implications for vessels operating within or calling at EU/EEA ports. For these voyages, a significant reduction in CO2 emissions translates directly into fewer EU Allowances (EUAs) needing to be purchased. With carbon prices fluctuating, but generally on an upward trajectory, minimizing ETS liabilities through cleaner fuels offers a powerful economic incentive.

Companies like BHP and Berge Bulk, by actively pursuing and validating such low-carbon fuel solutions, are positioning themselves ahead of the curve. Their investment in these trials will positively influence their standing in future [company rankings](/companies.html) on emissions performance, signaling a commitment to decarbonisation that extends beyond minimum compliance. The co-funding by the Maritime and Port Authority of Singapore further underscores the strategic importance of these trials for major maritime hubs looking to support sustainable bunkering solutions.

### Broader Industry Signals and Challenges

This pilot sends a strong signal to the broader shipping industry: scalable, lower-emissions fuel pathways are not only possible but are actively being developed and proven in real-world scenarios. The participation of a major charterer like BHP, alongside fuel suppliers and bunkering operations, demonstrates a cross-value chain commitment. BHP's statement on its intent to "test and trial alternative fuels that will help increase supply and send industry demand signals for further investment" highlights the critical role of demand aggregation in stimulating alternative fuel production.

However, challenges remain. The long-term availability and sustainability of *all* biofuel feedstocks, including waste animal fats, need continuous monitoring to prevent unintended consequences or competition with other sectors. Moreover, while B100 offers maximum emissions reduction, the cost premium over conventional fuels and the necessary adjustments to vessel fuel systems remain considerations for broader fleet-wide adoption. The insights gathered from this pilot on practical integration steps will be crucial for overcoming these hurdles.

### What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The BHP and GCMD biofuel pilot represents a tangible stride towards decarbonising the global shipping fleet. By demonstrating the efficacy of diversified biofuel feedstocks and achieving near-80% emissions reductions on a bulk carrier, it offers a credible, near-term solution for significantly lowering operational GHG emissions. This directly impacts vessel CII ratings, reduces exposure to EU ETS carbon costs, and sets a precedent for robust emissions reporting. While scalability and cost remain key challenges, such trials provide invaluable data and build confidence in alternative fuel pathways, paving the way for wider adoption and accelerating shipping's journey towards net-zero.
