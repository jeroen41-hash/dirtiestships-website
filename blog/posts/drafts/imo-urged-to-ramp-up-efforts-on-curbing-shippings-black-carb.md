Black carbon emissions from shipping in the Arctic are under renewed scrutiny, with environmental groups urging the IMO to accelerate regulatory action. The call to action comes after the latest MEPC meeting (MEPC 84) and highlights the urgent need to define the geographical scope of a proposed "polar fuel" measure to effectively curb these harmful emissions. Failure to act decisively will further exacerbate the disproportionate warming effect black carbon has on the rapidly melting Arctic region.

## The Urgency of Black Carbon Reduction in the Arctic

The Clean Arctic Alliance is pushing for a clearly defined geographical extent for the IMO's proposed polar fuel measure. This measure aims to reduce black carbon emissions, a potent short-lived climate pollutant. Black carbon, produced by incomplete combustion of fossil fuels, darkens snow and ice, increasing its absorption of solar radiation and accelerating melting. The Arctic is warming four times faster than the global average, and with Arctic winter sea ice tying for its lowest measured level in March, the urgency to mitigate black carbon's impact is critical. The recent European State of the Climate 2025 report further underscores this urgency, highlighting significant ice loss in Greenland and negative mass balances for European glaciers, with Iceland particularly affected.

## MEPC 84 and the Road to PPR 14

While the adoption of the North East Atlantic Emission Control Area was welcomed, the core issue of black carbon regulation requires more focused attention. A paper submitted to MEPC 84 highlighting the impact of black carbon was referred to a future IMO meeting, PPR 14 (likely in early 2027 based on the article). The crucial task now is for IMO member states, particularly Arctic nations, to collaborate and define the geographic application of the polar fuel measure before PPR 14. This collaborative effort must occur despite "geopolitical and energy turmoil," as emphasized by Dr. Sian Prior of the Clean Arctic Alliance. Without clear geographic boundaries, the effectiveness of any polar fuel regulation will be severely compromised. ## Polar Fuel Concept: A Closer Look

The "polar fuel" concept implies the use of cleaner fuels or technologies in Arctic waters. This could involve switching to distillate fuels with lower aromatic content, installing particulate filters, or adopting alternative fuels such as ammonia or hydrogen. The challenge lies in determining which ships, operating in which Arctic areas, will be subject to these requirements. Clear, enforceable rules are essential. These rules will have a direct financial impact on shipping companies operating in the region, which we track in our [company rankings](/companies.html) by assessing their EU ETS carbon costs.

## The Significance of Geographic Scope

Defining the geographic scope of the polar fuel measure is paramount. The Clean Arctic Alliance emphasizes that countries need to know exactly where these rules will apply on shipping charts. This clarity is vital for enforcement and compliance. The area defined could encompass all waters north of a certain latitude, specific Arctic shipping routes, or areas particularly sensitive to black carbon deposition. The chosen definition will directly influence the extent of emissions reductions and the impact on shipping operations. ## What this means for shipping emissions

The push for regulating black carbon emissions in the Arctic represents a crucial step towards mitigating the environmental impact of shipping. While progress has been made with the North East Atlantic Emission Control Area, a more targeted approach to black carbon is essential. The success of any polar fuel measure hinges on a clear, well-defined geographic scope, requiring collaborative effort from IMO member states. Ultimately, effective regulation of black carbon in the Arctic will not only protect this fragile environment but also contribute to broader efforts to decarbonize the shipping industry and combat climate change.
