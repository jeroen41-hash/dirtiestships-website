The latest data from the UK's Department for Energy Security and Net Zero (DESNZ), presented in the gov.uk "Greenhouse gas emissions from transport in 2024" report, reveals a concerning stagnation in the decarbonisation of the UK's transport sector, particularly when compared to other industries. While overall UK domestic emissions have plummeted by 53% since 1990, domestic transport emissions have only managed an 11% reduction over the same period, now accounting for a disproportionate 30% of total UK domestic GHG emissions. For the global shipping industry, the report offers a mixed signal, with international shipping's emissions showing a decrease from 1990 levels, yet the underlying trends and the broader regulatory environment suggest that complacency is not an option.

## UK Transport Emissions: A Stalling Progress?

The gov.uk report highlights that in 2024, domestic transport was responsible for 110.4 million tonnes of carbon dioxide equivalent (MtCO2e), a marginal 0.2% increase from 2023. While this remains 10% below pre-pandemic 2019 levels, the long-term trend is troubling. Since 2014, domestic transport has become the sector with the highest greenhouse gas emissions in the UK, a direct result of significant declines in other sectors like electricity supply. This disparity underscores the persistent challenge of decarbonising mobility, especially road transport, which constitutes 90% of domestic transport emissions, with cars and taxis alone contributing 53%. This stagnant progress within domestic transport serves as a stark reminder that while other sectors demonstrate rapid decarbonisation, transport remains a stubborn impediment to national emissions targets.

## International Shipping's Contribution: A Closer Look

Delving into the international component, the report provides specific figures for both international aviation and shipping. In 2024, international shipping emissions attributed to the UK stood at 5.0 MtCO2e. This figure represents a decrease from 6.0 MtCO2e in 2019 and a more substantial drop from 6.5 MtCO2e in 1990. In contrast, international aviation emissions soared from 15.5 MtCO2e in 1990 to 36.2 MtCO2e in 2024, indicating a very different trajectory for air travel compared to maritime.

While the reduction in international shipping's emissions from UK-attributed activities since 1990 might seem positive on the surface, it's crucial to consider the context. The report notes that international transport emissions globally have been steadily rising since 2022, approaching pre-pandemic levels after a sharp decline. This suggests that the UK-attributed figures may not fully reflect the global growth in maritime activity, which EU MRV data consistently tracks for ships calling at EU ports. Domestic shipping in the UK contributed 4.0 MtCO2e in 2024, a slight decrease from 4.5 MtCO2e in 2019 but still higher than 3.8 MtCO2e in 1990, indicating fluctuating but significant local impact.

## Beyond UK Borders: The Regulatory Landscape

The aggregate figures presented by the UK government are a vital piece of the puzzle, but they only tell part of the story for the global shipping industry. DirtiestShips.com leverages detailed, ship-specific data from [EMSA MRV](/emissions-data) to provide a granular view of emissions from vessels operating worldwide, including those calling at UK ports. While the UK operates its own MRV system, much of the global fleet that services the UK also falls under the European Union's stringent regulatory framework.

The EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) now covers shipping, imposing a direct cost on CO2 emissions from voyages touching EU ports. Similarly, the Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) ratings, calculated under IMO regulations and tracked by our [CII ratings tool](/cii.html), are increasingly impacting fleet management and charter party agreements. Vessels that frequently call at UK ports, especially those also engaged in EU trade, must adhere to these international and regional standards, which drive operational changes and investment in cleaner technologies. The UK's own plans for a potential shipping ETS will further integrate these carbon costs into operations.
