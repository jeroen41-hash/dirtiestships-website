## Hormuz Under Siege: Operational Disruption and Emissions Risks

The recent confrontation, detailed by gcaptain.com, saw U.S. forces strike Iranian military targets in response to attacks on three merchant vessels: the Marshall Islands-flagged tanker Al Rekayyat, the Saudi Arabia-flagged tanker Wedyan, and the Liberia-flagged Cyprus Prosperity. This rapid deterioration prompted IMO Secretary-General Arsenio Dominguez to issue one of his strongest warnings yet, urging flag states, shipowners, and operators to avoid transiting the Strait if crew safety cannot be assured.

Such advice, while crucial for human life, has immediate repercussions for shipping's environmental footprint. The Strait of Hormuz is a vital conduit for a significant portion of the world's seaborne oil and gas. Any avoidance or disruption necessitates rerouting, often around the Arabian Peninsula, leading to substantially longer voyages. These extended journeys translate directly into increased fuel burn and higher CO2 emissions per cargo unit, making it harder for companies to meet their environmental obligations and casting a shadow over industry-wide decarbonization efforts.

## EU ETS and Carbon Cost Implications

For vessels engaged in trade touching European ports, these operational disruptions carry a direct financial penalty through the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). As of January 2024, shipping has been integrated into the ETS, meaning vessels must surrender allowances (EUAs) for their reported emissions. Increased fuel consumption due to longer routes or slower speeds in hazardous areas directly inflates a company's total reported emissions under the EU MRV framework.

This surge in emissions translates to a greater demand for EUAs, driving up compliance costs. Companies that have carefully modeled their ETS liabilities based on optimized routes and fuel efficiency suddenly face unforeseen expenses. The revocation of sanctions waivers for Iranian oil exports further complicates the picture, potentially altering global oil trade flows and leading to more circuitous voyages for tankers sourcing crude from alternative regions. ## CII Ratings Under Pressure

The Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) is a critical measure of a ship's operational carbon efficiency. Calculated annually, it assigns a rating from A to E, with 'A' being the most efficient. Factors like voyage distance, cargo carried, and fuel consumption are central to this calculation. Geopolitical events forcing rerouting or requiring vessels to proceed at slower, safer speeds directly threaten a ship's CII rating.

A vessel forced to sail an additional hundreds or thousands of nautical miles to avoid a chokepoint, even if carrying the same amount of cargo, will consume significantly more fuel. This higher fuel consumption per unit of transport work (gram of CO2 per deadweight tonne-mile) will degrade its CII. What might have been an 'A' or 'B' rated vessel could easily slip to a 'C' or 'D' if operational patterns are consistently disrupted. Owners and operators, already grappling with strategies to improve or maintain their [CII ratings](/cii.html), now face an external variable that can rapidly undermine their efforts, pushing them towards non-compliance and potential regulatory scrutiny.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The renewed conflict in the Strait of Hormuz underscores how rapidly geopolitical instability can derail environmental progress in shipping. Increased voyage distances and altered trade routes due to security concerns directly lead to higher fuel consumption and, consequently, elevated CO2 emissions.
