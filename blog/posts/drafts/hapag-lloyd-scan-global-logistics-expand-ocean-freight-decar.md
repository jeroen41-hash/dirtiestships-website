As pressure mounts on global shippers to reduce their carbon footprint, particularly Scope 3 emissions, Scan Global Logistics (SGL) and Hapag-Lloyd are expanding their partnership to offer a "Ship Green" solution, allowing customers to claim verified emission reductions through a book-and-claim model. This initiative highlights the growing importance of demand-side action in decarbonizing the ocean freight sector, as companies face increasing scrutiny from investors, regulators, and customers.

## Book-and-Claim: A Flexible Solution for Scope 3 Emissions

The expanded partnership centers around Hapag-Lloyd's "Ship Green" solution, which utilizes a physical book-and-claim approach based on the Mass Balance principle (source: esgnews.com). This model allows Hapag-Lloyd to blend biofuel into its fuel mix, and customers can then claim the resulting emission reductions, even if their specific cargo wasn't transported using the biofuel. This is especially relevant considering the complexities of modern supply chains. The article notes that the book-and-claim approach provides more flexibility than route-specific alternatives, especially given the uneven rollout of low-carbon shipping infrastructure. While the article doesn't quantify the emission reduction potential, the appeal lies in its immediate actionability without disrupting existing logistics networks.

The approach directly addresses the challenges companies face in tackling Scope 3 emissions – indirect emissions from sources they don't own or control, such as freight and logistics. These emissions often represent a significant portion of a company's total carbon footprint, making them a key focus for reduction efforts. ## The Rise of Biofuel in Ocean Freight

Biofuel is presented as a "powerful solution" for customers, enabling emissions reduction without significant changes to their existing supply chains (source: esgnews.com). The collaboration reflects a broader trend within the shipping industry to explore and adopt alternative fuels to comply with stricter environmental regulations and meet customer demands for greener shipping options. However, it's important to acknowledge that the long-term viability and scalability of biofuel depend on sustainable sourcing and production practices. While the article mentions affordability as a benefit, the actual cost-effectiveness compared to traditional fuels and other alternative fuel options requires further analysis. Furthermore, the article lacks details on the specific type of biofuel used, its lifecycle emissions profile, and its availability across different regions, all of which are critical factors in assessing its true environmental impact.

## Regulatory and Market Drivers

The expanded partnership between SGL and Hapag-Lloyd underscores the growing pressure on companies to decarbonize their supply chains. As the article mentions, investors, regulators, and corporate buyers are demanding greater transparency and accountability regarding Scope 3 emissions (source: esgnews.com). This pressure is further amplified by regulations such as the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), which now includes maritime transport. Under the EU ETS, shipowners are required to purchase allowances for each tonne of CO2 emitted during voyages to and from European ports. This new cost burden is incentivizing companies to explore emissions reduction strategies, including the adoption of solutions like Ship Green. Understanding these costs and potential savings is becoming a key factor for companies to consider when optimizing their supply chains.

It is also important to note that from January 1, 2023, the International Maritime Organization (IMO) put into force amendments to MARPOL Annex VI which require ships to calculate their attained Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI) to measure their energy efficiency, and to establish their annual operational carbon intensity indicator (CII) and [CII rating](/cii.html).

## What this means for shipping emissions

The Hapag-Lloyd and Scan Global Logistics partnership illustrates a growing trend towards collaborative efforts to reduce emissions in ocean freight. While book-and-claim solutions using biofuel can provide a short-term pathway for companies to address Scope 3 emissions, it's crucial to recognize that they are not a silver bullet. The industry requires a comprehensive approach that includes investments in alternative fuels, energy efficiency measures, and technological innovation. Furthermore, increased transparency and standardization in emissions reporting are essential to ensure the credibility and effectiveness of decarbonization efforts.
