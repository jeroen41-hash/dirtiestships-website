Vale's recent order of two ethanol-powered Guaibamax vessels marks a significant step in the decarbonization of bulk shipping, a sector heavily scrutinized under evolving emissions regulations. The move highlights a growing interest in alternative fuels beyond LNG and methanol, and signals a potential shift in how mining giants are approaching their Scope 3 emissions within the maritime transport chain.

## Ethanol-Powered Giants: A Deep Dive

Vale's agreement with Shandong Shipping Corporation for the construction of two 325,000 dwt Guaibamax vessels powered primarily by ethanol represents a concrete investment in alternative fuel technology. According to the article from maritime-executive.com, these vessels are not solely reliant on ethanol, possessing the capability to also utilize methanol and heavy fuel oil (HFO). The design further anticipates future fuel shifts, allowing for conversion to Liquified Natural Gas (LNG) or ammonia. This multi-fuel approach hedges against the uncertainties surrounding the scalability and availability of any single alternative fuel source. Vale claims second-generation ethanol, considering the full fuel cycle, can reduce carbon emissions by around 90 percent compared with HFO. This is a substantial reduction and, if verified in operational conditions, could significantly impact the [CII ratings](/cii.html) of these vessels.

The order also includes an option for additional ships. This initial commitment, combined with the potential for expansion, suggests Vale is seriously considering ethanol as a viable long-term fuel option.

## Shandong Shipping's Role and Dual-Fuel Fleet Expansion

Shandong Shipping Corporation’s involvement is noteworthy. They will not only construct the ethanol-powered vessels but are already contracted to deliver ten dual-fuel (methanol and HFO) vessels to Vale, beginning in 2027. This suggests a broader strategic partnership centered around adopting alternative fuels in Vale's chartered fleet. Whether these vessels will achieve high [CII ratings](/cii.html) remains to be seen and will depend on the actual operational emissions, considering factors like fuel consumption, voyage optimization, and potential port delays.

## Energy Efficiency Technologies: A Holistic Approach

Beyond the alternative fuel source, these second-generation Guaibamax vessels will incorporate various energy efficiency technologies. The article mentions five rotor sails for wind-assisted propulsion, more efficient engines, hydrodynamic devices, a shaft generator, frequency inverters, and silicone paint. Vale estimates a 15 percent reduction in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions compared to the current generation of Guaibamax vessels due to this combination of technologies. This commitment aligns with Vale's "Ecoshipping program" and demonstrates a holistic approach to emissions reduction, acknowledging that alternative fuels alone may not be sufficient to meet future regulatory demands. It is important to note that wind-assisted propulsion, like rotor sails, will be considered in the [EU ETS](https://climate.ec.europa.eu/eu-action/eu-emissions-trading-system-ets_en) calculations, incentivizing investment in these technologies.

## Ethanol's Potential and Challenges

The article highlights the growing interest in ethanol as an alternative marine fuel. Compared to ammonia, ethanol is generally considered easier to handle. While Maersk is exploring ethanol aboard its methanol-fueled feeder ship *Laura Maersk*, the primary concern surrounding ethanol adoption is the availability and sustainability of its supply chain. The full lifecycle emissions benefit hinges on the production method, with "second-generation ethanol" (produced from non-food sources) offering the most significant reductions, according to Vale. Expanding ethanol production to meet maritime demand will require careful consideration of land use, water consumption, and the potential for unintended environmental consequences. This is not covered in the source article, highlighting the need for broader investigation into the topic.

## What this means for shipping emissions

Vale's investment in ethanol-powered Guaibamax vessels is a positive signal for the shipping industry. It demonstrates that major players are willing to explore alternative fuels beyond the current frontrunners of LNG and methanol. The integration of multiple energy-efficient technologies further underscores the commitment to reducing overall emissions. However, the scalability and sustainability of ethanol production remain critical challenges. As these vessels enter operation and their performance data becomes available through EMSA MRV, we will gain a clearer understanding of the true emissions reduction potential and the impact on Vale's [company rankings](/companies.html) relative to its peers. The success of this initiative could pave the way for wider adoption of ethanol as a viable fuel source within the global shipping fleet.
