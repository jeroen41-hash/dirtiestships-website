A concerning trend has emerged in maritime decarbonization: cargo owners are showing a decreased willingness to pay (WTP) a premium for low-carbon shipping. This softening of demand, revealed in a recent BCG survey, poses a significant challenge to the industry's efforts to meet increasingly stringent emissions regulations and long-term sustainability goals. The lower WTP, combined with existing cost pressures and regulatory uncertainty, means carriers need to re-evaluate their strategies for capturing value from low-carbon solutions.

## Declining Willingness to Pay: The Numbers

According to the BCG survey, the average WTP a premium for low-carbon fuels has fallen from 4.5% in 2024 to 3% in 2025, a level last seen in 2022 (hellenicshippingnews.com). This represents a significant shift in sentiment among cargo owners. Perhaps more strikingly, the segment willing to pay substantial premiums has vanished, with no respondents willing to pay above 20% compared to 3% last year. Even in traditionally strong markets like Europe, WTP has declined, from 5% to 3.5% among European cargo owners, despite strong regulation. Similarly, WTP among container cargo owners dropped from 4.5% to 3.5%.

This decline has negative implications for the financial viability of transitioning to alternative fuels and investing in energy-efficient technologies. If cargo owners are less willing to absorb the higher costs associated with cleaner shipping, it creates a barrier to widespread adoption, potentially slowing down the industry's progress towards emissions reduction targets. This also has a knock on effect for [CII ratings](/cii.html). Shippers unable to invest in technologies or fuels to improve their rating, may be stuck with higher costs or limited access to ports.

## Regulatory Uncertainty and its Impact

The survey highlights regulatory uncertainty as a major impediment to investment in low-carbon solutions. A substantial 60% of carriers see regulatory uncertainty as a barrier to optimizing fleet, investment, and commercial decisions (hellenicshippingnews.com). The recent delay in the potential adoption of the IMO's Net-Zero Framework, for example, only serves to exacerbate the issue.

This uncertainty stems from the lack of a unified global approach to carbon pricing and enforcement. While regional initiatives like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) are in place, the absence of a global carbon price and varying enforcement mechanisms create an uneven playing field. This makes it difficult for carriers to justify investments in low-carbon technologies, particularly given the long-term nature of these investments and the volatility of the carbon markets. It is important to track [company rankings](/companies.html) to determine who is investing regardless.

The EU ETS, which now includes maritime shipping, adds a carbon cost to voyages within and to/from the EU. As DirtiestShips.com covers, the cost of EU ETS allowances can significantly impact voyage economics, particularly for older, less efficient vessels with lower [CII ratings](/cii.html). Regulatory uncertainty further complicates the picture, as carriers grapple with the evolving landscape of emissions regulations and the potential for future policy changes.

## The Economic Mismatch

The core issue identified in the article is an "economic mismatch." Decarbonizing shipping requires significant upfront investment in long-term assets like dual-fuel vessels and energy-efficient technologies. However, the demand for low-carbon shipping and the willingness to pay a premium remain volatile and discretionary (hellenicshippingnews.com).

This creates a situation where the necessary capital investment may not materialize, particularly if the cost gap between conventional fuels and low-carbon alternatives remains wide. The survey reveals that 40% of cargo owners cite high costs relative to benefits as the primary barrier to adopting low-carbon fuels (hellenicshippingnews.com). The prospects for achieving cost parity with conventional fuels remain uncertain, hindering the industry's ability to transition to a more sustainable future.

## Long-Term Optimism and Pockets of Demand

Despite the challenges, the article expresses cautious optimism about the long-term value of low-carbon shipping. The key is for carriers to understand the drivers of price sensitivity and identify opportunities where demand remains strong. This could involve targeting specific segments or customer types who are more willing to pay a premium for sustainability.

Furthermore, carriers need to focus on cost-effective solutions and technologies that can improve energy efficiency and reduce emissions without significantly increasing costs. This includes optimizing vessel operations, adopting proven energy-saving devices, and exploring innovative solutions like wind-assisted propulsion and AI-powered voyage optimization. Data from [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) can help carriers identify areas for improvement and track their progress over time.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The decline in cargo owners' willingness to pay for low-carbon shipping is a concerning trend that could slow down the industry's decarbonization efforts. Regulatory uncertainty and the economic mismatch between long-term investment and volatile demand further complicate the situation. However, by understanding the drivers of price sensitivity, focusing on cost-effective solutions, and identifying pockets of demand, carriers can still make the business case for green solutions and contribute to a more sustainable future for the shipping industry. Continued monitoring of emissions data and regulatory developments is essential to navigate this evolving landscape.
