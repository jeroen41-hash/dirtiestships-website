The latest market review from SEA-LNG highlights a critical divergence in the shipping industry's decarbonization efforts: while liquefied natural gas (LNG) continues to dominate new alternative fuel orders, its long-term viability as a low-emission pathway remains hotly contested. Amid escalating regulatory pressure, including the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and tightening Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) ratings, understanding the true emissions impact of this dominant fuel choice is more crucial than ever for global shipping and its carbon footprint.

## LNG Dominance in New Orders: A Closer Look

According to SEA-LNG's 2026 Mid-Year Market Review, LNG dual-fuel vessels accounted for an overwhelming 73 newbuild orders in the first half of 2026, representing nearly 90% of all alternatively fueled vessels tracked by DNV. This significant preference underscores the industry's continued reliance on LNG as an "available" solution. The report notes that these orders are concentrated in liner shipping, particularly among containerships and pure car and truck carriers (PCTCs), with LNG accounting for 58% of container ship tonnage ordered compared to 36% for conventionally fueled vessels.

Fuel demand is also on the rise, with global LNG bunker volumes averaging approximately 770,000 cubic meters per month between January and May 2026 – a 13% increase from the previous year. This growth, driven by more LNG-powered vessels entering service and favorable fuel economics, indicates a deepening commitment to the LNG pathway across a significant segment of the global fleet. However, as we continuously track [company rankings](/companies.html) based on EU MRV data, it's essential to remember that fossil LNG still contributes to CO2 emissions and, crucially, methane slip.

## The Biomethane Bridge and Expanding Infrastructure

A key argument from LNG proponents, including SEA-LNG, is the potential for a seamless transition to lower-carbon fuels like liquefied biomethane (LBM) or bio-LNG using existing infrastructure and engines. The review points to a growing supply of biomethane, with European production capacity reaching 8.2 billion cubic meters annually by Q2 2026, a 17% increase year-on-year. The number of operational biogas plants also saw a significant jump from 1,678 to 1,975 in the same period.

Accompanying this, the bunkering infrastructure for LNG is expanding rapidly. Globally, 67 LNG bunker vessels are now in operation, with another 42 on order. The network of ports offering LNG bunkering has surged from just 10 a decade ago to 222 today. This infrastructure development is critical for facilitating wider adoption, and theoretically, for scaling the use of bio-LNG. Should bio-LNG become widely available and demonstrably reduce lifecycle emissions, it could significantly impact vessels' [CII ratings](/cii.html) by offering a lower carbon intensity fuel.

## Navigating Regulatory Realities and Carbon Costs

While SEA-LNG's report frames LNG as "the practical and realistic solution for shipping decarbonization," the industry's regulatory bodies and environmental critics remain divided. The debate centers on the long-term role of LNG, specifically concerning methane slip – the release of unburnt methane, a potent greenhouse gas, into the atmosphere – and upstream emissions during production and transport. While LNG can offer significant reductions in CO2 emissions compared to conventional heavy fuel oil, its overall greenhouse gas (GHG) footprint, when accounting for methane slip, is a point of contention.

For ships operating within the EU, the choice of fuel has immediate financial implications under the EU ETS. Even LNG-fueled vessels are subject to carbon costs for their CO2 emissions, and as the scheme expands and allowance prices potentially rise, the economic advantages of fossil LNG become less clear. Furthermore, the evolving framework for [CII ratings](/cii.html) could eventually incorporate a broader scope of GHG emissions beyond just CO2, potentially penalizing fuels with high methane slip. Accurate data from sources like EMSA MRV is vital for evaluating these complex emissions profiles and ensuring true decarbonization.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The continued dominance of LNG in newbuild orders signals the industry's preference for an immediately implementable solution. This trend, bolstered by expanding biomethane supply and bunkering infrastructure, suggests that a significant portion of the global fleet will rely on methane-based fuels for the foreseeable future. However, this pathway carries inherent risks regarding overall GHG emissions due to persistent concerns about methane slip.

For DirtiestShips.com, this means continued vigilance. We will closely monitor the actual emissions performance of these LNG-fueled vessels through available data, assessing their contributions to both CO2 and equivalent GHG emissions. The transition to bio-LNG offers a clearer path to emissions reduction, but its scalability and verifiable emissions savings remain key questions. As the industry grapples with the escalating costs of carbon under the [EU ETS](/news.html) and stricter [CII ratings](/cii.html), the true effectiveness of LNG as a decarbonization strategy will be judged not just by order books, but by real-world emissions data accessible through platforms like our [emissions data](/charts.html). The debate over LNG's climate credentials will undoubtedly continue to shape future maritime policy and investment decisions.
