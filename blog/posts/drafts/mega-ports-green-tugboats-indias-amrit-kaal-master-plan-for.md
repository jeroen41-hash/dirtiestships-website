India's ambitious $135 billion maritime investment plan, unveiled at India Maritime Week 2025, signals a potential shake-up in global shipping and a challenge to existing carbon emission trends. With a focus on port development, green initiatives, and a massive fleet expansion, this initiative could have significant implications for the industry's efforts to meet increasingly stringent environmental regulations, particularly concerning the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the [CII ratings](/cii.html) framework.

## Massive Investment, Green Ambitions

India Maritime Week 2025 showcased a surge in maritime investment commitments, jumping 41% from the Global Maritime India Summit (GMIS) in 2023, reaching a staggering $135 billion (12 lakh crore rupees) according to xpert.digital. This investment is strategically allocated across key areas: 30% to port development and modernization, 20% to sustainability and green initiatives, 20% to shipping and shipbuilding, 20% to port-led industrialization, and 10% to trade and knowledge partnerships. The 20% dedicated to "sustainability and green initiatives" could represent a meaningful step towards decarbonization, potentially impacting the [CII ratings](/cii.html) of ships calling at Indian ports in the future. However, further details on the specific green technologies and their projected emission reduction impacts are needed to fully assess the impact.

## Rebuilding a National Fleet

A significant portion of the investment, approximately $27 billion (20% of the total), is earmarked for shipping and shipbuilding. This move is driven by India's heavy reliance on foreign shipping companies, which currently transport about 95% of the country's foreign trade. This dependence results in an estimated $70-75 billion annual outflow of capital. The Shipping Corporation of India (SCI), plans to expand its fleet from 55 to 216 vessels by 2047, representing an investment of around $11.3 billion. Successfully executing this fleet expansion with modern, fuel-efficient vessels could significantly improve the average [CII ratings](/cii.html) of ships registered in India and reduce overall emissions. However, the type of vessels, their fuel consumption, and the adoption of alternative fuels will ultimately determine the environmental impact.

## Key Players and Regional Impact

The investment is driven by a mix of international logistics companies, Indian public sector undertakings (PSUs), and regional port authorities. DP World committed $5 billion, while APM Terminals (Maersk) pledged $2 billion for expanding the Pipavav port. Indian PSUs placed shipbuilding contracts worth approximately $5.4 billion. These investments will likely impact port efficiency and reduce turnaround times. However, the article lacks detailed information about how these port investments will support green initiatives, such as providing shore power or alternative bunkering fuels. These facilities are critical to decarbonizing port operations and reducing emissions from ships while at berth.

## Implications for EU ETS and Carbon Costs

While the India Maritime Week announcements signal a potential shift in global shipping dynamics, the immediate impact on the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) is uncertain. The EU ETS applies to 50% of emissions from voyages either arriving at or departing from an EU port, regardless of the ship's flag. If India's fleet expansion focuses on routes outside the EU, the direct impact on EU ETS costs might be limited. However, increased efficiency and the adoption of lower-emission fuels could indirectly reduce the carbon footprint of voyages connecting to EU ports, thereby lowering potential ETS costs for ship operators. The absence of detailed information about the types of vessels being built, their fuel efficiency, and their operational profiles makes it difficult to make a more precise assessment. ## What this means for shipping emissions

India's ambitious maritime plan presents a potential opportunity to reshape global shipping and contribute to emission reduction efforts. The extent of its impact will depend on the types of vessels added to the fleet, the adoption of alternative fuels and green technologies, and the integration of sustainable practices across the entire maritime sector. While the investment commitments are substantial, further transparency regarding the environmental performance of these projects is crucial to determine their true contribution to decarbonizing the shipping industry.
