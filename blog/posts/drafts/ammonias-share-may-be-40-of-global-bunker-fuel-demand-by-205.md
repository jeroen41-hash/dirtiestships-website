While the shipping industry grapples with the immense challenge of decarbonization, ammonia is emerging as a potential frontrunner in the race for alternative fuels. According to Amogy CEO Seonghoon Woo, ammonia could account for as much as 40% of global bunker fuel demand by 2050, a bold prediction that, if realized, would dramatically reshape the maritime energy landscape. This projection, based on advancements in ammonia-to-power technology and increasingly stringent environmental regulations, warrants close examination given its potential impact on shipowners, fuel suppliers, and the overall trajectory of shipping emissions.

## Amogy's Ammonia Vision: Cracking the Code

Amogy's confidence stems from its ammonia-to-power system, which "cracks" liquid ammonia into hydrogen and nitrogen, subsequently using the hydrogen to generate power via fuel cells or hydrogen engines (hellenicshippingnews.com). The company has already demonstrated this technology in various applications, including a tugboat in 2024, and aims to replace conventional four-stroke diesel marine engines with its ammonia modules by 2028-29, with main engine replacements following around 2033-2035 (hellenicshippingnews.com). This timeline suggests a phased approach to ammonia adoption, starting with auxiliary power and eventually scaling to primary propulsion.

Woo also highlights the scalability of ammonia production as a key advantage. Current global production stands at approximately 200 million metric tons per year, requiring an additional 200-300 million metric tons annually to meet potential marine fuel demand (hellenicshippingnews.com). While this represents a significant increase, the CEO notes that countries like India and China are already increasing production of green ammonia, derived from renewable hydrogen, and that green ammonia production costs are falling.

## Regulatory Tailwinds and Economic Considerations

The adoption of ammonia as a marine fuel is being driven by increasingly stringent environmental regulations. The EU ETS, FuelEU Maritime, and the IMO's Net Zero Framework all favor cleaner fuels, providing incentives for shipowners to explore alternatives to fossil fuels (hellenicshippingnews.com). The EU ETS, in particular, places a carbon cost on emissions, making zero-carbon fuels like green ammonia economically more attractive. Our site's [CII ratings](/cii.html) tool can help shipowners assess their current emissions performance and plan for compliance with these regulations.

Woo also argues that ammonia will ultimately be the most economical fuel choice, citing the narrowing price differential between green ammonia and fossil fuels, as well as geopolitical factors that are driving up conventional fuel costs (hellenicshippingnews.com). However, current price comparisons show that green ammonia remains significantly more expensive than traditional bunker fuels. According to Platts' data, green ammonia in Far East Asia was priced at $1,855.27/mt in March, compared to $913.32/mt for VLSFO in Singapore (hellenicshippingnews.com). The price gap is a major hurdle, and the actual narrowing of the price difference will depend on a great many factors.

## Safety and Infrastructure Challenges

Despite the potential benefits, the adoption of ammonia faces significant challenges. Safety concerns surrounding ammonia handling are often cited as a barrier, although Woo contends that these concerns are overhyped, pointing out that many cargo ships already carry ammonia (hellenicshippingnews.com). While ammonia is a common cargo, using it as a fuel requires new bunkering standards, crew training, and engine safety requirements. The recent successful factory acceptance test of an ammonia-burning engine by Everllence and HHI-EMD is a positive step, but widespread adoption will require significant investment in safety infrastructure and training.

Furthermore, the availability of green ammonia at major bunkering ports is currently limited. The partnership between NYK Line, Golden Island, and Yara Clean Ammonia to explore low-carbon ammonia supply in Singapore is a promising development (hellenicshippingnews.com), but a global network of green ammonia bunkering facilities is needed to support widespread adoption. Singapore's own projections estimate that ammonia could make up about 4% of its bunker fuel demand by 2035 (hellenicshippingnews.com), indicating that it will be several years before the infrastructure is ready.

## Power Generation as a Stepping Stone

Amogy is also pursuing ammonia-based stationary power applications, viewing them as a stepping stone to maritime adoption. The company has formed a joint venture with GS Engineering & Construction to deploy an ammonia-based power generation system of up to 40 MW in South Korea (hellenicshippingnews.com). This project, slated to ramp up from 1 MW in 2026-27 to 40 MW by 2029-30, will provide valuable experience in ammonia handling and power generation, potentially accelerating the development of maritime applications. This ties in well with governments in various countries that offer subsidies or carbon taxes to emitters.

What this means for shipping emissions:

Amogy's vision of ammonia capturing 40% of the bunker fuel market by 2050 is ambitious, but not impossible. While significant challenges remain in terms of cost, safety, and infrastructure, the regulatory pressure to decarbonize shipping, combined with technological advancements in ammonia-to-power systems, creates a pathway for wider adoption. Whether ammonia reaches a 40% market share will depend on continued innovation, supportive policies, and, crucially, a significant reduction in the cost of green ammonia. If these hurdles can be overcome, ammonia could play a major role in reducing shipping emissions and helping the industry meet its decarbonization goals.
