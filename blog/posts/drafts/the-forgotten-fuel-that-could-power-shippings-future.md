The search for shipping's "missing piece" in the energy transition is intensifying as the clock ticks on decarbonization targets. As LNG faces methane slip issues, green methanol grapples with scarcity, and ammonia struggles with toxicity, a once-shelved nuclear fuel – thorium – is re-emerging as a contender, buoyed by technological advancements and supply chain considerations.

## Thorium: Abundant Fuel, Reduced Waste

Unlike uranium, thorium isn't directly fissile, but when bombarded with neutrons inside a reactor, it transmutes into fissile uranium-233. This "breeding" process forms the basis of the thorium fuel cycle. The potential benefits are significant: according to a gcaptain.com article, thorium is three to four times more abundant than uranium in the Earth's crust, and is found in countries such as India, Brazil, Australia, the United States, Norway and Canada, sidestepping reliance on Russia which dominates supply of high-assay low-enriched uranium for conventional advanced reactors. Furthermore, advanced thorium cycles are projected to reduce long-lived radioactive waste by over 80% compared to conventional uranium. This reduction in waste could ease long-term storage concerns – a crucial factor for public acceptance.

## Molten Salt Reactors: A Technological Key

The key to unlocking thorium's potential lies in molten salt reactors (MSRs). First demonstrated in the 1960s, MSRs dissolve fuel in liquid fluoride salt, operating at atmospheric pressure. This eliminates the high-pressure risks associated with conventional water-cooled plants. If overheating occurs, the fuel salt expands, automatically slowing the reaction. In emergencies, a frozen salt plug melts, draining the fuel into a containment tank and halting the reaction. This inherent safety could address public concerns about nuclear power in shipping, which have historically hindered its adoption. The high operating temperatures also enable hydrogen production, ammonia synthesis, and desalination, adding to their versatility. MSRs can also be refueled online, extracting fission products during operation, which could improve efficiency.

## China's Thorium Breakthrough

China is leading the way in thorium reactor development. In October 2024, the Shanghai Institute of Applied Physics added thorium fuel to the operational 2-megawatt thermal TMSR-LF1 in Gansu Province, the first such loading in any molten salt reactor in the world, according to gcaptain.com. In November 2025, the institute announced that TMSR-LF1 had achieved full conversion of thorium to uranium-233, confirming the thorium breeding cycle. They also demonstrated continuous refueling without shutdown in April 2025. China plans a 100-megawatt thermal demonstration reactor by 2035, followed by a facility producing both electricity and hydrogen. This state-backed program highlights the strategic importance China places on thorium as an energy source.

## Copenhagen Atomics: Containerized Reactors

While China pursues a national strategy, Copenhagen Atomics is developing a commercially viable MSR designed to fit inside a standard 40-foot shipping container. These 100-megawatt thermal reactors are intended for assembly-line manufacture. The article doesn't provide detail, but mass production and modular design could significantly reduce the capital costs associated with traditional nuclear power, a major barrier to its adoption in shipping. The company is targeting a rate of at least one unit per week, but the path to mass production is complex and will require further technological advances.

## A Fuel Model Revolution?

The article suggests that nuclear fuel cycles could disrupt the traditional commodity model of marine fuels. Instead of being consumed, used fuel can be reprocessed to extract usable isotopes, creating a long-term energy asset. The most likely commercial model for shipping is reactor and fuel systems leased from specialist providers under energy-as-a-service arrangements, with operators purchasing power output while fuel remains within a managed supply chain, its residual value recovered across successive cycles. However, this model requires robust international regulations and safeguards to prevent proliferation and ensure safe handling of nuclear materials. This also needs to be reflected in emissions accounting and regulatory frameworks like the EU ETS so that nuclear powered ships can be accurately represented in [company rankings](/companies.html). Nuclear powered vessels, as zero emission at the smokestack, would receive superior [CII ratings](/cii.html) as well.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

Thorium-powered molten salt reactors offer a potentially transformative solution for decarbonizing shipping, addressing fuel supply concerns and reducing long-lived radioactive waste. While challenges remain, including regulatory hurdles and public acceptance, the technological advancements and the potential for a disruptive fuel model warrant serious consideration. Widespread adoption could significantly reduce the shipping industry's reliance on fossil fuels and help achieve its emissions reduction targets. The European Maritime Safety Agency's MRV data shows the scale of emissions, and alternative fuels are crucial to bringing that down. Thorium may be an option that could make an impact in the long term.
