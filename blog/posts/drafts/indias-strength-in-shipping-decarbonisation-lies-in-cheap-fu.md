The global shipping industry faces an unprecedented challenge: rapidly decarbonizing its operations amidst escalating regulatory pressure and the looming specter of significant carbon costs. As the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) expands its reach and stringent Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) ratings become critical for fleet viability, the search for economically viable, low-carbon fuels intensifies. New analysis from the Global Maritime Forum (GMF), as reported by Hellenic Shipping News, highlights India's pivotal potential in this transition, positioning the nation as a key global supplier of cheap, green hydrogen-based fuels that could fundamentally alter the cost and feasibility of maritime decarbonization.

## India's Ascendant Role in Green Fuel Production

GMF's research underscores India's unique strengths in the nascent green fuel market. The nation boasts some of the lowest production costs globally for green hydrogen and green ammonia, a direct result of its abundant renewable energy resources. This cost advantage is crucial for a shipping industry grappling with high fuel expenses and the premium traditionally associated with sustainable alternatives. India's strategic geographic location along major international trade routes further amplifies its appeal, attracting interest from international shipping companies looking to secure future bunkering solutions. Domestically, supportive policies like the Green Hydrogen Mission and the upcoming National Green Shipping Policy are actively fostering an environment conducive to large-scale green fuel production.

## The Demand-Side Challenge and Regulatory Imperative

Despite India's impressive production potential, GMF points to "uncertain demand" as a significant barrier for producers. Current international demand for green hydrogen and green ammonia is primarily concentrated in regions with robust regulatory frameworks, such as Japan, Singapore, South Korea, and Europe. These areas are actively supporting green fuel offtakes through policy incentives and mandates, offering a clear signal to producers.

This highlights a critical disconnect: while production capacity is growing in promising regions like India, a strong, unified global demand signal is still nascent. GMF advocates for a global regulatory scheme under the IMO’s Net-Zero Framework to provide the necessary clarity and encouragement for widespread adoption. Aligning India's production with international standards, such as the EU’s Renewable Fuel of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO) rules, alongside domestic regulations, is crucial for India to maximize its export competitiveness. However, India's heavy reliance on coal in its centralized grid presents a challenge, as it may complicate meeting the stringent renewable electricity criteria of international standards like EU RFNBO, potentially limiting market access for some producers.

## Bolstering Domestic Demand and Infrastructure

While the international demand signal matures, India is proactively cultivating domestic appetite for green fuels. Government policy ambitions and the decarbonization demands of key industries like steel and fertilizer production are driving this internal growth. Significantly, the designation of three Indian ports as green hydrogen hubs is a strategic move, directly contributing to the build-out of bunkering capacity and stimulating domestic demand for green maritime fuels. This forward-looking approach ensures that infrastructure development aligns with production capabilities. Furthermore, India's established base of a trained technical workforce is cited as another key strength, essential for the sophisticated operations required in green fuel production and handling.

## What this means for shipping emissions

India’s potential as a low-cost producer of green hydrogen and ammonia offers a beacon of hope for an industry under immense pressure to decarbonize. Unlocking this potential hinges on strong, harmonized global regulatory frameworks that provide clear demand signals to producers and certainty to consumers. Should these regulatory and infrastructure challenges be overcome, India could dramatically accelerate shipping's transition away from fossil fuels, driving down CO2 emissions, improving fleet-wide CII scores, and mitigating the financial burden of carbon pricing for shipping companies worldwide.
