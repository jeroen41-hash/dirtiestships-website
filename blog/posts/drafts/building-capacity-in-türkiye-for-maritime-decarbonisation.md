Türkiye Prepares for Maritime Decarbonization Amidst EU Pressure

As Türkiye aims for net-zero emissions by 2053, the nation's maritime sector faces increasing pressure to decarbonize, driven significantly by its close trade ties with the European Union and the cascading effect of EU regulations like the EU ETS and FuelEU Maritime. A recent project launch highlights Türkiye's proactive steps to build capacity and explore effective policy instruments to reduce greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions while maintaining its competitiveness in the global maritime market.

Capacity Building Program Launched

Ricardo, a global engineering and environmental consultancy, has been selected by the European Bank for Reconstruction and Development (EBRD) to deliver the “Türkiye Regulatory Setup, Market Mechanisms and Capacity Building for Maritime” program on behalf of Türkiye's Ministry of Transport and Infrastructure. This initiative, part of the broader ‘Maritime Decarbonisation and Green Shipping Programme for Türkiye’ co-funded by the European Union and EBRD, aims to advance the green agenda in Türkiye's maritime sector. The program officially launched on September 11, 2025, in Ankara, with subsequent awareness and training events in Istanbul (ricardo.com). These events are designed to engage stakeholders and share knowledge, with further training sessions planned for the first half of 2026. Complementing these industry sessions are study tours for government partners to learn from European maritime decarbonization policies.

Policy Design and Incentives

A core element of the Ricardo-led project involves providing expert input to the Ministry of Transport and Infrastructure on several key areas. These include the design of a monitoring, reporting, and verification (MRV) system for the maritime sector in Türkiye, assessing and evaluating options for maritime decarbonization policies and incentives, training industry stakeholders and building capacity within government entities, and assessing the potential for green shipping corridors from Türkiye while identifying potential for low / zero GHG fuel production to service the future needs of the sector (ricardo.com). The project's timeline is aligned with major milestones in EU maritime decarbonization policy, including the implementation of FuelEU Maritime and the expansion of the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) to cover 100% of shipping emissions within its scope. Given these changes, understanding the potential impact on Turkish-flagged vessels and port calls is critical. Financial Support and Stakeholder Engagement

The EBRD and EU funding aims to incentivize decarbonization by combining EU grants, EBRD loans, and co-financial investments. This financial support will unlock opportunities for alternative fuel infrastructure, onshore power supply in ports, and the construction of new low-GHG, energy/fuel-efficient vessels (ricardo.com). Crucially, the project seeks to convene diverse stakeholders across Türkiye's maritime value chain, including shipowners, shipbuilders, port operators, and bunkering service providers. This collaboration is intended to raise awareness of the benefits of decarbonizing the Turkish maritime sector.

EU ETS and Implications for Turkish Shipping

As the article points out, the full scope of the EU ETS now includes maritime emissions. This has significant implications for Turkish shipping companies operating to and from EU ports. Under the EU ETS, shipping companies are required to surrender allowances (EUAs) for each tonne of CO2 emitted on voyages within the EU, as well as 50% of the emissions on voyages entering or leaving the EU. The cost of these allowances can be substantial, directly impacting operational costs. For vessels receiving poor [CII ratings](/cii.html), the additional ETS costs will likely exacerbate existing disadvantages. The program in Türkiye aims to address these challenges by helping companies understand their obligations and explore strategies to reduce emissions and minimize their carbon costs. Understanding the [company rankings](/companies.html) of competitors in terms of emissions performance is now essential for Turkish operators.

What this means for shipping emissions

Türkiye's proactive engagement with maritime decarbonization, driven by EU regulations and supported by international expertise, signals a commitment to reducing shipping emissions. The capacity-building program, coupled with potential investments in green infrastructure and alternative fuels, could significantly impact the carbon footprint of Turkish-flagged vessels and port operations. This initiative is a crucial step towards aligning Türkiye's maritime sector with global climate objectives, although the ultimate success will depend on the effective implementation of policies and the active participation of all stakeholders.
