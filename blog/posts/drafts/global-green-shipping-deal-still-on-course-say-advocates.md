The global shipping industry, responsible for approximately 3% of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions, faces a pivotal moment as the UN's International Maritime Organization (IMO) navigates towards adopting a Net-Zero Framework aimed at decarbonizing the sector. While challenges and opposition remain, recent developments suggest the framework is still viable, setting the stage for potential adoption in November and impacting how ships will be rated under [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## IMO Talks Show Framework Still Alive

Despite attempts to derail it, the Net-Zero Framework for shipping emissions reduction remains a topic of active discussion at the IMO, according to dialogue.earth. The plan proposes standards for limiting vessel emissions and potentially requiring shipowners to pay for emissions exceeding defined limits, with revenue earmarked for mitigating climate change impacts in developing states. While the article suggests that slightly more than half of the voting countries support the framework, the article also notes that a two-thirds majority is needed for adoption.

The article highlights that the US and Saudi Arabia have historically opposed the framework, raising concerns about increased shipping costs and impacts on developing states. Last year, the US even threatened economic punishments against proponents, contributing to a delay in discussions. However, last week's IMO meeting saw reduced pressure from the US, potentially increasing the chances of adoption, but the US delegate still believes the framework would cause "serious economic harm".

## Contentious Points and Alternative Proposals

The Net-Zero Framework aims to achieve net-zero emissions from the shipping industry "by or around" 2050. This target aligns with broader global climate goals, but the specific mechanisms for achieving it remain controversial. The core issue revolves around how to fairly distribute the financial burden of decarbonization and ensure a just transition for all nations, particularly developing states that rely heavily on shipping.

An alternative, more "pragmatic" framework has been proposed by Liberia, Argentina, and Panama. This proposal would scrap the net-zero fund and prioritize "commercially viable" and "affordable" fuels. Critics argue that this would weaken the IMO's decarbonization commitments and delay progress. Implementing such a plan would also require a new draft and further discussion, potentially pushing back adoption by six months or more.

## Impact on EU ETS and Carbon Costs

Although the article does not directly mention the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), the IMO's Net-Zero Framework is closely linked to regional regulations like the EU ETS. The EU ETS now includes shipping, imposing a carbon cost on voyages within the EU and 50% of the emissions from voyages entering or leaving the EU. If the IMO framework involves financial penalties for exceeding emissions limits, this could potentially overlap or interact with the existing EU ETS carbon costs.

The [EU MRV data](/charts.html) provides insights into ship emissions within the EU's jurisdiction. If the IMO framework encourages the adoption of alternative fuels, the use of these fuels could be reflected in lower carbon emissions reported in the EU MRV data over time. Monitoring these trends will be crucial for assessing the combined impact of both the IMO framework and the EU ETS on shipping emissions.

## Geopolitical Tensions and Developing Nations

The article highlights the geopolitical tensions surrounding shipping emissions regulations. Vanuatu's delegate criticized the US for complaining about higher shipping costs, given the fuel and shipping crisis allegedly triggered by US actions in the Middle East. Developing island nations like Vanuatu are particularly vulnerable to the impacts of climate change and face disproportionate economic consequences from fuel price volatility.

The success of the Net-Zero Framework hinges on addressing these concerns and ensuring that the transition to a low-carbon shipping industry is equitable and does not exacerbate existing inequalities. If the net-zero fund is not implemented and the EU ETS is not properly accounted for, this could impact [company rankings](/companies.html) on this site and the way shipping companies prioritize their emissions reductions.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The fate of the IMO's Net-Zero Framework remains uncertain, but its continued viability signals a sustained global effort to decarbonize shipping. The outcome of the November meeting will be crucial in determining the future trajectory of shipping emissions and the role of international regulation. Even if the framework is adopted, its effectiveness will depend on the specific details and the willingness of member states to enforce the agreed-upon measures. The development of the Net-Zero Framework could be significant to future [CII ratings](/cii.html), as it is intended to reward low-emission ships.
