The successful retrofit of a 20,000 TEU containership to run on methanol represents a significant milestone in the decarbonization of global shipping, offering a tangible pathway for operators to improve their environmental performance and navigate increasingly stringent regulations. This first-of-its-kind conversion, completed in September 2025, provides invaluable lessons on transitioning the existing fleet to lower-emission fuels, directly impacting vessels' Carbon Intensity Indicator (CII) ratings and their exposure to rising EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) costs.

### A Megaship's Leap Towards Decarbonization

COSCO Shipping's initiative to convert its 7-year-old mega containership, the *COSCO Shipping Libra*, marks a pivotal moment for a sector under intense pressure to reduce its carbon footprint. The project, executed at CHI-Shanghai's yard, transformed the nearly 400-meter-long vessel to a dual-fuel methanol operation. This involved converting the MAN B&W 11S90 main engine and two of its four Wärtsilä auxiliary engines, alongside installing new methanol fuel tanks exceeding 15,000 cubic meters. The undertaking, involving close coordination between COSCO Shipping, CHI Shanghai, MARIC, DNV, and multiple suppliers, demonstrates the technical feasibility of deep retrofits on a monumental scale. This strategic investment by a shipping giant like COSCO offers a compelling case study for other carriers contemplating similar fleet adjustments to meet impending decarbonization targets.

### Navigating the Complexities of Retrofit: Lessons for the Fleet

Retrofitting an existing vessel, particularly one of this size and complexity, presented unique challenges compared to newbuilds. Integrating entirely new fuel systems into existing structural arrangements required innovative engineering and meticulous coordination among numerous parties. Yan Hao, Commercial Director at CHI-Shanghai, highlighted the unprecedented level of collaboration required. Initial challenges included extensive software commissioning for the first vessel, leading to a yard stay of 108 days for the *COSCO Shipping Libra*. However, the subsequent conversion of its sister vessel, *COSCO Shipping Gemini*, benefited directly from these lessons, reducing yard time to a more efficient 90 days. This rapid improvement underscores the potential for scalability and efficiency gains as expertise grows across the industry. A notable trade-off for COSCO was the loss of 456 TEU capacity on the *Libra* to accommodate the new methanol tanks and systems, a factor carriers must weigh in their operational planning.

### Direct Impact on CII Ratings and EU ETS Costs

For vessels like the *COSCO Shipping Libra*, converting to methanol offers a clear path to improving operational carbon intensity. While the ultimate emissions reduction depends on the origin of the methanol (green vs. grey), the dual-fuel capability inherently reduces reliance on conventional fuels, enhancing the vessel's environmental profile. This directly translates to improved [CII ratings](/cii.html), potentially moving a vessel from a 'D' or 'E' rating towards a 'C' or better. Such improvements are critical as poor CII ratings can lead to mandatory corrective action plans.

Furthermore, any reduction in CO2 emissions achieved through methanol use directly lowers a vessel's exposure to EU ETS carbon costs for voyages touching EU ports. With carbon prices a growing financial burden, proactive decarbonization through retrofits becomes an economic imperative, reducing operational expenses and enhancing competitiveness. The shift will be quantifiable in future EMSA MRV data submissions for these vessels, providing transparent evidence of their reduced emissions footprint, a key metric tracked on DirtiestShips.com's [emissions data](/charts.html) and impacting [company rankings](/companies.html).

### Paving the Way for Fleet-Wide Transformation

The successful completion of the world's first methanol mega containership conversion is more than just a single project; it is a proof-of-concept for the wider maritime industry. It demonstrates that decarbonization strategies are not solely dependent on newbuilds but can effectively address the significant proportion of the existing global fleet that falls within the "middle-aged" category. This project's insights, from phased construction strategies to the critical role of early class engagement with DNV, are crucial for future methanol and LNG retrofit projects. As the shipping industry seeks to meet ambitious IMO and regional targets, the ability to transform existing tonnage at industrial scale is paramount.

### What this means for shipping emissions

The conversion of the *COSCO Shipping Libra* is a powerful signal that large-scale retrofits are a viable, albeit complex, pathway to decarbonization. It underlines the potential for significant emissions reductions across the global fleet, improving vessels' regulatory compliance (CII) and reducing operational costs (EU ETS). While challenges like fuel availability and capacity trade-offs remain, the lessons learned from this pioneering project will accelerate the adoption of alternative fuels across the industry, contributing to a measurable reduction in global shipping emissions visible in our [emissions data](/charts.html). This pragmatic approach to decarbonization is essential for the industry to achieve its net-zero ambitions.
