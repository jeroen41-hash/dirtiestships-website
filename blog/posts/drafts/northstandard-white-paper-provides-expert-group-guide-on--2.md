Biofuels are rapidly emerging as a key, albeit complex, tool for shipping companies navigating increasingly stringent emissions regulations. A new white paper from NorthStandard, in collaboration with World Fuel and Veritas Petroleum Services (VPS), highlights both the opportunities and potential pitfalls of wider biofuel adoption in the maritime sector. This comes as the industry faces mounting pressure to decarbonize under regulations like FuelEU Maritime and the IMO's GHG reduction targets.

## Biofuel Adoption Soars Amid Regulatory Pressure

The NorthStandard white paper underscores the accelerating uptake of biofuels. VPS biofuel sample testing has reportedly increased twelvefold between 2021 and 2025, exceeding 1 million metric tons. This surge is largely driven by the appeal of biofuels as a "drop-in" solution, allowing ship operators to reduce emissions without significant modifications to existing vessel infrastructure. These biofuels can help owners improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html).

FuelEU Maritime, for instance, mandates a 2% reduction in greenhouse gas (GHG) intensity by 2025, escalating to 6% by 2030, 14.5% by 2035, and a substantial 80% by 2050. Similarly, the IMO targets a 65% reduction in well-to-wake GHG intensity by 2040 (compared to 2008). The white paper suggests that biofuel blends with 30%-40% "bio" components may be necessary to meet the IMO's 2040 target, whereas blends of 5-10% are already quite popular.

## The Quality Conundrum: Navigating Biofuel Blends

While biofuels offer a pathway to lower emissions, the NorthStandard report cautions against complacency. Fuel quality emerges as a critical concern. The paper highlights instances where fuels marketed as B100 (100% biofuel) were actually biofuel blends with unpredictable performance. Furthermore, the type of "bio" component – whether Fatty Acid Methyl Esters (FAME), Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil (HVO), or alternative substances like Cashew Nut Shell Liquid (CNSL) – significantly impacts fuel properties and performance.

The experts quoted in the paper lean toward HVO as the ideal biofuel, due to its similarities to traditional marine diesel fuels. However, availability and cost constraints limit its widespread use. As alternative feedstocks like CNSL are increasingly used to bridge the supply gap, vigilance regarding fuel quality becomes paramount. The experts emphasize the need for robust development protocols and oversight from industry bodies like ISO and CIMAC.

## Sustainability and Certification: Ensuring Compliance

The white paper emphasizes the importance of sustainability certification and chain-of-custody documentation to verify regulatory compliance. No biofuel should be transferred until the Union Database (UDB) transaction ID has been provided, and this ID should be digitally linked to the Bunker Delivery Note. This is critical to track the origins and carbon intensity of the fuels being used, and to ensure accurate reporting under regulations like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). Without such tracking, companies risk facing penalties and reputational damage. It remains to be seen whether the EU MRV will adopt these recommendations.

This focus on certification and traceability aligns with the broader push for transparency and accountability in the maritime sector, especially as [EU ETS carbon costs](/charts.html) are becoming very expensive. ## Managing Risks and Claims

NorthStandard reports already receiving biofuel quality-related claims and inquiries regarding proof of sustainability (PoS) documentation. This underscores the importance of thorough due diligence when sourcing and using biofuels. The paper stresses the need for careful fuel management and testing, particularly with novel feedstocks like CNSL. While improvements in refining and distillation have mitigated some quality issues associated with CNSL, buyers must remain vigilant about potential problems such as engine wear, corrosion, fouling, clogging, sludging, instability, and poor ignition.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The NorthStandard white paper serves as a timely reminder that while biofuels offer a promising pathway to decarbonization, they are not without their challenges. As the industry embraces biofuels to meet increasingly stringent emissions regulations and lower its carbon footprint, a focus on fuel quality, sustainability certification, and risk management is essential. Neglecting these aspects could lead to operational problems, regulatory non-compliance, and ultimately, undermine the industry's decarbonization efforts.
