The future of global shipping's decarbonization pathway is once again uncertain as a growing number of countries push for revisions to the IMO's Net-Zero Framework (NZF), designed to place a cost on lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions (GHG) from marine energy use starting in 2028. This potential overhaul, driven by a coalition of nations with varying concerns, threatens to derail the progress towards the IMO's ambitious emissions reduction targets and could significantly impact the industry's transition to cleaner fuels.

## A Rocky Road to Regulation

The NZF, initially approved in April 2025 by a 63-16 vote, aims to establish a mechanism for pricing GHG emissions throughout the lifecycle of marine fuels. However, opposition successfully stalled its adoption in October 2025, securing a 57-49 vote to delay negotiations by a year, according to hellenicshippingnews.com. This delay, coupled with the growing calls for revisions, casts a shadow over the framework's future and raises concerns about the IMO's ability to deliver on its commitment to reduce shipping GHG emissions by 20%-30% by 2030 and 70%-80% by 2040 from 2008 levels. This is against a backdrop of increasing pressure from the EU, which is already implementing the [EU ETS carbon costs](/news.html) for maritime.

## Diverging National Interests

The article highlights the diverse positions of IMO member states, revealing a complex web of competing interests. The US, under the Trump administration, has emerged as a fierce opponent, advocating for the framework's complete abandonment and rejecting any financial costs, fuel restrictions, or regional carbon rules. A coalition including Algeria, Bahrain, Iraq, Kuwait, Russia, Saudi Arabia, Somalia, and the UAE echoes these concerns, calling for a technology-neutral, consensus-based alternative without centrally set carbon prices. Meanwhile, Argentina, Liberia, and Panama oppose the establishment of an IMO Net Zero Fund, suggesting a market-responsive fuel intensity system without revenue collection and advocating for differentiated decarbonization requirements. Even Japan, which previously supported the framework, now suggests options to remove compulsory fund payments or revise decarbonization targets to enhance flexibility. On the other hand, Brazil, Fiji, Kiribati, Nauru, Palau, Solomon Islands, Tuvalu, Vanuatu, Mexico and Solomon Islands generally support the framework in its current form or with only minor changes. Greece, a major EU shipowning nation, dissents from the EU consensus, seeking revisions to achieve broader agreement among IMO members.

## Industry Weighs In

The potential implications of these regulatory uncertainties extend to the industry itself. While many shipping professionals acknowledge the importance of GHG pricing to incentivize the transition to low-carbon bunkers, the competitiveness of conventional fuels has been affected by factors such as the Hormuz shipping crisis. Platts data shows the monthly average delivered bunker price for very low sulfur fuel oil in Singapore last month was $913.32/metric ton, versus $855.41/mt of VLSFO equivalent for LNG and significantly higher prices for biobunkers and sustainable methanol. A lack of regulatory certainty could stifle investment in these more expensive but greener alternatives, potentially impacting [company emissions rankings](/companies.html) in the future.

Leading industry associations, including BIMCO, Cruise Lines International Association, Intercargo, Interferry, International Chamber of Shipping, Intertanko, and the World Shipping Council, have issued a joint statement reaffirming their commitment to the IMO as the global regulator for decarbonizing shipping, without directly commenting on whether the NZF should be revised. This stance suggests a desire for a unified global approach, but the ongoing debate highlights the challenges in achieving a consensus that satisfies all stakeholders. The impact on [CII ratings](/cii.html) remains to be seen, as delayed or weakened regulations could slow the adoption of technologies and practices needed to improve vessel efficiency and reduce emissions.

## The Risk of Inaction

Environmental groups and think tanks warn that reopening negotiations could undermine the IMO's progress towards its decarbonization goals. A UCL Energy Institute study suggests that tailoring the framework to appease opposing member states might not effectively promote the uptake of sustainable marine fuels and could encounter resistance from countries with higher decarbonization ambitions. Dominik Schneiter, CEO of WinGD, emphasizes that a GHG pricing element is crucial for achieving climate goals and that without robust incentives, the industry risks further endangering the climate.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The push for revisions to the IMO's Net-Zero Framework introduces significant uncertainty into the future of shipping emissions regulation. The ongoing debate highlights the conflicting priorities of different nations and the challenges in forging a global consensus. Any weakening or delay of the framework could slow down the transition to cleaner fuels, hinder the achievement of emissions reduction targets, and ultimately impact the shipping industry's contribution to global climate change. Monitoring the outcomes of the upcoming MEPC 84 session and tracking the evolving positions of key stakeholders will be crucial for understanding the trajectory of shipping decarbonization.
