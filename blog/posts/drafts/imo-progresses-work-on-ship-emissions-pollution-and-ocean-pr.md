The IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) concluded its 84th session with a renewed commitment to tackling shipping emissions, setting the stage for crucial negotiations on the IMO Net-Zero Framework. While concrete measures remain elusive, the session signaled a willingness to bridge divides and advance the development of mid-term measures aimed at reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from ships. The stakes are high, as the shipping industry accounts for a significant portion of global CO2 emissions, and regulatory certainty is crucial for incentivizing investment in cleaner technologies and fuels.

## IMO Net-Zero Framework: A Path Forward?

The central focus of MEPC 84 was the IMO Net-Zero Framework, a collection of "mid-term measures" designed to curb GHG emissions from ships. According to the [news feed](/news.html), nearly 100 delegations presented views on the framework. The committee has agreed to establish an intersessional Working Group to resolve concerns and push for broader agreement on a global measure before MEPC 85. Two inter-sessional meetings are planned, along with an expert workshop on "chain of custody" models to track fuel origin and ensure proper emissions tracing. The aim is to prepare the ground for a substantive discussion at MEPC 85 in late 2026. Given the urgent need to decarbonize shipping, the success of these meetings will be critical. The focus on fuel chain of custody is important, as it relates directly to calculating accurate emissions factors and ensuring that the benefits of alternative fuels are properly accounted for. ## North-East Atlantic ECA: A Regional Step Towards Cleaner Seas

Beyond the global framework, MEPC 84 also took concrete action on regional air pollution. The adoption of a new Emission Control Area (ECA) in the North-East Atlantic will introduce stricter limits on nitrogen oxides (NOx), sulphur oxides (SOx), and particulate matter (PM). The ECA, encompassing the exclusive economic zones and territorial seas of several European nations, will require ships to use fuel with a sulphur content of no more than 0.10% from September 2028. This measure directly addresses the harmful effects of these pollutants, reducing risks of respiratory and cardiovascular diseases, as well as improving visibility and reducing acidification. While the ECA primarily targets air pollution, it also has implications for GHG emissions, as the use of lower-sulphur fuels can sometimes lead to marginal reductions in CO2 intensity.

## Marine Plastic Litter and Other Environmental Measures

The IMO also addressed other pressing environmental concerns. MEPC 84 adopted the 2026 Strategy and Action Plan to Address Marine Plastic Litter from Ships, reaffirming the goal of zero plastic waste discharges by 2030. This strategy aims to improve port reception facilities, strengthen regulatory compliance, and expand public awareness and training. Additionally, the Committee agreed to develop a mandatory code governing the maritime transport of plastic pellets. These measures reflect a growing awareness of the broader environmental footprint of the shipping industry, beyond just CO2 emissions.

## Underwater Radiated Noise: A Growing Concern

Underwater radiated noise (URN) from ships is also gaining attention as a potential threat to marine life. MEPC 84 agreed in principle to extend the experience-building phase for URN by two years, aiming to address barriers to implementing IMO's Revised URN Guidelines. The Committee also considered commissioning an IMO study on URN emissions to inform future policy measures. While still in the early stages of development, these efforts signal a growing recognition of the need to mitigate the impact of shipping on marine ecosystems.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The MEPC 84 session highlights both the progress and the challenges in decarbonizing the shipping industry. The commitment to the IMO Net-Zero Framework signals a willingness to move forward, but significant hurdles remain in reaching a consensus on concrete measures. Meanwhile, regional initiatives like the North-East Atlantic ECA demonstrate that tangible progress is possible.
