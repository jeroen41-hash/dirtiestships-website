The shipping industry's path to decarbonization hinges on international agreement, and a revised proposal submitted to the IMO by Liberia, Argentina, and Panama aims to break the deadlock that stalled progress last year. This proposal, set for discussion at the upcoming MEPC 84th session, seeks to forge a consensus on emissions reduction targets, a crucial step given the lack of enforcement mechanisms in international maritime law.

## Ditching the Stick, Embracing the Carrot

The key difference in this revised approach is the removal of the proposed IMO fund, which would have levied fines on non-compliant vessels. This element proved highly contentious, with many member states viewing it as punitive and potentially disadvantaging smaller shipowners operating tramp vessels. The concern was that it would effectively be a tax on shipping without necessarily directing funds towards decarbonization in a transparent and equitable manner. Instead, the new proposal focuses on incentives to encourage the uptake of cleaner fuels and the adoption of emissions-reducing technologies. This shift towards a "carrot" approach, rather than a "stick," could be the key to unlocking broader support.

## Flexibility and Technological Agnosticism

The Liberian proposal emphasizes setting targets on a five-year cycle, aligning them with the availability of alternative fuels and technological advancements. This flexible approach recognizes the evolving nature of decarbonization solutions and allows shipowners to plan fleet upgrades and transitions in a cost-effective manner. Critically, the proposal does not limit emissions reductions to specific fuel types. It embraces a "well-to-wake" perspective, incentivizing any method that reduces overall emissions, including technologies like onboard carbon capture systems and wind-assisted propulsion. This technological agnosticism is a notable departure from approaches that solely focus on fuel switching, potentially opening the door for a wider range of innovative solutions. This is important as our [CII ratings](/cii.html) tool demonstrates the impact of various technologies of ship efficiency and emissions reduction.

## The United States and the Gulf States: A Shifting Landscape?

The article highlights that the United States' position remains uncertain, with the US representative, Marco Sylvester, potentially still opposed. However, the backing of the proposal by Saudi Arabia and other Gulf countries, previously opponents of the initial framework, signals a potential shift in the negotiating landscape. This broader support is crucial for achieving the necessary consensus at the IMO.

## Implications for EU ETS and Company Rankings

While the IMO proposal operates on a global scale, its success will indirectly impact regional regulations like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). A clear global framework for decarbonization could harmonize efforts and reduce the risk of conflicting regulations. Conversely, continued deadlock at the IMO could strengthen the case for more stringent regional measures. This proposal directly impacts our [company rankings](/companies.html) because the more rapidly shipping companies transition to clean methods, the higher they will rank. This is an area we follow closely on our [emissions data](/charts.html) pages. As ships are added to the EU ETS scheme, the economic incentives for emissions reductions grow, and the pressure to comply with targets rises.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The Liberian proposal represents a pragmatic attempt to bridge the divide within the IMO and establish a framework for reducing shipping emissions. By shifting away from punitive measures and embracing incentives, flexibility, and technological agnosticism, it aims to create a pathway that is both ambitious and achievable. While the success of the proposal hinges on securing broader support, particularly from key players like the United States, the backing of countries like Saudi Arabia suggests a growing recognition of the need for action. Ultimately, the outcome of the MEPC 84th session will have a significant impact on the pace and direction of the shipping industry's decarbonization journey. If adopted, this would provide a framework for evaluating companies by the [CII ratings](/cii.html) and give companies clear paths to reduce carbon costs, which in turn will affect their [company rankings](/companies.html).
