The race to decarbonize shipping is intensifying, with e-methane emerging as a potential contender to replace fossil fuels. A recent agreement between Titan Clean Fuels and TURN2X highlights this shift, promising e-methane supply to the maritime industry from 2028 and setting the stage for significant shifts in compliance with upcoming EU regulations and [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## Titan Clean Fuels Secures e-Methane Supply

According to marinelink.com, Titan Clean Fuels, which currently operates seven bunker vessels capable of delivering to 52 ports, has signed an off-take agreement with TURN2X. TURN2X's production plant in Miajadas, Spain, will convert renewable energy and biogenic CO₂ into ISCC-certified e-methane. This e-methane will be transported to major European ports where Titan will bunker it to ship operators. The agreement itself doesn't specify volumes or contract duration, limiting our ability to assess its immediate impact on overall emissions reduction.

## E-Methane's Potential for Emissions Reduction

The key appeal of e-methane lies in its potential for substantial GHG emission reductions. The article states that e-methane can achieve net-zero GHG emissions on a well-to-wake basis. Furthermore, it offers up to 95% reduction of NOx and achieves virtually zero SOx and particulate matter emissions. These figures position e-methane as a far cleaner alternative to traditional fossil fuels, addressing concerns about air pollution alongside carbon emissions. However, the article also notes that the exact emission reductions depend on the equipment and engine technology used, which indicates that the full benefits of e-methane may not be realized across all vessels and operational profiles.

## FuelEU Maritime and RFNBO Incentives

The most compelling aspect of this development lies in its implications for compliance with FuelEU Maritime regulations. The article emphasizes that ships using e-methane will be eligible for FuelEU Maritime overcompliance and the commercial benefits of banking and pooling. Titan plans to establish a FuelEU pool, allowing companies with compliance surpluses or deficits to trade credits.

Furthermore, TURN2X’s e-methane is a certified renewable fuel of non-biological origin (RFNBO), which provides a significant advantage under FuelEU Maritime. From 2025 to 2033, shipping companies can essentially double-count the impact of RFNBO when calculating FuelEU performance. A minimum of 2% RFNBO usage may also be mandated from 2034 if RFNBO adoption remains below 1% of reported maritime energy use by 2031. This double-counting incentive is designed to accelerate the adoption of RFNBOs like e-methane. While this initiative is encouraging, the article doesn't quantify the costs, creating uncertainty about real-world impact. The cost differential between e-methane and conventional fuels, along with the scale of production, will ultimately determine its competitiveness and uptake. Tracking these trends with data from [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) will be essential.

## Infrastructure and Energy Security

The marinelink.com article highlights a crucial advantage of e-methane: its compatibility with existing LNG infrastructure. E-methane can be used in existing LNG bunkering equipment, terminals, and LNG dual-fuel vessels with minimal modification. This is significant because there are approximately 222 ports worldwide with LNG bunkering infrastructure and around 850 LNG-fueled vessels in operation, with an additional 642 on order. This drop-in compatibility drastically reduces the barriers to adoption compared to other alternative fuels that require completely new infrastructure.

The article also points out that scaling up the production of green fuels like e-methane can directly support energy security, given the EU's commitment to phasing out Russian LNG. This adds a geopolitical dimension to the push for alternative fuels, making it a strategic imperative as well as an environmental one.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The Titan Clean Fuels and TURN2X agreement represents a step towards decarbonizing shipping, specifically addressing both carbon and air pollutant emissions. The incentives provided by FuelEU Maritime, particularly the double-counting of RFNBOs, could drive early adoption of e-methane. However, the scale of production, the cost competitiveness of e-methane, and the actual well-to-wake emissions reductions achieved in real-world operations will determine its ultimate success. The impact on company emission profiles and [company rankings](/companies.html) remains to be seen, but the direction is clear: shipping companies need to embrace alternative fuels to navigate the increasingly stringent regulatory landscape.
