The approval of safety guidelines for ammonia as a fuel at the International Maritime Organization’s (IMO) Maritime Safety Committee (MSC 111) marks a critical, albeit early, step towards decarbonizing the shipping industry. While this doesn't directly impact immediate CO2 emissions data, the guidelines pave the way for the adoption of alternative fuels, which are crucial for long-term reductions tracked on platforms like DirtiestShips.com. The implications for ship owners facing increasing pressure from the EU ETS and scrutiny via [CII ratings](/cii.html) are significant.

## IMO Approves Ammonia Fuel Safety Guidelines

The approved guidelines, focused on safety protocols for ammonia as fuel on IGC (International Gas Carrier) ships, are the result of collaboration between the Belgian Federal Public Service Mobility and Transport (FPS Mobility), EXMAR, and Lloyd’s Register. This is a welcome change, given many regulations are written in ivory towers with a limited understanding of the realities on the water. This collaboration aims to bridge the gap between theoretical frameworks and practical application. According to the article, the FPS Mobility took the lead in drafting the guidelines, drawing on EXMAR’s experience in midsize gas carrier shipbuilding.

## Real-World Experience Shaping Regulations

Nathalie Deleuze, FPS Mobility’s Maritime Expert in Alternative fuels, emphasized the importance of aligning regulatory frameworks with the "realities on the ground." Celine Audenaerdt, KBRV’s Head of Environmental and Technical Affairs, highlighted the success of combining "strong in-house competence and hands-on experience" to develop robust and practical safety guidelines. Kristof Coppé, Director Fleet Operations & Technical Business Development at EXMAR, noted that these guidelines draw from "more than four decades of operational experience."

This emphasis on practical experience is particularly important in the context of alternative fuels. While the potential benefits of ammonia in terms of emissions reduction are well-documented, the risks associated with its use are also significant. Ammonia is toxic and corrosive, requiring stringent safety measures to prevent accidents and environmental damage.

## From Concept to Reality: Ammonia's Emerging Role

Liam Blackmore, Global Head of Technology Adoption and Integration at Lloyd’s Register, stated that the approval of these guidelines "provides a clear signal that ammonia is moving from concept to a fuel with an emerging international safety framework." He further noted that it "demonstrates how close collaboration between industry, flag administrations and technical organisations can accelerate the safe adoption of new fuels at scale."

This emerging framework will be essential for encouraging investment in ammonia-fueled vessels and infrastructure. As highlighted on DirtiestShips.com, the costs associated with complying with regulations like the EU ETS are increasing financial pressure on ship operators to adopt lower-emission technologies. The availability of clear safety guidelines reduces the perceived risk associated with using ammonia and makes it a more attractive option for companies looking to reduce their carbon footprint and improve their [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## Implications for EU ETS and CII Ratings

The move towards alternative fuels like ammonia is intrinsically linked to the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS). As the cost of carbon credits rises, ship owners are increasingly incentivized to reduce their emissions. Ammonia, if produced using renewable energy sources (green ammonia), has the potential to significantly lower a vessel's carbon footprint, potentially improving a company's position in our [company rankings](/companies.html).

Furthermore, vessels operating on ammonia will likely receive higher [CII ratings](/cii.html) compared to those burning traditional fossil fuels. This is because the CII rating system takes into account a vessel's energy efficiency and carbon intensity. Ships with lower carbon intensity will receive better ratings, making them more attractive to charterers and investors.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The approval of safety guidelines for ammonia as fuel is a crucial step towards the wider adoption of alternative fuels in the shipping industry. While the immediate impact on emissions data may be minimal, these guidelines lay the groundwork for a transition to lower-carbon fuels, which is essential for meeting the IMO's greenhouse gas reduction targets. As more ammonia-fueled vessels enter operation, we can expect to see a gradual decrease in the overall carbon intensity of the global fleet. The journey towards decarbonization is a long one, but this approval signifies progress.
