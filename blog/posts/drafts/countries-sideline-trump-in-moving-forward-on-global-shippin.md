The global shipping industry, a significant contributor to greenhouse gas emissions, is inching closer to a carbon pricing mechanism despite resistance from some nations. Recent talks at the International Maritime Organization (IMO) signal progress in finalizing technical elements of the Net-Zero Framework (NZF), a potentially groundbreaking agreement to put a carbon price on maritime emissions, a first for any global industry sector. This move could have profound implications for shipping companies, their [CII ratings](/cii.html), and their exposure to the EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS).

## Broad Support for Carbon Pricing Emerges

According to hellenicshippingnews.com, 62 countries actively participated in the IMO's Intersessional Working Group on Greenhouse Gas Emissions (ISWG-GHG-21), focusing on the design and implementation of the NZF. A significant majority (39) specifically engaged in discussions regarding the collection and distribution of revenues generated by the carbon price. This broad engagement, encompassing nations from Africa, Europe, Asia, South America, and the Pacific, suggests a growing consensus on the need to address shipping's carbon footprint, and counteracts claims the IMO is a 'house divided'.

The potential revenue from the carbon price is substantial, estimated at $10-12 billion per year. The discussions on how to distribute these funds are particularly important for developing nations, some of whom are advocating for these funds to support a just transition in Africa, enabling the continent to become a renewable energy hub.

## Resistance and Geopolitical Tensions

Despite the overall positive momentum, the path to implementing a global carbon price for shipping is not without obstacles. Several countries, including Japan, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Russia, Argentina, and the US, are actively opposing or seeking to weaken the NZF. Japan has suggested scrapping the carbon price altogether. Oil-producing nations are pushing to weaken agreed limits on carbon intensity in fuels, effectively prolonging the use of fossil fuels. The US, under the Trump administration, rejects the NZF entirely. These nations are largely against the interests of vulnerable countries who would lose access to critical finance.

These divisions highlight the geopolitical complexities surrounding climate action in the maritime sector. The willingness of other nations to move forward despite this opposition can be seen, according to some delegates, as "a quiet act of resistance" against those seeking to undermine climate agreements. These political discussions will continue at the Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC 84) later this week.

## The Role of the EU and Company Implications

The EU is expected to play a crucial role in the upcoming MEPC 84 discussions. With the EU already implementing its own ETS for shipping, a strong and united EU stance in favor of the NZF will be crucial in the face of anticipated geopolitical hostility, as the article indicates.

For shipping companies, the eventual adoption of a global carbon price will add to the compliance costs already incurred under the EU ETS. Companies with poor [CII ratings](/cii.html) will likely face even higher costs, as they may be required to purchase more carbon credits to offset their emissions. This will inevitably impact [company emissions rankings](/companies.html) and potentially affect competitiveness.

## Concerns over Biofuels

Alongside carbon pricing, the IMO discussions also addressed the use of biofuels in shipping. Safeguards are being considered to prevent the industry from expanding into unsustainable biofuels, which could have negative impacts on the environment and food security. This reflects a growing awareness of the potential pitfalls of relying solely on alternative fuels without proper sustainability standards.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The ongoing IMO negotiations represent a critical juncture for the decarbonization of the shipping industry. While resistance remains from some nations, the broad support for a global carbon price signals a growing recognition of the need for decisive action. The final outcome will significantly impact the cost of shipping, incentivize the adoption of cleaner technologies, and determine the industry's trajectory towards meeting its climate targets. The pressure is on to keep the NZF alive. The next round of political discussions are vital.
