Viking Cruises has floated out its new vessel, the Viking Libra, touted as the world's first hydrogen-powered cruise ship. While hailed as a step towards zero-emission cruising, the Libra's ultimate environmental impact remains to be seen, especially considering the complexities of hydrogen production and the wider regulatory pressures on the shipping industry to decarbonize under initiatives like the EU ETS.

## Viking Libra: A Hybrid Approach to Hydrogen Power

According to h2-view.com, the Viking Libra, a 54,500GT vessel, boasts a hybrid diesel-liquid hydrogen fuel cell propulsion system. This allows for potential operations running purely on hydrogen. Fincantieri, the shipbuilder, stated the hybrid system is capable of delivering 6MW of power. The PEM fuel cell system was developed by Isotta Fraschini Motori, a Fincantieri subsidiary. Liquid hydrogen will be stored onboard using a containerized system. The Libra will accommodate 998 guests in 499 staterooms and is slated for delivery to Viking in November.

While Viking's CEO Torstein Hagen claims the Libra is their "most environmentally friendly vessel yet," the reality is more nuanced. The vessel relies on a hybrid system, meaning it can still burn diesel. The crucial factor, not yet disclosed, is what percentage of the time it will actually run on hydrogen. This has a direct impact on its potential [CII ratings](/cii.html). More significantly, the environmental credentials of the hydrogen itself are paramount.

## The Hydrogen Question: Green, Blue, or Grey?

The source of hydrogen for the Viking Libra is a critical unknown. "Lifecycle emissions will depend on the hydrogen source," the article correctly points out. Hydrogen production pathways vary significantly in their environmental impact:

*   **Grey Hydrogen:** Produced from natural gas via steam methane reforming (SMR), releasing CO2 into the atmosphere. This offers little to no emissions reduction.
*   **Blue Hydrogen:** Also produced from SMR, but with carbon capture and storage (CCS). While better than grey, CCS is not 100% effective, and there are upstream methane leakage concerns.
*   **Green Hydrogen:** Produced via electrolysis powered by renewable energy sources. This is the only truly zero-emission hydrogen pathway.

If Viking sources grey or blue hydrogen, the Libra's emissions reduction will be marginal, and it may even contribute more greenhouse gases than a more efficient diesel-powered vessel using compliant fuels. Transparency regarding the hydrogen sourcing is therefore essential. This also directly affects the vessel's potential score in our [company rankings](/companies.html) of environmentally responsible shipping operators.

## EU ETS and the Cost of Emissions

The European shipping industry is under increasing pressure to decarbonize, driven in part by the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). Since January 2024, vessels over 5,000 GT calling at EU ports are included in the ETS, requiring companies to purchase allowances for their greenhouse gas emissions.

The [EU ETS](/news.html) applies to 50% of emissions from voyages starting or ending outside the EU, and 100% of emissions from voyages between EU ports. This makes alternative fuels, like hydrogen (if green), increasingly attractive, despite the current higher upfront costs. As carbon prices rise, the economic incentive to switch to cleaner fuels will grow stronger.

Without further information on where Viking intends to source the Hydrogen from it is impossible to ascertain whether this will prove to be a cost-saving initiative, or an expensive marketing stunt.

## Alternative Fuels: Batteries, Ammonia, and E-Methanol

The article notes that hydrogen's role in shipping is becoming less clear, with smaller vessels favouring batteries and larger ships exploring hydrogen derivatives like ammonia and e-methanol. Batteries are suitable for short-sea routes, but are limited by energy density for long voyages. Ammonia and e-methanol offer higher energy density but have their own challenges. Ammonia is toxic, and e-methanol production requires green hydrogen and captured CO2, both of which are energy-intensive.

The European Maritime Safety Agency (EMSA) through their [MRV data](/emissions data.html) has tracked emissions of vessels calling at EU ports, including those using alternative fuels, such as LNG and methanol. It’s very likely that in future MRV reports, the EU will collect data on Hydrogen-fuelled ships, as well. This data will allow DirtiestShips.com to better assess the real-world performance and carbon footprint of various fuel technologies, including the Viking Libra.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The Viking Libra represents a potentially positive step towards decarbonizing the cruise sector, but its true impact hinges on the source of its hydrogen. If Viking commits to sourcing green hydrogen, the Libra could significantly reduce emissions. However, if the vessel relies on grey or blue hydrogen, the environmental benefits will be limited, and Viking could face scrutiny under the EU ETS and from environmentally conscious consumers. Ultimately, transparency and commitment to sustainable hydrogen production will determine whether the Viking Libra becomes a symbol of genuine progress or just another example of greenwashing in the shipping industry.
