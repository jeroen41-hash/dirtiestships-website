The global shipping industry is grappling with intensifying pressure to decarbonize, and Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage (OCCS) is emerging as a promising, albeit complex, pathway. While shipowners are increasingly investing in pilot projects demonstrating significant CO2 capture rates, the broader commercial deployment of OCCS hinges critically on the evolution of regulatory frameworks and the development of adequate port infrastructure. This disjunction between technological readiness and policy recognition is creating a critical hurdle for the shipping sector's decarbonization efforts and directly impacts how emissions are tracked and reported on platforms like DirtiestShips.com.

## Onboard Carbon Capture: Progress and Potential

The shipping industry is actively pushing the boundaries of OCCS technology, with numerous pilot projects showcasing encouraging results across diverse vessel types. Norwegian gas carrier operator Solvang has demonstrated a 70% CO2 capture rate on one of its ethylene carriers using a chemical absorption-based system. Value Maritime, another key player, has installed its amine-based OCCS system on vessels operated by major companies including Berge Bulk, Eastern Pacific Shipping, X-Press Feeders, Ardmore Shipping, and Mitsui O.S.K. Lines (MOL). These installations span bulk carriers, container ships, tankers, and other vessel types, achieving capture rates of up to 40% according to a recent Lloyd's Register (LR) report.

Further advancing the technology, Seabound achieved an impressive 78% CO2 capture rate and over 90% SOx removal during sea trials on the *Sounion Trader*. This success has led to its first full-scale commercial installation on a cement carrier, where the captured CO2 is stored onboard as limestone for reuse at Heidelberg Materials’ Brevik cement plant. These projects, along with initiatives by NYK Line and Hokkaido Electric Power exploring offloading and reuse, underscore a growing confidence in OCCS as a viable emissions reduction tool. The widespread adoption by a variety of [company rankings](/companies.html) highlights the industry's commitment to finding practical solutions.

## The Regulatory Roadblock: CII, EU ETS, and FuelEU Maritime

Despite the evident technological progress, the regulatory landscape remains a significant barrier to OCCS adoption. The International Maritime Organisation's (IMO) existing frameworks – the Energy Efficiency Existing Ship Index (EEXI), Energy Efficiency Design Index (EEDI), and the critical [CII ratings](/cii.html) – currently lack dedicated methodologies to account for emissions captured by OCCS. This absence means that vessels employing OCCS, even with high capture rates, may not see the direct improvements in their CII scores that would reflect their true carbon intensity reductions. For operators closely monitoring their [emissions data](/charts.html) and compliance, this is a major disincentive.

While the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) already recognizes permanently stored CO2, providing a potential pathway for OCCS to reduce [EU ETS carbon costs](/news.html), the equally impactful FuelEU Maritime regulation is still awaiting methodological updates. The LR report emphasizes that this regulatory void "erodes the near-term business case" for OCCS, even where the technology is technically feasible. The primary compliance levers continue to focus on fuel type rather than post-combustion abatement, creating a disconnect that hinders investment and widespread adoption.

## Infrastructure: The Missing Link

Beyond regulatory certainty, the physical infrastructure for handling captured CO2 presents another monumental challenge. The successful commercial deployment of OCCS necessitates robust port infrastructure for receiving, storing, and transporting captured CO2. Key locations identified with existing or planned facilities – such as Rotterdam, Esbjerg, Immingham, Antwerp-Bruges, Dunkirk, Wilhelmshaven, Singapore, and Norway’s Northern Lights terminal – are heavily concentrated in Northern Europe.

Crucially, the LR report highlights a significant geographical imbalance: most of the world's busiest shipping corridors, including the US, Asia, and the Middle East, largely lack the necessary reception capacity. This absence means that even if a vessel captures CO2 at sea, it may struggle to offload it efficiently, undermining the entire OCCS value chain. This infrastructure gap underscores why the development of such facilities is as critical as the onboard technology itself, impacting the overall effectiveness of emissions reduction strategies tracked via EU MRV data.

## What this means for shipping emissions

Onboard Carbon Capture and Storage holds undeniable potential to make a substantial contribution to shipping's decarbonization efforts, particularly in the interim period before green fuels become widely available. However, for OCCS to translate into meaningful, recognized reductions in shipping emissions, there must be a synchronized advancement on multiple fronts. Regulatory bodies, especially the IMO and the EU (for FuelEU Maritime), must urgently develop clear methodologies to credit captured CO2 within existing and future compliance frameworks like [CII ratings](/cii.html). Concurrently, significant investment in global port infrastructure is essential to create the ecosystem necessary for offloading, storing, and reusing captured carbon. Without these crucial developments, the impressive technological gains made by innovators will struggle to translate into tangible, compliant, and economically viable pathways for a cleaner shipping industry.
