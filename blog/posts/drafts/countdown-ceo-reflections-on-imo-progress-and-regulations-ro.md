The future of maritime decarbonization hangs in the balance following recent IMO meetings that saw a previously agreed-upon Net-Zero Framework delayed due to significant opposition. While the industry has largely accepted the need for decarbonization, the "how" and "when" remain contentious, with the IMO's progress facing headwinds, threatening to stall progress on emissions reduction targets.

## IMO Net-Zero Framework Stalled

The article from hellenicshippingnews.com highlights the challenges facing the International Maritime Organization (IMO) in its efforts to regulate greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from shipping. After years of work and negotiations, the IMO initially approved a Net-Zero Framework in April 2025, which was intended to provide clarity, incentives, and mechanisms for a just and equitable transition. However, its adoption was delayed in October 2025 due to "strong opposition from some Member States." This delay raises concerns about the commitment of some nations to multilateral regulation of GHG emissions. The article does not name the opposing member states, which reduces the transparency of the process.

## Signs of Hope Amidst Uncertainty

Despite the setback, there are glimmers of hope. Member States who opposed the Net-Zero Framework reaffirmed their commitment to the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy. They also signaled a willingness to engage on a potential way forward. Progress was made on guidelines related to GHG fuel intensity calculation, fuel certification, rewards for zero- and near-zero energy, onboard carbon capture, and life-cycle assessment of fuels. This suggests a shared interest in advancing the decarbonization agenda, even if the specific mechanisms remain under debate. The article also mentions constructive debate on the Energy Efficiency Framework, specifically metrics and enforcement mechanisms.

## Strategic Adjustment to Decarbonization Efforts

Acknowledging the geopolitical shifts and changing market conditions, the Center mentioned in the article is strategically adjusting its work. They will concentrate resources in two core areas: fleet transition and regulation/policy. Fleet transition will focus on accelerating the adoption of energy efficiency measures and low-emissions technologies. Regulation and policy will focus on providing science-based insights to inform industry standards and regulatory development. This shift indicates a move towards practical solutions and informed policymaking, crucial for navigating the complexities of maritime decarbonization.

## The EU ETS and IMO Regulations

The IMO's struggles to implement a global carbon pricing mechanism highlight the importance of regional initiatives like the EU ETS, which now includes shipping. As companies face increasing carbon costs under the EU ETS, they have a greater incentive to invest in fuel-efficient technologies and alternative fuels. The success of the EU ETS in driving decarbonization could serve as a model for future global regulations, but the IMO must overcome its internal divisions to create a truly effective framework. The interplay between regional and global regulations will shape the future of maritime decarbonization.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The delay in adopting the IMO Net-Zero Framework is a setback, but not a complete defeat. The commitment to the 2023 IMO GHG Strategy and the progress on technical guidelines offer a foundation for future progress. However, success depends on Member States approaching future meetings with good faith and a willingness to compromise. Without a global framework, regional initiatives like the EU ETS will continue to drive decarbonization efforts, but a unified approach is essential for achieving meaningful reductions in shipping emissions. The coming months will be critical for determining whether the IMO can overcome its challenges and deliver on its decarbonization goals.
