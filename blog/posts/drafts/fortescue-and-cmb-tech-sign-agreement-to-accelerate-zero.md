The recent agreement between Fortescue and CMB.TECH to charter up to 12 ammonia-capable Newcastlemax vessels marks a significant development in the shipping industry's decarbonization efforts, particularly for the high-emission dry bulk sector. This move directly addresses the escalating pressure from regulators and stakeholders, impacting everything from [CII ratings](/cii.html) to the financial burden of EU ETS carbon costs, and sets a precedent for how major players might navigate the transition to lower-carbon fuels. For a sector under intense scrutiny for its CO2 output, such large-scale commitments to alternative fuel technologies are critical indicators of future emissions trends.

## A Fleet of Ammonia-Capable Newcastlemaxes

The core of the agreement involves Fortescue chartering 12 Newcastlemax dry bulk vessels, each with a substantial 210,000 deadweight tonnes (dwt), from Bocimar, CMB.TECH’s dry bulk shipping arm. This is not merely a hypothetical pledge; up to three of these vessels are slated for delivery with dual-fuel ammonia engines by the end of 2026. The remaining nine will be ammonia-ready, allowing for future conversion to operate on this alternative fuel. This phased approach highlights both the urgency to deploy cleaner vessels and the current realities of engine technology and fuel infrastructure development. The sheer size and number of these vessels – a dozen large bulk carriers – underscore the potential impact on overall fleet emissions within the dry bulk segment.

## The Promise and the 'Green Ammonia' Condition

The potential environmental benefits are significant, with the combined fleet capable of reducing carbon dioxide emissions by approximately 250,000 tonnes a year *if* fuelled by green ammonia, compared to conventional marine fuels. This figure represents a substantial cut for a single fleet. However, the crucial qualifier "if fuelled by green ammonia" cannot be overlooked. As Fortescue's Director Katie Charuga noted, "The next challenge is scaling the use of green ammonia."

For DirtiestShips.com, this distinction is paramount. Vessels operating on fossil fuels, even if "ammonia-ready," will continue to register high emissions in [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) and receive lower [CII ratings](/cii.html) (D or E grades) until they switch to the cleaner fuel. The financial implications are equally stark: if these vessels operate within the EU/EEA, the use of conventional fuels will incur significant EU ETS carbon costs. The 250,000 tonnes of CO2 avoided annually translates directly into millions of euros in avoided carbon allowances under the EU ETS, offering a clear economic incentive alongside the environmental one. ## Dry Bulk Shipping's Decarbonization Frontier

The Newcastlemax class of vessel is a workhorse of the global dry bulk trade, moving vast quantities of iron ore, coal, and grain. This sector has historically been challenging to decarbonize due to the long voyages, high energy demands, and the need for robust, reliable propulsion systems. Fortescue, as a major iron ore producer, is both a significant contributor to shipping emissions through its chartered fleet and a potential driver of change. This agreement directly addresses their 'Scope 3' emissions – those generated by their value chain, including shipping. By targeting this specific vessel type, the partnership sends a strong signal that even hard-to-abate segments of shipping can transition. It also aligns with the broader industry discussions on alternative fuels, moving beyond talk to tangible investment in new shipbuilding technologies.

## Market Signals and the Road Ahead

CMB.TECH CEO Alexander Saverys rightly states that this agreement "sends a powerful signal to the market, particularly at a time when there is doubt about the decarbonisation of shipping." This deal showcases a commitment to ammonia as a viable marine fuel, backed by concrete orders and a clear timeline for deployment. Such large-scale charters are vital for stimulating the supply chain for green ammonia production, bunkering infrastructure, and engine development, creating the necessary demand Fortescue refers to. It helps validate the investment case for green ammonia, encouraging other shipowners and charterers to follow suit. However, the success of this initiative in truly reducing overall emissions hinges on the availability, cost-effectiveness, and widespread adoption of *green* ammonia – an aspect our platform will continue to monitor closely in our [news feed](/news.html).

### What this means for shipping emissions

This agreement is a concrete step towards decarbonizing a significant portion of the dry bulk fleet, offering a clear pathway to substantial CO2 reductions if green ammonia is consistently utilized. It demonstrates that major players are moving beyond pilot projects to larger-scale commitments, which is essential for scaling alternative fuel solutions. The challenge now shifts from 'if' to 'how soon and how green' these ammonia-capable vessels genuinely become.
