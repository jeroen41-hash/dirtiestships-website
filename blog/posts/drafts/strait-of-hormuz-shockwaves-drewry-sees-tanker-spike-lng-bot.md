The escalating crisis in the Strait of Hormuz, triggered by recent US and Israeli strikes on Iranian targets, poses a significant threat to global shipping emissions. The effective shutdown of this crucial chokepoint is forcing vessels to reroute, leading to longer voyages and increased fuel consumption, with potential ramifications for [CII ratings](/cii.html) and [EU ETS](/ets.html) carbon costs for impacted voyages.

## Tanker Rates Surge Amidst Geopolitical Uncertainty

Drewry Maritime Research highlights that the Strait of Hormuz handles roughly 15 million barrels per day, representing about one-third of global seaborne crude trade (gcaptain.com). The sudden disruption has seen ship transits plummet by over 80% as owners and charterers reassess risks, including the withdrawal of war-risk insurance. While some bypass capacity exists via pipelines in Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Iraq, this amounts to only about 4 million barrels per day, leaving a substantial shortfall.

The immediate consequence is a potential spike in Very Large Crude Carrier (VLCC) rates due to longer voyages from alternative sources like the U.S. Gulf and West Africa to Asia. This increase in tonne-mile demand would contribute directly to higher fuel consumption and, consequently, increased CO2 emissions. The longer distances will negatively impact the annual [CII ratings](/cii.html) of the vessels undertaking these journeys as higher fuel consumption leads to a lower rating.

## LNG Bottlenecks and Realignment of Trade Flows

The Strait of Hormuz is also vital for Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG), accounting for approximately 20% of global LNG supply. Drewry estimates that 2 million tonnes per week from Qatar and the UAE could be immediately affected, potentially rising to 5-6 million tonnes per month if the disruption persists.

While LNG prices are expected to rise, shipping rates may initially lag as vessels idle in safe waters due to reduced demand from Europe and parts of Asia. However, a prolonged disruption will compel Asian buyers to seek supplies from the U.S. and Australia, again increasing tonne-mile demand and emissions. Vessels rerouting for longer voyages will burn significantly more fuel, potentially pushing them into a lower [CII rating](/cii.html) category.

## Refined Product Disruptions and European Vulnerability

Refined products, including naphtha, diesel, and jet fuel, face considerable strain. The Strait handles roughly 2.9 million barrels per day, about 17% of global seaborne product trade. The impact is compounded by Iranian drone strikes on Saudi Arabia’s Ras Tanura refinery.

Naphtha is particularly vulnerable, with approximately 1.2 million barrels per day transiting Hormuz, primarily destined for Northeast Asia. This disruption is likely to cause a shortage in the Asian petrochemical market, potentially redirecting flows towards India and the United States, boosting tonne-mile demand for Long Range (LR) product tankers.

Europe, already grappling with structural diesel deficits, faces particular vulnerability in diesel and jet fuel. Losing Middle East supply would force a pivot toward India and the U.S., lengthening voyage distances. The increased distances traveled by tankers to replace the refined products will further impact the [company rankings](/companies.html) as higher fuel consumption will increase each company's attributable emissions.

## Bunker Costs and EU ETS Implications

The spike in crude oil prices, with Brent crude rising above $80 per barrel, directly impacts bunker costs, raising voyage expenses across the global fleet. This increased cost, combined with longer voyages, will significantly affect vessels operating within the [EU ETS](/ets.html) region. The additional fuel consumed on these extended voyages will incur additional carbon costs, impacting the profitability of these routes. Companies with poorer [CII ratings](/cii.html) might face additional penalties from charterers.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The crisis in the Strait of Hormuz underscores the vulnerability of global shipping to geopolitical events and their subsequent impact on emissions. Rerouting vessels to avoid the conflict zone will lead to increased fuel consumption, negatively affecting [CII ratings](/cii.html), and increasing carbon costs under the [EU ETS](/ets.html). While the duration of the disruption remains uncertain, its potential to drive up shipping emissions is undeniable, highlighting the need for the industry to accelerate its transition to cleaner fuels and more efficient operations to mitigate the impact of such events on the sector's environmental footprint and compliance with emissions regulations.
