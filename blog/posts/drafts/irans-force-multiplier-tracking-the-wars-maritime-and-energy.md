The escalating tensions in the Strait of Hormuz, highlighted in a recent Washington Institute report, present a significant, albeit indirect, threat to global shipping emissions. Iran’s increasing control over this vital chokepoint is disrupting energy flows and potentially impacting the efficiency, and therefore emissions, of vessels navigating the region. This has the potential to impact shipping companies and their [CII ratings](/cii.html).

## Iran's Strait Control: A New Reality

The Washington Institute report details how Iran has established itself as a key "traffic controller" in the Strait of Hormuz since the start of the current conflict. This control manifests in several ways, including delaying vessels, influencing routes, and reportedly demanding transit fees. This has significant implications for the energy market, but also for the overall efficiency of shipping operations in the area. The report states that between March 5th and March 20th at least 28 Iranian-linked oil tankers transited the strait.

## Rerouting and Inefficiencies: Emissions Implications

While the article doesn’t explicitly discuss emissions, the disruptions described inherently lead to increased fuel consumption and, consequently, higher CO2 output. Rerouting, delays, and potentially slower speeds all contribute to this. The report mentions the *Karachi* (IMO 9903413) deviating from its usual route, sailing around Iran’s Larak Island. Such detours, while seemingly minor, can add considerable distance and time to voyages, particularly when multiplied across numerous vessels.

Furthermore, the uncertainty and potential for delays could lead to "hurry up and wait" scenarios, where ships are forced to increase speed to make up for lost time, further increasing fuel consumption. This is especially relevant given the increasing focus on ships' [CII ratings](/cii.html) and the pressure to improve energy efficiency. Ships that are forced to take longer routes may find themselves slipping in their CII ratings.

## LNG and Dry Cargo: Wider Impact

The report also highlights the impact on LNG tankers, noting they have been virtually nonexistent in the strait since late February, and disruptions to dry cargo vessels. While oil tankers are the primary concern, any disruption to shipping lanes impacts all vessel types. The *Giacometti* (IMO 9615377), a dry cargo vessel owned by Oceanbulk Shipping, is mentioned as one of nine vessels entering and leaving the Persian Gulf since March 15th. The source article is thin on detail here, but broadly we know that general cargo and container ships are already facing pressure under the EU ETS, and any disruptions will only add to the cost burden. These costs may then be passed on to shippers, impacting global trade.

## EU ETS and the Cost of Disruption

The EU's Emissions Trading System (ETS) now applies to shipping, making companies responsible for the carbon emissions of their voyages to and from EU ports. Increased journey times and fuel consumption in the Strait of Hormuz directly translate to higher carbon costs for companies operating in the region. This makes companies vulnerable to negative placement on [company rankings](/companies.html).

The cost of EU Allowances (EUAs) is significant, and any increase in emissions due to geopolitical instability will add to the financial burden on shipping companies. Moreover, the potential for increased freight rates, driven by higher fuel costs and carbon taxes, will ultimately be borne by consumers.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The situation in the Strait of Hormuz, as detailed by the Washington Institute report, presents a clear risk to global shipping's efforts to reduce emissions. While the immediate impact is on energy markets, the ripple effects through shipping inefficiencies, rerouting, and potential speed increases will inevitably lead to increased CO2 output. This highlights the interconnectedness of geopolitical stability, energy security, and environmental sustainability within the maritime industry. Monitoring these developments through data sources like [EMSA MRV data](/charts.html) and [CII ratings](/cii.html) is crucial for understanding the full scope of the emissions impact.
