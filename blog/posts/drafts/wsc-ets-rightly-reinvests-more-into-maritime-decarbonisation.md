The World Shipping Council (WSC), representing the global container shipping and vehicle carrier industries, has voiced a cautious welcome for the European Commission's latest revisions to the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). While applauding measures aimed at accelerating alternative fuel adoption and reinvesting revenues into maritime decarbonisation, the WSC raises concerns that new port rules could inadvertently target competition rather than genuinely reducing emissions. For DirtiestShips.com, these developments are critical, as the EU ETS directly impacts the CO2 footprint of global shipping, influencing everything from [CII ratings](/cii.html) to the financial viability of sustainable operations, all tracked meticulously through reported EU MRV data.

## Catalysing Decarbonisation: The Role of Fuel Price Parity

A central plank of the WSC's positive reception is the proposed fuel mechanism, designed to mirror aviation's approach by closing the significant price gap between conventional and alternative maritime fuels. Sustainable fuels can currently cost anywhere from 100 to 400 percent more than traditional bunkers, a prohibitive barrier to their widespread adoption. By mitigating this disparity, the EU aims to incentivise uptake, stimulate investment in production infrastructure, and position Europe as a leader in alternative fuel bunkering. This is a direct response to a fundamental challenge the industry faces: even with "cleaner" ships, the necessary "cleaner fuels" are not yet economically viable or widely available.

## The €160 Billion Question: Fuel Availability and Investment

As Simon Bergulf, Vice President, Environment and Climate at the World Shipping Council, highlighted, "Liner shipping has already invested over €160 billion in ships that can operate on renewable fuels, but these cleaner ships need cleaner fuels." This staggering investment underscores the industry's commitment, but also its vulnerability to the lack of infrastructure and affordable fuel supply. For companies navigating complex regulatory landscapes, the availability of cost-effective alternative fuels is paramount. Without it, even the most modern vessels risk being saddled with poor [CII ratings](/cii.html) due to continued reliance on high-emissions conventional fuels, impacting their operational efficiency and marketability.

## ETS Revenue Reinvestment: A Strategic Necessity

The WSC strongly supports the reinvestment of a significant portion of ETS revenues back into maritime decarbonisation. This "sensible and necessary step" is seen as crucial for achieving the EU's ambitious climate goals. Potential uses for these funds include strengthening European ports through alternative fuel infrastructure and electrification, benefiting all vessels that call at European ports and contribute to the ETS. Such strategic investment is vital for reducing the carbon intensity of port operations, which are often overlooked but contribute substantially to overall shipping emissions. DirtiestShips.com tracks these holistic emissions footprints, understanding that true decarbonisation extends beyond the ship itself. Companies making proactive investments in shoreside power and alternative fuel bunkering will likely see improvements in their overall [company rankings](/companies.html) as the industry progresses.

## The Transhipment Conundrum: Competition vs. Emissions Reduction

While welcoming the recognition that the current ETS design can make European ports less competitive for cargo transhipped between two non-EU markets, the WSC expresses significant concern about the proposed expansion of the transhipment list. The new rules risk penalising neighbouring non-EU ports within 150 nautical miles purely based on their infrastructure (e.g., deep water, long berths) rather than actual transhipment activity. "The ETS should be focused on cutting emissions, not making neighbouring non-EU ports less competitive," Bergulf stated. This highlights a potential flaw where policy, intended to reduce emissions, could instead distort competition without a clear link to measurable CO2 reductions. Such policy missteps can have unintended consequences, potentially shifting emissions rather than eliminating them, a trend DirtiestShips.com constantly monitors through comprehensive [emissions data](/charts.html).

### What this means for shipping emissions

The EU ETS revisions present a mixed bag for global shipping's decarbonisation efforts. While the focus on bridging the alternative fuel price gap and reinvesting ETS revenues is a positive step towards driving down overall CO2 emissions, the contentious transhipment rules could introduce market distortions without a clear emissions benefit. The actual impact on [CII ratings](/cii.html) and [EU ETS carbon costs](/?tag=ETS) for shipping companies will largely depend on the effective implementation of fuel incentives and whether competitive concerns truly align with emissions reduction goals.
