Vale and Shandong Shipping Partner to Pioneer Ethanol-Fueled Bulk Carriers

A significant step towards maritime decarbonization was announced this week, as Vale, the Brazilian mining giant, inked a deal with China's Shandong Shipping Corporation to construct the world’s first ocean-going vessels primarily powered by ethanol. This ambitious project aims to drastically reduce greenhouse gas emissions from the transportation of iron ore, a sector that contributes significantly to shipping's carbon footprint and is increasingly under scrutiny from regulators and consumers alike. While details are still emerging, this partnership represents a tangible move towards alternative fuels and highlights the growing pressure to improve [CII ratings](/cii.html) across the industry.

## Details of the Vale/Shandong Shipping Agreement

According to marinelink.com, the agreement involves the construction of two Guaibamax bulk carriers under 25-year contracts, with deliveries scheduled to commence in 2029. The vessels will boast a substantial cargo capacity of 325,000 tonnes and measure approximately 340 meters in length. While the initial order is for two vessels, the agreement includes options for additional ships, suggesting a potential for significant expansion of the ethanol-fueled fleet. Crucially, these vessels will not be limited to ethanol alone, with the ability to run on methanol and conventional bunker fuel, offering operational flexibility. Furthermore, the design allows for future conversion to liquefied natural gas (LNG) or ammonia, ensuring these ships remain adaptable to evolving fuel technologies and regulations.

## The Potential of Ethanol in Decarbonizing Shipping

Vale estimates that the use of ethanol could reduce greenhouse gas emissions by around 90% compared with heavy fuel oil. This is a substantial claim, and the exact reduction will depend heavily on the lifecycle emissions of the ethanol production process, including land use changes and fertilizer application. The environmental impact of different ethanol production pathways is a complex topic, but this figure provides a compelling target for reducing the carbon intensity of Vale's shipping operations.

## Energy Efficiency Technologies and Vale's Ecoshipping Program

Beyond the primary ethanol propulsion system, the newbuilds will also incorporate a suite of energy-efficiency technologies. These include rotor sails, hydrodynamic devices, shaft generators, frequency inverters, and silicone coatings. These technologies, while individually contributing smaller emissions reductions, collectively contribute to significant improvements in overall fuel efficiency. The project falls under Vale’s broader Ecoshipping program, aimed at improving fleet efficiency and reducing emissions across its value chain. Vale has committed to reducing Scope 3 emissions by 15% by 2035, with maritime transport playing a critical role in achieving this target. This commitment aligns with increasing investor and regulatory pressure to reduce emissions throughout the supply chain. Companies with high emissions from their shipping activities risk poorer [company rankings](/companies.html) if they fail to address this challenge.

## The Broader Regulatory and Economic Context

While the marinelink.com article does not explicitly mention the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), this agreement is undoubtedly influenced by the increasing cost of carbon emissions. As of January 1, 2024, shipping has been included in the EU ETS, requiring shipowners to purchase allowances for their CO2 emissions on voyages to, from, and within the EU. The price of these allowances can significantly impact the operating costs of vessels, particularly those using high-emission fuels. Ethanol, with its potential for lower CO2 emissions, could offer a way to reduce exposure to the EU ETS carbon costs. Furthermore, vessels calling at EU ports are required to report their emissions data through the EU MRV (Monitoring, Reporting, and Verification) regulation, administered by EMSA. The data collected through MRV informs the [CII ratings](/cii.html) assigned to ships, further incentivizing emissions reductions. The higher the CII rating, the less likely the vessel will encounter port restrictions.

What this means for shipping emissions:

The Vale and Shandong Shipping agreement signifies a concrete step toward exploring alternative fuels in the bulk carrier sector. The potential 90% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions, if realized, could have a significant impact on the overall carbon footprint of iron ore transportation. While the first vessels are not expected until 2029, this investment demonstrates a commitment to decarbonization and provides a valuable test case for the feasibility and scalability of ethanol as a marine fuel. The project underscores the growing importance of emissions reduction in the shipping industry, driven by both regulatory pressures like the EU ETS and increasing demands from stakeholders for sustainable practices. The performance of these vessels, tracked through systems like the EU MRV and reflected in [CII ratings](/cii.html), will be closely watched by the industry.
