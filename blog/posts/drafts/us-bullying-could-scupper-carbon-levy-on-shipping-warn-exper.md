The potential for a global carbon levy on shipping, a key tool for decarbonizing the industry and raising funds for climate action, is facing significant headwinds. A report in *The Guardian* alleges that pressure from the United States has led to Panama reversing its support for the measure, potentially unraveling years of international negotiations at the International Maritime Organization (IMO). This development casts a shadow over the future of shipping emissions regulations and the industry's ability to meet its decarbonization targets.

## Panama's Shift and US Influence

The core of the issue revolves around Panama, a crucial maritime state due to the Panama Canal which handles approximately 5% of global shipping volume. According to *The Guardian*, Panama, along with Liberia and Argentina, has co-sponsored a proposal to the IMO that would effectively dismantle the carbon levy. The report attributes Panama's change of heart to "unprecedented" bullying from the US, citing concerns about potential repercussions and past statements by former President Trump regarding Panama. This action follows a previous delay in the levy's implementation secured at an IMO meeting in October, allegedly due to countries switching or abstaining their votes after US intervention.

Liberia, a country with one of the world's largest shipping registries, further complicates the situation. As registration flags are often tied to less rigorous regulation and environmental standards, Liberia's co-sponsorship introduces a governance concern. Liberia's CII ratings are also generally lower, given the older age of ships registered to their flag. While the article doesn't mention specific ships or companies, the shift in position of key maritime states underscores the fragility of international agreements on shipping emissions.

## Implications for IMO Climate Commitments

The proposed carbon levy was intended to support the IMO's greenhouse gas reduction targets agreed upon in 2023. Without it, the industry faces a significant challenge in meeting these targets. The article quotes John Maggs of the Clean Shipping Coalition, who argues that the proposal "completely abandons" commitments to environmental integrity and environmental justice. The levy was designed not only to incentivize cleaner shipping practices but also to provide funds to mitigate any disproportionate negative impact on developing states. Its removal would leave vulnerable nations without vital support. The IMO's Marine Environment Protection Committee meeting in April will be critical in determining the fate of the levy and the broader decarbonization agenda. However, this meeting has been pushed to April 2026, further delaying regulation.

## The Role of Carbon Pricing and the EU ETS

The stalled global levy contrasts sharply with regional efforts like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), which now includes maritime emissions. The EU ETS places a carbon price on emissions from ships calling at EU ports, creating a financial incentive for operators to reduce their carbon footprint. The cost of EU ETS compliance varies based on fuel type and vessel efficiency, influencing operational decisions and potentially accelerating the adoption of alternative fuels. With the IMO levy potentially off the table, regional schemes like the EU ETS become even more crucial in driving near-term emissions reductions, although the lack of global consensus undermines the overall effectiveness. Vessels with low CII ratings calling in Europe will feel a greater impact to their voyage costs from the ETS.

## Geopolitical Tensions and Alternative Fuels

The article highlights the complexities of international climate negotiations, particularly the influence of geopolitical tensions. Greece, for example, is reportedly embarking on a partnership with Saudi Arabia in exchange for opposing the carbon levy. This points to the powerful influence of fossil fuel interests in shaping shipping regulations. The article also notes concerns that the proposal from Panama, Liberia, and Argentina could "lock in" liquefied fossil gas (LNG) for shipping, hindering the transition to cleaner alternatives like hydrogen. This highlights the importance of policies that promote truly sustainable fuels and prevent the industry from becoming reliant on fossil fuel alternatives that offer limited emissions reductions.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The alleged US interference and the subsequent reversal by key maritime states represent a major setback for global efforts to decarbonize shipping. With the future of the IMO carbon levy uncertain, the industry faces a fragmented regulatory landscape, with regional initiatives like the EU ETS taking on even greater importance. Without a globally consistent carbon pricing mechanism, achieving significant emissions reductions will be considerably more challenging, potentially jeopardizing the industry's ability to meet its climate targets and creating competitive distortions between companies operating in different regions.
