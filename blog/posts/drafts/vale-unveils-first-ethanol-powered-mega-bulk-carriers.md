Vale's commitment to building ethanol-powered mega bulk carriers signals a potentially disruptive shift in maritime decarbonization strategies, challenging the industry's focus on methanol and ammonia. The move, while still several years away from deployment, underscores the growing pressure on shipping companies to meet increasingly stringent emissions targets under regulations like the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) and the IMO's [CII ratings](/cii.html) scheme.

## Ethanol Enters the Fray: A 90% Reduction Claim

Vale, in partnership with Shandong Shipping Corporation, is set to construct a new generation of "Guaibamax" bulk carriers designed to run primarily on ethanol. According to the gcaptain.com report, the first vessel is anticipated to enter service in 2029. Vale claims that, on a "well-to-wake" lifecycle basis, second-generation ethanol could slash carbon emissions by roughly 90% compared to heavy fuel oil (HFO). This is a significant claim, surpassing many projections for methanol and ammonia when accounting for their own upstream emissions.

Each vessel will be enormous, measuring 340 meters in length and possessing a 325,000-tonne carrying capacity. The design incorporates fuel flexibility, allowing the ships to operate on methanol and conventional bunker fuel in addition to ethanol, with future adaptability for LNG and ammonia. Ten dual-fuel (methanol and HFO) vessels are already on order from Shandong, with deliveries scheduled to begin in 2027.

It's important to note that the 90% reduction is contingent on the source and production method of the ethanol. Second-generation ethanol, derived from non-food biomass, is crucial to achieving this level of emissions reduction. The article doesn't specify the origin of Vale's intended ethanol supply, a detail which will be crucial for validating the project's environmental benefits through EU MRV reporting requirements once the ships enter service.

## Vale's Ecoshipping Program and Scope 3 Ambitions

The ethanol-powered ships are part of Vale's broader "Ecoshipping" program. This program reflects the growing concern over Scope 3 emissions - those generated indirectly from a company's value chain. For a mining giant like Vale, shipping represents a substantial portion of its Scope 3 emissions. The company has already committed to reducing these emissions by 15% by 2035.

Rodrigo Bermelho, Vale’s Director of Shipping, highlights the strategic advantage of combining fuel flexibility with wind-assisted propulsion. Each vessel will be equipped with five rotor sails, alongside hull optimization, shaft generators, advanced coatings, and frequency-controlled systems, targeting an additional 15% emissions reduction compared to existing Very Large Ore Carriers (VLOCs). The combined effect of alternative fuel and efficiency measures suggests a significant commitment to decarbonizing Vale's logistical operations.

The integration of wind-assisted propulsion systems further lowers the carbon intensity of these vessels, contributing to improved [CII ratings](/cii.html) and potentially reducing the cost of EU ETS carbon allowances when operating within the EU.

## The Economics of Ethanol: A Viable Alternative?

While ammonia and methanol have garnered considerable attention as potential alternative fuels, Vale's focus on ethanol introduces a compelling alternative. Ethanol benefits from established biofuel supply chains, offering potentially lower upfront infrastructure costs compared to more nascent ammonia bunkering networks. However, the availability of sustainable, second-generation ethanol at scale remains a critical factor.

The cost competitiveness of ethanol versus HFO, methanol, and ammonia, especially when factoring in carbon pricing mechanisms like the EU ETS, will ultimately determine its long-term viability. ## What this means for shipping emissions

Vale's investment in ethanol-powered mega bulk carriers represents a significant bet on an alternative fuel pathway. While challenges remain in scaling sustainable ethanol production, the potential for substantial emissions reductions, coupled with existing biofuel infrastructure, makes it a contender to watch. The project's success will depend on transparently verifiable emissions reductions and cost-effectiveness compared to other fuels under evolving carbon regulations. It signals a diversification of decarbonization strategies within the shipping industry and highlights the importance of considering the full lifecycle emissions of alternative fuels.
