The renewed U.S. sanctions targeting tankers involved in Iranian and Russian oil trade are poised to tighten the screws on maritime emissions, potentially impacting [CII ratings](/cii.html) for affected vessels and increasing compliance costs under the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). The move signals a shift back to aggressive enforcement, directly impacting shipping companies and raising the stakes for those skirting sanctions.

## U.S. Sanctions Target "Shadow Fleet"

The U.S. Treasury Department has sanctioned over two dozen individuals, companies, and vessels connected to Iranian oil magnate Mohammad Hossein Shamkhani, according to gcaptain.com. This network is accused of facilitating the movement of Iranian and, significantly, Russian petroleum. The sanctions coincide with the expiration of a 30-day waiver that allowed the sale of Iranian oil already loaded on vessels. The targeted vessels include crude, product, and LPG carriers operating under various flags and ownership structures to obscure their activities. The Treasury's action aims to disrupt the "shipping backbone" supporting Iran's oil trade.

## Specific Vessels and Ownership Structures Named

The Treasury Department explicitly identified several vessels. The Mozambique-flagged LPG carrier AURA (IMO 9274563), owned and operated by Marshall Islands-based Aura Lines Inc., is accused of transporting over three million barrels of Iranian LPG since the beginning of 2025. Additionally, a group of tankers managed by India-based Fleet Tanqo Private Limited, including the HORAE (IMO 9413004), VERSA (IMO 9379301), ANAYA (IMO 9326885), DAPHNE V (IMO 9321677), and SILVAR (IMO 9291262), were targeted. These vessels allegedly carried more than 20 cargoes of Russian petroleum products in 2025, highlighting the intersection of Iranian and Russian trade. Vessels linked to Hapuka Marine Ltd., Nardie International S.A., and Anika Lines Inc. were also named. This detailed targeting underscores the U.S. government's attempt to dismantle the network facilitating sanctioned oil exports.

## Impact on CII Ratings and EU ETS

While the article doesn't directly mention the [CII ratings](/cii.html), these sanctions are likely to influence the ratings of the targeted vessels. Increased idling time due to rerouting, slower steaming to avoid detection, or longer voyages to circumvent sanctioned areas can negatively affect a vessel's CII rating. Moreover, any vessel calling at EU ports will be subject to the EU ETS, and sanctioned entities may face difficulties complying with the reporting and emissions allowance requirements. The [EU ETS carbon costs](/news.html) associated with non-compliance can be substantial, adding financial pressure on companies already operating in a high-risk environment. The involvement of Russian petroleum products also adds another layer of complexity given existing sanctions targeting Russia.

## Enforcement and Disruption of Trade Flows

Beyond financial sanctions, the U.S. is actively enforcing a blockade of Iranian ports, reportedly turning back at least nine vessels. This blockade, coupled with the threat of secondary sanctions, is slowing traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, as shipowners reassess the risks of operating in the region. The article from gcaptain.com states that movements have slowed sharply. This disruption to trade flows can increase voyage distances and, consequently, overall emissions. The combination of financial penalties and active interdiction represents a significant escalation in pressure on Iranian oil exports.

## What this means for shipping emissions

The renewed U.S. sanctions and enforcement actions will likely lead to increased inefficiencies in the shadow tanker fleet, resulting in higher emissions per ton-mile. Rerouting, slower steaming, and longer waiting times will all contribute to increased fuel consumption and CO2 output. While the overall volume of oil transported may decrease, the emissions intensity of that transportation is almost certain to rise. This underscores the complex interplay between geopolitical events, sanctions regimes, and the environmental performance of the shipping industry.
