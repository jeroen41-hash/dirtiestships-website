The path to decarbonizing global shipping is riddled with political obstacles, and a recent report highlights deep divisions among nations regarding the implementation of key environmental regulations. With discussions at the International Maritime Organization (IMO) stalling, the urgency for meaningful action to curb shipping emissions becomes increasingly critical. The future of maritime decarbonization hangs in the balance, dependent on overcoming these divisions and embracing more aggressive policies.

## Deep Divisions Emerge at MEPC 84

The Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) 84 meeting exposed significant disagreements among member states concerning the proposed Net-Zero Framework (NZF). According to Transport & Environment (T&E), these divisions center on core elements of the framework, including its economic component and the role of Liquefied Natural Gas (LNG) in the decarbonization pathway. Alternative frameworks presented by Argentina, Japan, and Fiji further underscore the lack of consensus. The economic element, integral to the NZF and the 2023 IMO GHG strategy, faces contention, jeopardizing the framework's potential effectiveness. These disagreements cast doubt on the IMO's ability to secure a comprehensive agreement that effectively drives decarbonization, as reported by safety4sea.com.

## NZF Shortcomings and the Need for Further Regulation

Even if the NZF is adopted in its current form, it may fall short of achieving the IMO's 2030 emission reduction targets. T&E estimates that the draft deal is projected to miss these objectives by approximately 8%, even without considering delays or the potential indirect emissions from biofuels. This raises concerns about the overall effectiveness of the NZF in addressing the climate crisis. Furthermore, the NZF would only apply a price to roughly 10% of shipping's total greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, a fraction of the comprehensive coverage offered by regional pricing schemes such as the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS). The limited scope of the NZF underscores the necessity for supplementary regulations to meet climate goals and adequately fund the transition to cleaner shipping practices.

## Regional Frameworks Offer a Path Forward

The article highlights the importance of regional frameworks as potential catalysts for maritime decarbonization. The EU ETS, for example, covers 100% of emissions on applicable voyages, offering a much broader scope than the proposed NZF. By implementing robust, integrated regional policies and actively enforcing them, governments can circumvent the stalled progress at the IMO and accelerate the transition to cleaner shipping. These regional frameworks can serve as models for global action and provide incentives for industry players to adopt sustainable practices. For example, companies whose ships regularly call at EU ports will already be impacted by EU ETS carbon costs. This may drive investment in more efficient ships and alternative fuels. ## Industry Initiatives Require Clear Business Cases

While acknowledging the potential role of industry initiatives from ports, fuel producers, and shipping companies, T&E emphasizes the need for clear business cases rather than regulatory uncertainty. Industry stakeholders require predictable and stable regulatory environments to justify investments in sustainable technologies and practices. Without clear business cases, industry initiatives may lack the necessary momentum to drive widespread decarbonization. The lack of international consensus on regulations creates significant uncertainty, making it challenging for businesses to commit to long-term sustainability strategies. This also feeds into [CII ratings](/cii.html) as companies will be unsure how to invest to improve their ship's ratings.

## IMO Delays Jeopardize Transition

The repeated delays at the IMO threaten to derail the transition to a more sustainable shipping industry. As highlighted by T&E, persistent stalling jeopardizes the transition, emphasizing the urgency of action. If MEPC 85 fails to overcome opposition and pass an ambitious measure, it will become increasingly difficult to achieve meaningful decarbonization within the sector. In the absence of a global framework, maritime decarbonization will struggle to gain substantial momentum without additional incentives and policies. The article from safety4sea.com underscores the critical need for governments to remain committed to an effective global measure while actively pursuing alternative policies and collaborating with solution-oriented nations.

## What This Means for Shipping Emissions

The deadlock at the IMO underscores the need for a multi-pronged approach to tackling shipping emissions. While a global agreement remains the ideal outcome, regional frameworks like the EU ETS and proactive industry initiatives are essential for driving progress in the short term. The success of these efforts hinges on clear regulatory signals, robust enforcement, and a willingness among governments and industry stakeholders to prioritize sustainability over short-term economic gains. Without decisive action, the shipping industry risks falling short of its decarbonization goals and contributing to further environmental damage.
