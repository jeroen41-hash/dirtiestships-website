The European Sea Ports Organisation (ESPO) has issued a critical assessment of the proposed adjustments to the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS) for shipping, highlighting persistent concerns over "carbon leakage" and potential unintended consequences for the sector's decarbonisation. This analysis underscores the complex challenge of implementing robust climate policies without inadvertently creating economic disadvantages or simply displacing emissions, a dynamic closely tracked by DirtiestShips.com through [emissions data](/charts.html) and [company rankings](/companies.html).

### Carbon Leakage and Evasive Port Calls: A Persistent Concern

ESPO's primary welcome of the European Commission's efforts centers on the recognition of carbon and business leakage, a phenomenon where ships might alter routes or port calls to avoid EU ETS costs. This issue has been a consistent point of contention, threatening the level playing field for EU ports. The Commission's proposed adjustments to the list of "neighbouring ports" are seen as a step towards mitigating such "evasive port calls."

However, ESPO's cautious optimism points to the inherent difficulty. While changes may reduce certain forms of evasion, "EU ports still suffer a disadvantage towards the surrounding non-EU ports," notes Isabelle Ryckbost, Secretary General of ESPO. For DirtiestShips.com, this raises critical questions about the true impact on global CO2 emissions. If vessels simply offload cargo at non-EU ports and transport it overland, the net reduction in maritime emissions might be negligible, or even lead to an increase due to less efficient land-based transport. Our [CII ratings](/cii.html) tool assesses the operational efficiency of individual ships, but the broader picture of supply chain emissions, distorted by regulatory arbitrage, remains a challenge to fully capture.

### The Debate Over Smaller Ships and Modal Backshift

A significant point of contention for ESPO is the proposal to extend the scope of EU ETS Maritime to smaller ships, lowering the current threshold from 5,000 GT (Gross Tonnage) to 400 GT. ESPO firmly opposes this expansion, fearing a detrimental "modal backshift to road transport."

This concern is rooted in the competitive dynamics of freight. Short-sea shipping, often touted as a more environmentally friendly alternative to road transport for intra-European freight, could become less competitive if smaller vessels are burdened with additional carbon costs. The consequence? Goods might increasingly be moved by trucks, which, per tonne-kilometre, often have higher CO2 emissions than maritime transport, especially for longer distances. Such a shift would directly contradict Europe's policy goals to enhance short-sea shipping and decarbonise the entire transport sector. Analyzing the overall emissions impact requires a holistic view, ensuring that policies designed to clean up one sector do not inadvertently increase emissions in another.

### IMO Alignment: The Global Dimension of Decarbonization

ESPO also stresses the importance of reviewing the EU ETS once an agreement is reached at the International Maritime Organisation (IMO) on its Net-Zero Framework. Ryckbost highlights that "the global context and global solution must be taken into account."

This plea underscores a fundamental challenge in maritime decarbonisation: shipping is inherently global. Regional regulations like the EU ETS, while ambitious, risk creating market distortions if not harmonised with international efforts. The IMO's upcoming framework could introduce global carbon pricing mechanisms or operational efficiency standards that interact with, or potentially supersede, regional measures. ### What this means for shipping emissions

ESPO's assessment reveals the delicate balance policymakers must strike between environmental ambition and economic competitiveness. While the EU ETS aims to put a price on carbon and incentivize cleaner shipping, the risk of carbon leakage and modal backshift could undermine its effectiveness in genuinely reducing overall emissions. For DirtiestShips.com, this highlights the critical need for continuous monitoring of real-world emissions data and a flexible regulatory framework that can adapt to global developments. True decarbonization requires policies that prevent evasion, encourage cleaner modes, and ideally, are harmonized internationally to achieve a level playing field and maximum environmental impact. The ongoing debate emphasizes that the path to net-zero is not just about setting targets, but meticulously designing mechanisms that drive verifiable, systemic change.
