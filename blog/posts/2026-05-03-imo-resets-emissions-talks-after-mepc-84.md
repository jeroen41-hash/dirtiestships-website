The International Maritime Organization said its Marine Environment Protection Committee is “back on track” towards a global shipping emissions deal after closing its 84th session in London, according to IMO.

Nearly 100 delegations faced divisions over mid-term measures under the IMO Net-Zero Framework, with competing proposals on cutting greenhouse gas emissions from ships pushed into a new negotiating phase.

IMO Secretary-General Arsenio Dominguez said: “We are back on track, but we have to rebuild trust. I encourage you to maintain this momentum through your intersessional work and to prepare submissions that can bring the membership together.”

Member states agreed to move negotiations into an intersessional working group to narrow differences ahead of MEPC 85 in six months.

Two rounds are scheduled for September and November, alongside a technical workshop on fuel “chain of custody” systems aimed at tightening emissions tracking across supply chains.

A resumed extraordinary session is set for 4 December, subject to confirmation.

The committee also adopted a resolution condemning attacks on commercial shipping in the Strait of Hormuz, warning that further incidents could trigger large-scale marine pollution involving oil, hazardous substances and debris from fires and explosions.

A new Emission Control Area in the North-East Atlantic was approved, introducing stricter limits on nitrogen oxides, sulphur oxides and particulate matter.

The regime will enter into force on 1 September 2027, with full implementation in 2028, covering waters up to 200 nautical miles from Greenland, Iceland, the Faroe Islands, Ireland and the Atlantic coasts of the United Kingdom, France, Spain and Portugal.

Ships operating in the area will be required to use fuel with sulphur content of no more than 0.10%.

The committee endorsed a 2026 strategy and action plan targeting zero plastic discharges from ships by 2030, alongside work on a mandatory code for transporting plastic pellets.

It also advanced amendments to the Ballast Water Management Convention and agreed to extend work on underwater radiated noise through 2028, including a new IMO study to support future regulation.
