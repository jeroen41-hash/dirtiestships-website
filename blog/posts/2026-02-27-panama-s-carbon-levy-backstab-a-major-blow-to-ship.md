The already fragile global consensus on shipping decarbonization has suffered a potentially fatal blow. A leaked document reveals that Panama, a maritime powerhouse, is actively working to **cancel the proposed carbon levy on shipping** at the International Maritime Organization (IMO). This move, detailed in a paper co-sponsored by Liberia and Argentina and obtained by the Guardian, directly undermines years of painstaking negotiations and endangers the ambitious targets set forth in the IMO's Net-Zero Framework (NZF).

As a reminder, the UN approved the *initial draft* of the IMO's **Net-Zero Framework (NZF) in April 2025**. This framework, years in the making, proposes a **global shipping emissions tax starting in 2028**. The revenue generated would fund cleaner maritime technologies and support developing nations in transitioning to low-carbon shipping. The NZF also outlines crucial emission reduction targets: at least **20% by 2030, 70% by 2040, and net-zero emissions by 2050.** These targets now hang precariously in the balance.

Why is Panama's opposition so damaging? It's not just another nation chiming in. Panama is a *massive* player in the maritime industry. Home to the Panama Canal, which handles approximately **5% of global shipping every year**, the country wields significant influence within the IMO. More importantly, Panama operates the largest flag registry in the world. Ships flying the Panamanian flag are subject to its national regulations, and Panamanian support for a carbon levy would have created a **significant incentive for adoption** among many shipowners and nations.

The motivation behind Panama's stance is murky, but the article suggests a fear of geopolitical vulnerability. The Guardian notes that Panama's leaders are particularly concerned, referencing Donald Trump's previous mentions of annexing the country, along with Canada and Greenland. Apparently unnerved by the US's actions in Venezuela, Panama is potentially acting out of fear and seeking to avoid any policies that might attract unwanted attention.

Whatever the reasoning, this action puts Panama squarely in opposition to countries like the UK, which "continue to back the carbon levy." Proponents of the levy see it as a crucial step, arguing that even a "small tax" could generate "billions of dollars a year for climate action." This revenue is essential for funding the development and deployment of the very technologies needed to meet the IMO's ambitious decarbonization goals.

## What this means

Panama's move is a **severe setback** for global efforts to decarbonize shipping. As a major flag state and key player in global trade, its opposition to the carbon levy significantly weakens the prospects of achieving the IMO's Net-Zero Framework targets. This action raises serious questions about the **future of international cooperation** on climate action within the maritime industry.
